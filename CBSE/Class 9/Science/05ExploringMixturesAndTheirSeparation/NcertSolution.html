NCERT Solutions: Chapter 5

1. Which of the following mixtures are correctly classified as homogeneous (Hm) and heterogeneous (Ht)?
Solution:

Correct Option: (iv) Muddy water — Ht, Milk — Ht, Blood — Ht, Brass — Hm

Reasoning:
- Muddy water is a suspension (Heterogeneous).
- Milk is an emulsion/colloid (Heterogeneous).
- Blood is a colloid (Heterogeneous).
- Brass is an alloy, which is a solid solution of metals (Homogeneous).
Options (i) and (iii) misclassify milk as Hm and smoke as Hm. Option (ii) misclassifies Brass as Ht.

2. Which among the following mixtures show the Tyndall Effect? (a) air and dust particles, (b) copper sulfate and water, (c) starch and water, (d) acetone and water.
Solution:

Correct Option: (iii) a and c

Reasoning: The Tyndall effect is shown by colloids and fine suspensions, as their particles are large enough to scatter a beam of light. Air with dust (suspension/aerosol) and starch mixed with water (colloid) will scatter light. Copper sulfate in water and acetone in water form true solutions (particles < 1 nm), which do not scatter light.

4. Solve the following problems:
(i) A cake recipe uses 75 g sugar, 420 g flour, and 5 g sodium hydrogencarbonate. Express concentration of each.
(ii) A brass alloy contains 70% copper by mass. Calculate quantities of copper and zinc in 120 g of brass.
Solution:

(i) Cake Recipe Concentrations:

Total Mass of mixture = $75\text{ g} + 420\text{ g} + 5\text{ g} = 500\text{ g}$.

Using the mass percentage formula: $$ \% (m/m) = \frac{\text{Mass of component}}{\text{Total mass of mixture}} \times 100 $$

  • Sugar: $$ \frac{75}{500} \times 100 = 15\% $$
  • Flour: $$ \frac{420}{500} \times 100 = 84\% $$
  • Sodium Hydrogencarbonate: $$ \frac{5}{500} \times 100 = 1\% $$

(ii) Brass Alloy Quantities:

Total mass of alloy = 120 g. Copper is 70% by mass. Zinc makes up the remaining 30%.

  • Mass of Copper: $$ \frac{70}{100} \times 120\text{ g} = 84\text{ g} $$
  • Mass of Zinc: $$ \frac{30}{100} \times 120\text{ g} = 36\text{ g} $$
5. The label on a cooking oil pack says one litre (910 g). If mixed with water, will it form a separate layer? Which substance on top? How to separate?
Solution:

Yes, oil and water are immiscible liquids and will form separate layers.

Which substance will be on top?
Density of the oil = $\frac{\text{Mass}}{\text{Volume}} = \frac{910\text{ g}}{1000\text{ mL}} = 0.91\text{ g/mL}$.
Since the density of water is $1.0\text{ g/mL}$, the cooking oil is lighter (less dense) than water. Therefore, the oil will float on top.

How to separate?
We use a Separating Funnel. The heavier liquid (water) forms the bottom layer and can be carefully drained out by opening the stopcock, leaving the oil behind.

6. Assertion (A): Solutions do not exhibit the Tyndall effect. Reason (R): The particles in solutions are larger than 100 nm, so they cannot scatter light. Choose the correct option.
Solution:

Correct Option: (iii) A is true, but R is false.

Explanation: Solutions indeed do not exhibit the Tyndall effect because their particles are extremely small. However, the Reason statement claims the particles are larger than 100 nm, which is completely false (solution particles are typically less than 1 nm in diameter).

8. Two miscible liquids, A (BP 60°C) and B (BP 90°C). Suggest a method to separate them.
Solution:

Method: Simple Distillation

Because A and B are miscible liquids and the difference in their boiling points ($90^\circ\text{C} - 60^\circ\text{C} = 30^\circ\text{C}$) is greater than 25°C, they can be successfully separated by simple distillation. Liquid A (with the lower boiling point) will vaporise first, travel through the condenser, and be collected as distillate.

9. Compare evaporation, crystallization and distillation. In which situation, would you prefer each?
Solution:
  • Evaporation: Used to separate a dissolved solid from a liquid solvent when we only care about recovering the solid and don't need the solvent (which escapes as vapour).
  • Crystallization: A purification method preferred over evaporation for obtaining highly pure solid crystals. Evaporation might cause some solids to decompose or char, whereas crystallization yields pure, geometric solid crystals.
  • Distillation: Used when we want to recover both the solute and the solvent (by condensing the vapour), or to separate two miscible liquids with sufficiently different boiling points.
10. Blood is a colloidal mixture. (i) What would happen if blood behaved like a true suspension? (ii) Identify dispersed phase and dispersion medium.
Solution:

(i) If blood were a true suspension, the heavy blood cells would settle down at the bottom of our blood vessels under gravity. This would block circulation, stop oxygen transport, and be fatal.

(ii) Dispersed Phase: Blood cells (Red blood cells, white blood cells, platelets).
Dispersion Medium: Blood Plasma (the liquid portion).

12. Why is distillation an effective method for separating a mixture of water and acetone?
Solution:

Distillation is highly effective because water and acetone are miscible liquids that have a large difference in their boiling points. Acetone boils at approximately 56°C, while water boils at 100°C. The difference is 44°C (which is greater than the required 25°C threshold). When heated, acetone vaporises completely well before water reaches its boiling point, allowing pure acetone to be condensed and collected.

13. Answer questions based on the solubility Table 5.4.
Solution:

(i) Mass of KNO3 for 50g water at 40°C:
At 40°C, solubility of Potassium Nitrate is 62 g per 100 g of water. Therefore, for exactly half the water (50 g), we need half the mass of the salt: $$ \frac{62}{2} = 31\text{ g} $$

(ii) Cooling KCl saturated solution from 80°C to 25°C:
At 80°C, 54 g of KCl is dissolved per 100 g of water. As it cools to room temperature (25°C), the solubility drops (to around 36 g). The excess dissolved solute (approximately $54\text{ g} - 36\text{ g} = 18\text{ g}$) will separate out, and the student will observe solid crystals of potassium chloride forming at the bottom of the container.

(iii) Effect of temperature on solubility:
The solubility of all the given solid salts increases with an increase in temperature. Comparing them, Potassium Nitrate shows the most drastic increase (from 21g to 167g), while Sodium Chloride shows very little change (from 36g to 37g).

14. Students A, B, and C prepare sugar solutions. (i) Calculate % m/m concentration for each. (ii) Whose solution is most concentrated?
Solution:

(i) Concentration Calculations:

  • Student A: 20 g sugar in 80 g water. Total mass = 100 g.
    $$ \% (m/m) = \frac{20}{100} \times 100 = 20\% $$
  • Student B: 20 g sugar in 100 g water. Total mass = 120 g.
    $$ \% (m/m) = \frac{20}{120} \times 100 \approx 16.67\% $$
  • Student C: 30 g sugar in 80 g water. Total mass = 110 g.
    $$ \% (m/m) = \frac{30}{110} \times 100 \approx 27.27\% $$

(ii) Most Concentrated:
Student C has the most concentrated solution ($27.27\%$) because they dissolved the highest mass of solute in the lowest mass of solvent.
