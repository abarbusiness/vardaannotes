TISSUES IN ACTION

1. Introduction to Tissues

In multicellular organisms, cells do not function alone. There is a hierarchy of organisation: Cells → Tissues → Organs → Organ Systems → Organism.

Concept Tissue: A tissue is a group of similar cells that work together to perform a specific function. The formation of tissues leads to the division of labour in multicellular organisms, significantly increasing the body's efficiency.

2. Plant vs. Animal Tissues

Plants and animals have distinct body designs and lifestyles, leading to differences in their tissue structures:

  • Mobility: Plants are stationary and require supporting tissues to stay firm and upright (which usually have rigid cell walls and are often dead). Animals are mobile and require tissues that provide flexibility and enable locomotion.
  • Nutrition: Plants possess tissues to synthesise their own food via solar energy (photosynthesis). Animals possess tissues adapted to digest and absorb food obtained from their surroundings.
  • Growth Pattern: Plant growth is limited to specific regions containing actively dividing cells. Animal growth is more uniform across the body.

3. Plant Tissues

Plant tissues are primarily classified based on their ability to divide into Meristematic Tissues and Permanent Tissues.

3.1 Meristematic Tissue (Tissues for Growth)

These tissues consist of actively dividing cells responsible for the growth of the plant. Based on their location, they are of three types:

  • Apical Meristem: Located at the tips of roots and shoots. It is responsible for the increase in length (primary growth).
  • Lateral Meristem: Located along the circumference of stems and roots (e.g., cambium). It is responsible for the increase in girth or diameter (secondary growth). Its activity creates the annual growth rings visible in tree trunks.
  • Intercalary Meristem: Located at the nodes or the base of internodes (e.g., in grasses). It helps in the rapid regrowth and branching of plants after they are cut or grazed.
Important Characteristics of Meristematic Cells: They are small, have thin cell walls, a large prominent nucleus, dense cytoplasm, and are tightly packed with no intercellular spaces. They typically lack vacuoles because they need to divide rapidly rather than store food or maintain rigidity.

3.2 Permanent Tissues

Meristematic cells eventually lose their ability to divide and take on specific roles through a process called differentiation, forming permanent tissues. They are divided into Simple and Complex permanent tissues.

A. Protective Tissues:

  • Epidermis: The outermost single layer of cells. It secretes a waxy, water-resistant layer called the cuticle (cutin) to prevent water loss and protect against mechanical injury.
  • In roots, epidermal cells bear root hairs to increase water absorption area.
  • In leaves, the epidermis contains small pores called stomata, which facilitate gas exchange and transpiration (water loss).

B. Simple Permanent Tissues (Supporting Tissues):

  • Parenchyma: Living cells with thin walls, loosely packed with intercellular spaces. Mainly stores food and performs photosynthesis (if it contains chlorophyll).
  • Collenchyma: Living cells with unevenly thickened corners (due to pectin). Provides support and flexibility to parts like stems and tendrils, allowing them to bend without breaking.
  • Sclerenchyma: Dead cells with very thick walls due to lignin deposition. Provides hard, rigid strength (e.g., coconut husk, walnut shells, wood).

C. Complex Permanent Tissues (Conducting/Vascular Tissues):

  • Xylem: Transports water and minerals upwards from roots. Consists mostly of dead cells: tracheids, vessels, and xylem fibres. The only living component is xylem parenchyma.
  • Phloem: Transports synthesized food from leaves to all parts of the plant. Consists mostly of living cells: sieve tubes, companion cells (which regulate sieve tubes), and phloem parenchyma. The only dead component is phloem fibres.
Fact Crown Gall Disease: Caused by the bacterium Agrobacterium tumefaciens, this disease creates tumour-like swellings in plants due to uncontrolled cell division. Scientists now use this bacterium as a tool in genetic engineering to introduce useful genes into plants!

4. Animal Tissues

Animal tissues are highly specialised and fall into four major categories based on their function.

4.1 Epithelial Tissue

Forms the outer covering of the body (skin) and lines internal organs/cavities. The cells are tightly packed to prevent germ entry and regulate the exchange of substances.

Function Structure Type Location Example
Exchange/Rapid Diffusion Single layer of thin, flat cells (Squamous) Lungs, lining of blood vessels
Protection Many layers of cells (Stratified Squamous) Skin, mouth, oesophagus
Secretion Cuboidal or Columnar cells (Glandular) Salivary glands, sweat glands, stomach
Sensory Receptor cells with hair-like cilia (Ciliated) Nostrils, taste buds, inner ear
Absorption Tall, pillar-like cells (Columnar) Lining of the small intestine

4.2 Connective Tissue

Connects, supports, and binds other tissues. They feature cells embedded in a non-living matrix.

  • Blood: Has a fluid matrix called plasma. Contains Red Blood Cells (RBCs - transport oxygen via haemoglobin), White Blood Cells (WBCs - fight infection), and Platelets (blood clotting).
  • Bone: Has a hard, rigid matrix composed of calcium and phosphorus compounds. Provides framework and protection.
  • Cartilage: Has a soft, flexible, jelly-like matrix. Provides cushioning and shock absorption at joints, nose, and ears.
  • Ligaments: Connects bone to bone. Stabilises joints and prevents dislocation.
  • Tendons: Connects muscle to bone. Transmits force to allow movement.

4.3 Muscular Tissue

Responsible for movement. Contains specialised contractile proteins.

  • Skeletal Muscles: Voluntary control (you control them). Attached to bones. Cells are long, cylindrical, unbranched, multinucleate, and striated (have light and dark bands).
  • Smooth Muscles: Involuntary control. Found in the stomach and intestines (e.g., digestion). Cells are spindle-shaped, single nucleus, and non-striated.
  • Cardiac Muscles: Involuntary control. Found only in the heart. Cells are cylindrical, branched, have a single nucleus, faint striations, and beat tirelessly throughout life.

4.4 Nervous Tissue

Forms the control and coordination network (brain, spinal cord, nerves).

  • The functional unit is the Neuron (nerve cell).
  • Parts of a Neuron:
    • Cell Body: Contains the nucleus and controls the cell.
    • Dendrites: Short, branched structures that receive signals from other neurons.
    • Axon: A long fibre that transmits messages away from the cell body to the axon terminals.

5. The Musculoskeletal System & Joints

The combination of bones, muscles, joints, cartilage, tendons, and ligaments forms the musculoskeletal system, functioning under nervous system control.

  • Joint: A junction between two or more bones.
  • Ball and Socket Joint: Allows free movement in all directions (e.g., Shoulder joint, hip joint).
  • Hinge Joint: Allows movement in only one direction, like a door hinge (e.g., Elbow, knee).
  • Pivot Joint: Allows rotational movement from side to side (e.g., connection between skull and spine).
  • Fixed Joint: Does not allow any movement, providing rigid protection (e.g., bones of the skull protecting the brain).
