In the previous chapter, we learned about the heating effects of electric current. We know that an electric current-carrying wire also behaves like a magnet. This chapter will explore magnetic fields and electromagnetic effects, as well as electromagnets.

Hans Christian Oersted's Discovery

  • Hans Christian Oersted was a 19th-century scientist.
  • In 1820, he accidentally discovered that a compass needle deflected (moved) when an electric current was passed through a metal wire placed nearby.
  • This observation proved that electricity and magnetism are related phenomena.
  • His research later led to technologies like the radio, television, and fiber optics.
  • The unit of magnetic field strength, the oersted, is named in his honor.
Activity 12.1: Demonstrating the Magnetic Effect
This activity confirms Oersted's finding.
Setup: A straight, thick copper wire (XY) is placed in an electric circuit. A small compass is placed near the wire.
Action: Current is passed through the circuit by inserting the key.
Observation: The compass needle deflects.
Conclusion: This deflection means that the electric current passing through the copper wire has produced a magnetic effect. This shows electricity and magnetism are linked.

12.1 Magnetic Field and Field Lines

We are familiar with the fact that a compass needle deflects when brought near a bar magnet.

  • A compass needle is actually a small bar magnet itself.
  • The ends of the compass needle point approximately towards the North and South directions.
  • The end pointing to the north is called the north seeking or north pole.
  • The other end pointing to the south is called the south seeking or south pole.
  • A fundamental rule of magnets is: like poles repel, while unlike poles attract each other.

Visualizing the Magnetic Field

Activity 12.2: Using Iron Filings
Setup: A bar magnet is placed on a sheet of white paper. Iron filings are sprinkled uniformly around the magnet.
Action: The board is tapped gently.
Observation: The iron filings arrange themselves in a specific pattern.
Explanation: The magnet exerts its influence in the region surrounding it. This region is said to have a magnetic field. The magnetic field is the area around a magnet where its force can be detected.
Activity 12.3: Using a Compass Needle
Result: Join the points marked on the paper with a smooth curve. This curve represents a magnetic field line.
Observation: The deflection of the compass needle increases as it is moved closer to the poles of the magnet.

Properties of Magnetic Field Lines

A magnetic field is a quantity that has both direction and magnitude.

  • Property 1: Convention: By convention, magnetic field lines emerge (come out) from the north pole and merge (go in) at the south pole. (This is outside the magnet).
  • Property 2: Inside the Magnet: Inside the magnet, the direction of the field lines is from its south pole to its north pole.
  • Property 3: Closed Curves: Because of the first two properties, magnetic field lines are closed curves.
  • Property 4: Strength: The strength of the magnetic field is shown by the degree of closeness of the field lines. The field is stronger where the field lines are crowded (closer together), such as near the poles.
  • Property 5: No Intersection: No two field-lines are ever found to cross each other.
