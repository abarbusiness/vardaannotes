CELL: THE BUILDING BLOCK OF LIFE

1. Introduction to Cells

The cell is the fundamental, structural, and functional unit of all living organisms. Every living being, from a tiny bacterium to a giant tree, is made up of cells.

  • Origin of Life: Scientists believe life originated in water, particularly in environments like hot springs (e.g., Puga Valley in Ladakh) which housed heat-loving bacteria called thermophiles.
  • Unicellular Organisms: Organisms made of only a single cell (e.g., bacteria, yeast, amoeba).
  • Multicellular Organisms: Organisms composed of millions of cells working together (e.g., plants, animals, humans).
  • Levels of Organization: Cells → Tissues → Organs → Organ Systems → Organism.
Fact The Limits of Human Vision: The limit of resolution of the human eye is 0.1 mm. Anything smaller than this requires a microscope to be seen distinctly.

2. Discovery of the Cell and Microscopy

  • Robert Hooke (1665): He was the first person to observe a cell. Using a self-designed microscope, he examined a thin slice of cork and noticed box-like compartments, which he named "cells".
  • Light Microscope: Uses visible light and glass lenses (objective and eyepiece) to magnify objects. Common in school laboratories.
  • Electron Microscope: Uses a beam of electrons instead of light to produce highly magnified images. It allows scientists to observe fine cellular structures at the nanometre scale (1 nm = one-billionth of a metre).

3. Structural Organization of a Cell

Almost all cells consist of three primary components:

  1. Plasma Membrane (Cell Membrane)
  2. Cytoplasm
  3. Nucleus

3.1 The Cell Membrane (Plasma Membrane)

The cell membrane is a thin, flexible boundary that protects the cell's contents and separates it from the external environment. It is described by the Fluid-Mosaic Model:

  • Made up of a lipid bilayer (fats) and embedded proteins.
  • The lipids allow flexibility ("fluid"), while the embedded proteins act like gatekeepers, arranged like tiles ("mosaic").
  • It is selectively permeable, allowing some substances to pass while blocking others.
Concept Osmosis vs. Diffusion
Diffusion: The net movement of particles from an area of higher concentration to lower concentration.
Osmosis: The specific diffusion of water across a selectively permeable membrane from a dilute solution (more water) to a concentrated solution (less water).

Behavior of Cells in Different Solutions:

  • Hypotonic Solution: The outside solution has more water than the cell. Water enters the cell, causing it to swell.
  • Isotonic Solution: The concentration is equal inside and outside. No net movement; cell size remains the same.
  • Hypertonic Solution: The outside solution has less water (more solute) than the cell. Water leaves the cell, causing it to shrink.

3.2 The Cell Wall (Plants, Fungi, Bacteria)

Unlike animal cells, plant cells possess an additional rigid outer layer called the cell wall, primarily made of cellulose.

  • Provides structural support, allowing plants to stand upright and withstand wind/rain.
  • Prevents plant cells from bursting when placed in hypotonic (dilute) solutions.
  • When a plant cell loses water through osmosis in a hypertonic solution, the inner contents shrink away from the rigid cell wall.

4. Prokaryotic vs. Eukaryotic Cells

Cells are broadly classified into two categories based on their nuclear structure:

Characteristics Prokaryotic Cell (e.g., Bacteria) Eukaryotic Cell (e.g., Plants, Animals)
Nucleus Primitive; lacks a nuclear membrane. Genetic region is called a nucleoid. True, well-defined nucleus enclosed by a nuclear membrane.
Size Small (1 to 10 µm) Larger (10 to 100 µm)
Organelles Membrane-bound organelles are absent. Membrane-bound organelles are present (e.g., mitochondria, ER).
Organism Type Usually unicellular Can be unicellular or multicellular
Important Acellular Organisms: Viruses, viroids, and prions are considered acellular (lacking a cell structure) infectious agents. Viruses consist only of genetic material and a protein coat.

5. Cell Organelles

The cytoplasm contains specialized, membrane-bound structures called organelles, each functioning like a specific department in a factory.

5.1 Nucleus (The Control Centre)

  • Covered by a double-layered nuclear membrane with pores for material transfer.
  • Contains a dense body called the nucleolus (where ribosome subunits are synthesized).
  • Contains Chromatin material (entangled mass of DNA) which condenses into rod-shaped Chromosomes during cell division.
  • Chromosomes contain genetic information via DNA. Functional segments of DNA are called genes.

5.2 Ribosomes (Protein Factories)

Tiny structures found floating freely in the cytoplasm or attached to the ER. They are the primary sites for protein synthesis.

5.3 Endoplasmic Reticulum (The Manufacturing Factory)

A network of membranes involved in synthesis and transport:

  • Rough ER (RER): Has ribosomes attached to its surface (making it look rough). Responsible for manufacturing and transporting proteins.
  • Smooth ER (SER): Lacks ribosomes. Responsible for synthesizing lipids (fats) and some hormones.

5.4 Golgi Apparatus (Packaging & Shipping)

  • Consists of stacks of flattened, sac-like structures.
  • Discovered by Camillo Golgi.
  • Modifies, sorts, and packages materials (proteins/lipids) synthesized by the ER into vesicles. Also involved in lysosome formation.

5.5 Lysosomes (The Clean-up System)

  • Single membrane-bound sacs filled with powerful digestive enzymes.
  • Break down waste materials, foreign agents, and damaged cell organelles, keeping the cell clean.

5.6 Mitochondria (The Powerhouse of the Cell)

  • Produces energy required for cellular activities in the form of ATP (Adenosine Triphosphate) through cellular respiration.
  • Possesses a double membrane: Outer is smooth, inner is deeply folded into finger-like projections called cristae (increases surface area for chemical reactions).
  • Unique trait: They have their own DNA and ribosomes, allowing them to synthesize some of their own proteins.

5.7 Plastids (Found Only in Plant Cells)

Double-membrane organelles involved in food synthesis and storage. Like mitochondria, they have their own DNA and ribosomes.

  • Chloroplasts: Contain the green pigment chlorophyll. Sites of photosynthesis. Contain a semi-fluid matrix called stroma.
  • Chromoplasts: Contain non-green pigments (yellow, orange, red). Provide bright colors to flowers and fruits to attract pollinators.
  • Leucoplasts: Colorless plastids that store food materials like starch, oils, and proteins (e.g., in potatoes).

5.8 Vacuoles (Storage and Support)

  • Plant Cells: Have a single, large central vacuole filled with cell sap. It stores water, minerals, and wastes, and maintains turgor pressure (keeping the cell firm).
  • Animal Cells: Have very small, temporary vacuoles.

6. Cell Division

Cells divide to allow organisms to grow, replace damaged tissue, and reproduce. The two main types are:

  • Mitosis: Used for general growth and repair. One parent cell divides to form two genetically identical daughter cells. The chromosome number remains the same.
  • Meiosis: Used only for sexual reproduction (occurring in testes/ovaries). It is a two-step process where one parent cell forms four daughter cells (gametes). The chromosome number in daughter cells is halved. This ensures genetic diversity.

7. The Cell Theory

The unifying principle of biology was formulated by three prominent scientists:

  • Matthias Schleiden (1838): Stated that all plants are made of cells.
  • Theodor Schwann (1839): Stated that all animals are made of cells.
  • Rudolf Virchow (1855): Expanded the theory, stating that new cells arise only from pre-existing cells.
Concept The Classical Cell Theory:
1. All living organisms are made up of one or more cells.
2. The cell is the basic unit of structure and function in living beings.
3. All cells arise from pre-existing cells.

8. Cell Growth and Death

  • Programmed Cell Death (PCD): A genetically organized process of selective cell destruction necessary for normal development (e.g., eliminating webbed tissue between fingers during embryonic development).
  • Contact Inhibition: Normal animal cells stop dividing when they come into contact with neighboring cells, maintaining growth balance.
  • Cancer/Tumors: Errors in mitosis cause cells to lose contact inhibition, leading to uncontrolled, abnormal cell division known as cancer.
