1. What is a Chemical Reaction?

A chemical reaction is a process where substances undergo a chemical change to form new substances with different properties.

You can tell a chemical reaction has occurred by observing one or more of the following:

  • Change in Color
  • Change in State
  • Evolution of a Gas
  • Change in Temperature

2. Chemical Equations

A chemical equation is the symbolic representation of a chemical reaction.

  • Reactants: Substances that take part in the reaction (written on the left).
  • Products: New substances formed (written on the right).

3. Types of Chemical Reactions

3.1 Combination Reaction (Synthesis)

A reaction in which two or more reactants combine to form a single product. It's like building something bigger from smaller pieces.

General Form: A + B → AB

Exothermic Reactions: Reactions that release heat energy, causing the temperature of the surroundings to rise. Most combination reactions are exothermic.

    2Mg(s) + O‚ÇÇ(g) → 2MgO(s)
    Observation: Burns with a dazzling white flame.
  • Respiration (An Exothermic Process): Glucose from our food combines with oxygen to provide energy.

3.2 Decomposition Reaction

A reaction in which a single compound breaks down into two or more simpler substances. It's the opposite of a combination reaction and requires energy.

General Form: AB → A + B

Endothermic Reactions: Reactions that absorb heat energy, causing the temperature of the surroundings to fall. All decomposition reactions are endothermic.

Types of Decomposition Reactions:

    2H‚ÇÇO(l) → 2H‚ÇÇ(g) + O‚ÇÇ(g)
    Observation: The volume of hydrogen gas collected at the cathode is double the volume of oxygen gas collected at the anode.

3.3 Displacement Reaction

A reaction in which a more reactive element displaces (or removes) a less reactive element from its salt solution.

General Form: A + BC → AC + B

A reaction in which there is an exchange of ions between two reactant compounds to form two new compounds.

General Form: AB + CD → AD + CB

4. Oxidation and Reduction (Redox Reactions)

  • Oxidation is defined as the gain of oxygen or the loss of hydrogen.
  • Reduction is defined as the loss of oxygen or the gain of hydrogen.

These two processes always occur simultaneously in a reaction, which is called a Redox Reaction.

CuO + H‚ÇÇ → Cu + H‚ÇÇO
  • Copper oxide (CuO) loses oxygen to become Copper (Cu). Therefore, CuO is reduced.
  • Hydrogen (H‚ÇÇ) gains oxygen to become water (H‚ÇÇO). Therefore, H‚ÇÇ is oxidized.

5. Effects of Oxidation in Daily Life

5.1 Corrosion

This is the process where metals are damaged or "eaten away" by reacting with substances in their surroundings, such as moisture, air, and acids.

  • Rusting of iron (forms a reddish-brown flaky substance).
  • Formation of a black coating on silver.
  • Formation of a green coating on copper.

5.2 Rancidity

This is the slow oxidation of oils and fats present in food materials, which results in an unpleasant smell and taste.

Prevention: To prevent food from becoming rancid, manufacturers often flush bags of chips and other snacks with an unreactive gas like nitrogen, which prevents oxygen from reacting with the food. Storing food in airtight containers also helps.
