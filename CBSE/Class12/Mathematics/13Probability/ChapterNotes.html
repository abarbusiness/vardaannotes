Chapter 13: Probability

Dear Class 12 Student! Probability in Class 12 takes a massive leap from the simple coin tosses of Class 11. We are no longer just finding the chance of an event; we are dealing with events that happen sequentially, dependently, and conditionally. Bayes' Theorem is one of the most famous and highly weighted 5/6-mark questions in Board exams. Furthermore, the Binomial Distribution forms the cornerstone of statistics in JEE. Master the logic, and the math will follow naturally!

1. Review of Class 11 Basics

Before diving into advanced probability, let's brush up on the foundational axioms.

  • Sample Space ($S$): The set of all possible outcomes of a random experiment.
  • Mutually Exclusive Events: Events that cannot occur simultaneously. Their intersection is empty. $\mathbf{P(A \cap B) = 0}$.
  • Exhaustive Events: A set of events whose union perfectly forms the entire sample space. $\mathbf{A \cup B \cup C = S}$.
  • Axiomatic Approach: The probability of an event $E$ is given by $P(E) = \frac{n(E)}{n(S)}$.
Crucial Formulas 1. Addition Theorem: For any two events $A$ and $B$:
$$P(A \cup B) = P(A) + P(B) - P(A \cap B)$$ (Note: If A and B are mutually exclusive, $P(A \cap B) = 0$, so $P(A \cup B) = P(A) + P(B)$).

2. De Morgan's Laws in Probability:
Probability of "neither A nor B":
$$P(A' \cap B') = P((A \cup B)') = 1 - P(A \cup B)$$ Probability of "not A or not B":
$$P(A' \cup B') = P((A \cap B)') = 1 - P(A \cap B)$$
Practice Problem 1: Basic Formulas Question: Given $P(A) = 0.5$, $P(B) = 0.6$, and $P(A \cup B) = 0.8$. Find $P(A \cap B)$ and $P(\text{neither A nor B})$.
Solution:
1. Find $P(A \cap B)$:
Using the Addition Theorem: $P(A \cup B) = P(A) + P(B) - P(A \cap B)$.
$0.8 = 0.5 + 0.6 - P(A \cap B) \implies 0.8 = 1.1 - P(A \cap B)$.
$P(A \cap B) = 1.1 - 0.8 = \mathbf{0.3}$.
2. Find $P(\text{neither A nor B})$:
This is $P(A' \cap B')$. Using De Morgan's Law:
$P(A' \cap B') = 1 - P(A \cup B)$.
$P(A' \cap B') = 1 - 0.8 = \mathbf{0.2}$.

2. Conditional Probability

Concept: In real life, the probability of an event can change if we possess extra information. The probability of an event $E$ occurring, given that another event $F$ has already occurred (or is known to be true), is called the Conditional Probability of $E$ given $F$.

Conditional Probability Formula $$P(E|F) = \frac{P(E \cap F)}{P(F)} \quad \text{provided } P(F) \neq 0$$ Read as: "Probability of E given F". The sample space restricts from the entire set S down to just the set F.

Properties of Conditional Probability

  • $P(S|F) = P(F|F) = 1$
  • If $A$ and $B$ are any two events and $F$ has already occurred:
    $P((A \cup B)|F) = P(A|F) + P(B|F) - P((A \cap B)|F)$
  • $P(E'|F) = 1 - P(E|F)$
Practice Problem 2: Conditional Probability Question: A family has two children. What is the probability that both the children are boys given that at least one of them is a boy?
Solution:
1. Sample Space for two children: $S = \{BB, BG, GB, GG\}$. So, $n(S) = 4$.
2. Let $E$ be the event "both children are boys".
$E = \{BB\} \implies P(E) = 1/4$.
3. Let $F$ be the event "at least one child is a boy" (This is our 'given' condition).
$F = \{BB, BG, GB\} \implies P(F) = 3/4$.
4. Find the intersection $E \cap F$ (both are boys AND at least one is a boy):
$E \cap F = \{BB\} \implies P(E \cap F) = 1/4$.
5. Apply Conditional Probability Formula:
$P(E|F) = \frac{P(E \cap F)}{P(F)} = \frac{1/4}{3/4} = \mathbf{\frac{1}{3}}$.

3. Multiplication Theorem on Probability

The conditional probability formula can be rearranged to find the probability of the simultaneous occurrence (intersection) of two or more events sequentially.

Multiplication Theorem Formulas From $P(E|F) = \frac{P(E \cap F)}{P(F)}$, we cross-multiply to get:
$$P(E \cap F) = P(F) \cdot P(E|F)$$ Similarly, if E happens first:
$$P(E \cap F) = P(E) \cdot P(F|E)$$
Extension to Three Events:
$$P(A \cap B \cap C) = P(A) \cdot P(B|A) \cdot P(C|(A \cap B))$$

With vs. Without Replacement:
- If an item is drawn from a bag and replaced, the probabilities for the second draw remain unchanged. The events are independent.
- If it is drawn without replacement, the total number of items decreases, altering the sample space for the next draw. The events are dependent, and we MUST use the Multiplication Theorem.

Practice Problem 3: Without Replacement Question: An urn contains 10 black and 5 white balls. Two balls are drawn from the urn one after the other without replacement. What is the probability that both drawn balls are black?
Solution:
1. Total balls initially $= 15$.
2. Let $E$ be the event "first ball is black".
$P(E) = \frac{10}{15} = \frac{2}{3}$.
3. Let $F$ be the event "second ball is black". Since the first black ball was NOT replaced, there are now $9$ black balls left out of $14$ total balls.
The probability of the second ball being black, given the first was black, is $P(F|E) = \frac{9}{14}$.
4. We need the probability of both being black: $P(E \cap F)$.
$P(E \cap F) = P(E) \cdot P(F|E) = \frac{2}{3} \times \frac{9}{14} = \frac{18}{42} = \mathbf{\frac{3}{7}}$.

4. Independent Events

Definition: Two events $E$ and $F$ are said to be independent if the occurrence or non-occurrence of one event has absolutely no effect on the probability of the occurrence of the other event.

Mathematical Condition for Independence (Must-Know) If $E$ and $F$ are independent, then $P(E|F)$ simply equals $P(E)$. Substituting this into the Multiplication Theorem gives the universal test for independence:
$$P(E \cap F) = P(E) \cdot P(F)$$ If this equation holds true, the events are independent. If it does not hold, they are dependent.

Important Deductions from Independence

  • If $A$ and $B$ are independent, then $P(A|B) = P(A)$ and $P(B|A) = P(B)$.
  • If $A$ and $B$ are independent, then the following pairs are ALSO perfectly independent:
    1. $A'$ and $B'$
    2. $A$ and $B'$
    3. $A'$ and $B$
  • Crucial Distinction: Students often confuse "Mutually Exclusive" with "Independent".
    - Mutually Exclusive means they cannot happen together: $P(A \cap B) = \mathbf{0}$.
    - Independent means they don't affect each other: $P(A \cap B) = \mathbf{P(A) \cdot P(B)}$.
    (Note: Two events with non-zero probabilities can NEVER be both mutually exclusive and independent!).
Practice Problem 4: Proving Independence Question: A die is thrown. Let A be the event "the number appearing is a multiple of 3" and B be the event "the number appearing is even". Are the events A and B independent?
Solution:
1. Sample space $S = \{1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6\}$.
2. Event A (multiple of 3): $A = \{3, 6\} \implies P(A) = 2/6 = 1/3$.
3. Event B (even number): $B = \{2, 4, 6\} \implies P(B) = 3/6 = 1/2$.
4. Intersection $A \cap B$ (multiple of 3 AND even): $A \cap B = \{6\} \implies P(A \cap B) = 1/6$.
5. Check the condition for independence: $P(A) \cdot P(B) = (1/3) \times (1/2) = 1/6$.
6. Since $P(A \cap B) = P(A) \cdot P(B) = 1/6$, the events A and B are strictly Independent.

5. Theorem of Total Probability

Before understanding Total Probability, we must understand the concept of a Partition of a Sample Space.

  • A set of events $E_1, E_2, \dots, E_n$ represents a partition of the sample space $S$ if:
    1. They are pairwise mutually exclusive ($E_i \cap E_j = \phi$ for any $i \neq j$).
    2. They are collectively exhaustive ($E_1 \cup E_2 \cup \dots \cup E_n = S$).
    3. They all have non-zero probabilities ($P(E_i) > 0$).

Theorem Statement & Formula Let an event $A$ be associated with a sample space containing a partition $E_1, E_2, \dots, E_n$. Event A can occur simultaneously with any of these partitions. The Total Probability of A is the sum of the probabilities of these mutually exclusive intersections:
$$P(A) = P(E_1)P(A|E_1) + P(E_2)P(A|E_2) + \dots + P(E_n)P(A|E_n)$$ $$P(A) = \sum_{j=1}^{n} P(E_j)P(A|E_j)$$
Practice Problem 5: Total Probability Question: Bag I contains 3 red and 4 black balls. Bag II contains 5 red and 6 black balls. One bag is selected at random and a ball is drawn from it. What is the probability that the drawn ball is red?
Solution:
1. Identify Partitions (The choice of bags):
Let $E_1$ = Event of choosing Bag I, $E_2$ = Event of choosing Bag II.
Since bags are chosen at random, $P(E_1) = 1/2$ and $P(E_2) = 1/2$.
2. Identify Event A: Let $A$ = Event that the drawn ball is Red.
3. Find Conditional Probabilities:
$P(A|E_1)$ = Probability of drawing red from Bag I = $3/7$.
$P(A|E_2)$ = Probability of drawing red from Bag II = $5/11$.
4. Apply Total Probability Theorem:
$P(A) = P(E_1)P(A|E_1) + P(E_2)P(A|E_2)$
$P(A) = \left( \frac{1}{2} \times \frac{3}{7} \right) + \left( \frac{1}{2} \times \frac{5}{11} \right)$
$P(A) = \frac{3}{14} + \frac{5}{22}$
LCM of 14 and 22 is 154.
$P(A) = \frac{33 + 35}{154} = \frac{68}{154} = \mathbf{\frac{34}{77}}$.

6. Bayes' Theorem (5/6-Mark Board Guarantee)

Concept: Bayes' Theorem calculates Reverse or Posterior Probability. In the Total Probability theorem, we calculated the chance of an event A happening. In Bayes' Theorem, event A has already happened, and we are asked to find the probability that it originated from a specific specific "cause" or partition $E_i$.

Bayes' Formula If $E_1, E_2, \dots, E_n$ form a partition of the sample space, and A is an event that has occurred:
$$P(E_i|A) = \frac{P(E_i) \cdot P(A|E_i)}{\sum_{j=1}^{n} P(E_j) \cdot P(A|E_j)}$$ (In plain English: The probability of a specific branch divided by the Total Probability of all branches).
Steps to Solve Bayes' Word Problems 1. Identify the Partitions ($E_1, E_2 \dots$): What are the initial distinct choices/causes? (e.g., Choosing Bag 1 or Bag 2, Machine A, B, or C). State their prior probabilities $P(E_1), P(E_2)$.
2. Identify Event A: What is the common final event that is stated as "is known to have happened" or "is found to be"? (e.g., "A defective item is found").
3. Find Conditionals: Calculate $P(A|E_1), P(A|E_2)$, etc.
4. Apply Formula: Set up Bayes' fraction to find the required reverse probability (e.g., $P(E_1|A)$).
Practice Problem 6: Classic Bayes' Problem Question: A factory has two machines A and B. Machine A produces 60% of the items and Machine B produces 40%. 2% of the items produced by Machine A are defective, and 1% produced by Machine B are defective. If an item is drawn at random and is found to be defective, what is the probability that it was produced by Machine B?
Solution:
1. Partitions: Let $E_1$ = Item produced by A, $E_2$ = Item produced by B.
$P(E_1) = 60/100 = 0.6$, $\quad P(E_2) = 40/100 = 0.4$.
2. Event A: Let $A$ = The item drawn is defective.
3. Conditionals:
$P(A|E_1)$ = Defective given it's from A = $2\% = 0.02$.
$P(A|E_2)$ = Defective given it's from B = $1\% = 0.01$.
4. Apply Bayes' Theorem: We need the probability it came from Machine B given it is defective, i.e., $P(E_2|A)$.
$P(E_2|A) = \frac{P(E_2)P(A|E_2)}{P(E_1)P(A|E_1) + P(E_2)P(A|E_2)}$
$P(E_2|A) = \frac{0.4 \times 0.01}{(0.6 \times 0.02) + (0.4 \times 0.01)}$
$P(E_2|A) = \frac{0.004}{0.012 + 0.004} = \frac{0.004}{0.016} = \frac{4}{16} = \mathbf{\frac{1}{4} = 0.25}$.

7. Random Variables and Probability Distributions

  • Random Variable ($X$): A real-valued function whose domain is the sample space of a random experiment. It assigns a numerical value to each possible outcome (e.g., Let X be the "number of heads" in 3 coin tosses. X can take values 0, 1, 2, or 3).
  • Probability Distribution: A systematic table listing all possible values of the random variable $X$ ($x_1, x_2, \dots, x_n$) alongside their corresponding probabilities ($p_1, p_2, \dots, p_n$).
  • Strict Conditions for a Valid Distribution:
    1. Every probability must be non-negative: $p_i \ge 0$.
    2. The sum of all probabilities MUST equal exactly 1: $\sum_{i=1}^{n} p_i = 1$.

8. Mean and Variance of a Random Variable

Formulas for Mean and Variance Mean / Expected Value ($\mu$ or $E(X)$): The weighted average of all possible values. It tells us what to expect on average over many trials.
$$\mu = E(X) = \sum_{i=1}^{n} x_i p_i$$
Variance ($\sigma^2$ or $Var(X)$): Measures the spread or dispersion of the random variable around the mean.
Formula 1: $\sigma^2 = \sum_{i=1}^{n} (x_i - \mu)^2 p_i$
Formula 2 (Easier for calculation): $\sigma^2 = E(X^2) - [E(X)]^2$
Where $E(X^2) = \sum_{i=1}^{n} x_i^2 p_i$.

Standard Deviation ($\sigma$): The square root of the variance.
$$\sigma = \sqrt{Var(X)}$$
Practice Problem 7: Mean and Variance Question: Two coins are tossed simultaneously. Let X denote the number of heads. Find the probability distribution of X, its mean, and variance.
Solution:
1. Sample space $S = \{HH, HT, TH, TT\}$. $n(S) = 4$.
2. $X$ can take values 0 (no heads), 1 (one head), or 2 (two heads).
- $P(X=0) = P(\{TT\}) = 1/4$
- $P(X=1) = P(\{HT, TH\}) = 2/4 = 1/2$
- $P(X=2) = P(\{HH\}) = 1/4$
(Check: $1/4 + 1/2 + 1/4 = 1$. The distribution is valid).

3. Calculate Mean $E(X)$:
$E(X) = \sum x_i p_i = (0 \times \frac{1}{4}) + (1 \times \frac{1}{2}) + (2 \times \frac{1}{4}) = 0 + \frac{1}{2} + \frac{1}{2} = \mathbf{1}$.

4. Calculate Variance:
First find $E(X^2) = \sum x_i^2 p_i = (0^2 \times \frac{1}{4}) + (1^2 \times \frac{1}{2}) + (2^2 \times \frac{1}{4}) = 0 + \frac{1}{2} + 1 = 1.5 = 3/2$.
Variance $Var(X) = E(X^2) - [E(X)]^2 = \frac{3}{2} - (1)^2 = \frac{3}{2} - 1 = \mathbf{0.5 \text{ or } \frac{1}{2}}$.

9. Bernoulli Trials and Binomial Distribution

Note: While often rationalized in recent NCERT syllabi, the Binomial Distribution is absolutely essential for JEE, R.D. Sharma problems, and competitive exams.

Bernoulli Trials: A random experiment is a Bernoulli trial if it satisfies these conditions:
1. There is a finite number of trials.
2. The trials are independent.
3. Each trial has exactly two outcomes: Success ($S$) or Failure ($F$).
4. The probability of success ($p$) remains strictly constant in each trial. Probability of failure is $q = 1 - p$.

Binomial Distribution Formula The probability of getting exactly $r$ successes in $n$ Bernoulli trials is given by the Binomial Distribution formula:
$$P(X = r) = {^nC_r} \cdot p^r \cdot q^{n-r}$$ Where ${^nC_r} = \frac{n!}{r!(n-r)!}$ is the binomial coefficient.

Mean and Variance of Binomial Distribution:
- Mean ($\mu$) = $\mathbf{np}$
- Variance ($\sigma^2$) = $\mathbf{npq}$
- Standard Deviation ($\sigma$) = $\mathbf{\sqrt{npq}}$
Practice Problem 8: Binomial Probability Question: A fair die is thrown 5 times. Getting a '6' is considered a success. Find the probability of getting exactly 3 successes. Also, find the mean and variance of this distribution.
Solution:
1. Let success be getting a 6. Probability of success $p = 1/6$.
2. Probability of failure $q = 1 - p = 1 - 1/6 = 5/6$.
3. Total number of trials $n = 5$. We want $r = 3$ successes.
4. Apply Binomial Formula:
$P(X = 3) = {^5C_3} \cdot (1/6)^3 \cdot (5/6)^{5-3}$
$= {^5C_3} \cdot (1/6)^3 \cdot (5/6)^2$
We know ${^5C_3} = \frac{5!}{3!2!} = 10$.
$P(X = 3) = 10 \times \frac{1}{216} \times \frac{25}{36} = \frac{250}{7776} = \mathbf{\frac{125}{3888}}$.

5. Mean ($\mu$): $np = 5 \times (1/6) = \mathbf{5/6}$.
6. Variance ($\sigma^2$): $npq = 5 \times (1/6) \times (5/6) = \mathbf{25/36}$.
