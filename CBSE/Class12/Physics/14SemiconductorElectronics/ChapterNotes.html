Chapter 14: Semiconductor Electronics

Chapter Overview

This chapter bridges standard physics with modern electronic technology. It covers energy bands, p-n junctions, diodes, and their application as rectifiers.

14.1 Energy Bands in Solids

Inside a crystal, the discrete energy levels of individual atoms split into continuous bands of energy due to the interaction between neighboring atoms.

  • Valence Band (VB): The band occupied by valence electrons. It is almost full.
  • Conduction Band (CB): The band above the valence band. If an electron reaches this band, it becomes free to conduct electricity.
  • Energy Gap ($E_g$): The forbidden gap between VB and CB.

Classification Based on Conductivity:

  • Conductors: CB and VB overlap ($E_g \approx 0$). Plenty of free electrons available.
  • Insulators: Large band gap ($E_g > 3 \text{ eV}$). No electrons in Conduction Band.
  • Semiconductors: Small band gap ($E_g < 3 \text{ eV}$, e.g., Silicon $\approx 1.1 \text{ eV}$, Germanium $\approx 0.74 \text{ eV}$).

14.2 Intrinsic and Extrinsic Semiconductors

Intrinsic: Pure semiconductor without any impurities. Number of free electrons ($n_e$) equals number of holes ($n_h$).

Extrinsic: Reached by adding a small amount of impurity to an intrinsic semiconductor (Doping).

Types of Extrinsic Semiconductors:

  1. n-type: Doped with Pentavalent impurities (e.g., P, As, Sb). Each impurity atom donates an electron. $n_e \gg n_h$.
  2. p-type: Doped with Trivalent impurities (e.g., Al, B, In). Each impurity atom creates a hole (absence of electron). $n_h \gg n_e$.

14.3 p-n Junction Formation

When a p-type and n-type material are joined, a p-n junction is formed. Two main processes occur:

  • Diffusion: Holes from p-side and electrons from n-side cross the junction.
  • Drift: An internal electric field pulls minority carriers across the junction.

The region near the junction with no free charge carriers is called the Depletion Region. The potential difference across this region is the Barrier Potential ($V_B$).

14.4 Semiconductor Diode

A diode is a two-terminal p-n junction device. It allows current to flow in one direction but blocks it in the other.

Biasing of a Diode:

  1. Forward Bias: p-side connected to positive terminal, n-side to negative. Depletion layer width decreases. Current flows easily.
  2. Reverse Bias: p-side connected to negative, n-side to positive. Depletion layer width increases. Very little (leakage) current flows.

14.5 Diode as a Rectifier

Rectification is the process of converting Alternating Current (AC) into Direct Current (DC).

Half-wave Rectifier: Uses one diode. Only one half of the AC cycle is converted to DC.

Full-wave Rectifier: Uses two diodes (with center-tap transformer) or a bridge circuit. Both halves of the AC cycle are converted to DC.

Final Tip: For numericals, remember the Mass Action Law for semiconductors: $n_e \times n_h = n_i^2$, where $n_i$ is the intrinsic carrier concentration.
