Chapter Overview

In this chapter, we dive into the heart of the atom—the nucleus. We cover nuclear composition, binding energy, nuclear forces, and the high-energy processes of fission and fusion.

13.1 Composition and Size of Nucleus

The nucleus is composed of protons and neutrons, collectively known as nucleons. Atomic mass is measured in Atomic Mass Units (u), where $1 \text{ u} = \frac{1}{12}^{th}$ of the mass of a $^{12}\text{C}$ atom.

Nuclear Density: The nucleus is incredibly dense. Almost all mass of the atom is packed into a volume $10^{15}$ times smaller than the atom itself. Interestingly, nuclear density is independent of the mass number ($A$).

Nuclear Radius ($R$):

Experiments show that the volume of a nucleus is proportional to its mass number $A$. Therefore, the radius is:

$$ R = R_0 A^{1/3} $$

Where $R_0 \approx 1.2 \times 10^{-15} \text{ m}$ (or 1.2 femptometers/fermi).

13.2 Isotopes, Isobars, and Isotones

  • Isotopes: Atoms of same element having same atomic number ($Z$) but different mass number ($A$). (e.g., $^{1}\text{H}, ^{2}\text{H}, ^{3}\text{H}$).
  • Isobars: Atoms of different elements having same mass number ($A$) but different atomic number ($Z$). (e.g., $^{40}_{18}\text{Ar}, ^{40}_{20}\text{Ca}$).
  • Isotones: Atoms of different elements having same number of neutrons ($A-Z$).

13.3 Nuclear Force

The force that binds protons and neutrons together in the nucleus despite the strong electromagnetic repulsion between protons. It is the strongest known force in nature.

Key Properties:

  • Short-ranged (acts only over distances of $\sim$ few fermis).
  • Strongly attractive but becomes repulsive at very short distances (< 0.5 fm).
  • Charge-independent (acts same between $p-p, n-n, \text{ and } p-n$).
  • Non-central and dependent on spin.

13.4 Mass Defect and Binding Energy

The observed mass of a nucleus is always less than the sum of the masses of its constituent nucleons. This difference is called the Mass Defect ($\Delta m$).

$$ \Delta m = [Z m_p + (A-Z) m_n] - M_{nucleus} $$

Einstein's Mass-Energy Equivalence:

Energy and mass are inter-convertible: $E = \Delta m c^2$. The energy equivalent of the mass defect is the Binding Energy ($E_b$) of the nucleus.

$$ E_b = \Delta m c^2 $$

For $1 \text{ u}$ mass defect, the energy is approximately $931.5 \text{ MeV}$.

13.5 Radioactivity

The spontaneous disintegration of unstable nuclei by emitting radiations ($\alpha, \beta, \gamma$).

  • Alpha Decay: Emission of a helium nucleus $^{4}_{2}\text{He}$. Atomic number decreases by 2, mass number by 4.
  • Beta Decay: Emission of an electron ($\beta^-$) or positron ($\beta^+$). Atomic number changes by $\pm 1$, mass number remains same.
  • Gamma Decay: Emission of high-energy photons. Nucleus moves from excited state to ground state. No change in $A$ or $Z$.

13.6 Nuclear Reaction: Fission and Fusion

Nuclear Fission: A heavy nucleus splits into two medium-sized nuclei, releasing a massive amount of energy (e.g., Uranium-235 in nuclear reactors).

Nuclear Fusion: Two light nuclei combine to form a heavier nucleus, releasing even more energy than fission (e.g., source of energy in the Sun, Hydrogen-Hydrogen fusion).
