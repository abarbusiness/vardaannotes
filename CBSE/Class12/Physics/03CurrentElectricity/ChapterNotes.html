Chapter 3: Current Electricity

Dear Class 12 Student! Chapters 1 and 2 dealt with charges at rest (Electrostatics). Now, we set those charges into motion. Current Electricity is one of the highest-weightage chapters in both CBSE Boards and JEE Main. The derivations of Drift Velocity and Ohm's Law are almost guaranteed board questions, while Kirchhoff's rules and instrument-based problems dominate competitive exams. Let's conquer it!

3.1 Electric Current & Current Density

Electric Current ($I$): The time rate of flow of electric charge through any cross-section. $$ \begin{aligned} I = \lim_{\Delta t \to 0} \frac{\Delta q}{\Delta t} = \frac{dq}{dt} \end{aligned} $$ SI Unit: Ampere (A). It is a scalar quantity. Even though current has a direction (positive to negative), it does not obey vector addition laws (it obeys Kirchhoff's scalar addition).

Current Density ($\vec{J}$): The current flowing per unit area normal to the direction of flow. It is a vector quantity. $$ \begin{aligned} \vec{J} = \frac{dI}{dA} \hat{n} \quad \implies \quad I = \int \vec{J} \cdot d\vec{A} \end{aligned} $$ SI Unit: $A/m^2$. Its direction is the direction of conventional current (flow of positive charge).

3.2 Ohm’s Law

Macroscopic Ohm's Law: At a constant physical state (temperature, mechanical strain, etc.), the current flowing through a conductor is directly proportional to the potential difference applied across its ends. $$ \begin{aligned} V \propto I \implies V = IR \end{aligned} $$ Where $R$ is the electrical resistance of the conductor.

Board Alert Resistance ($R$): Opposition offered to the flow of charge. $R = \rho \frac{l}{A}$.
Resistivity ($\rho$): Resistance of a conductor of unit length and unit area. It depends only on the nature of the material and temperature, NOT on dimensions ($l, A$).

Vector Form of Ohm's Law (Microscopic Form)

Consider a conductor of length $l$ and area $A$. Potential difference $V = E l$, and current $I = J A$.
From $V = IR$, substituting these gives: $$ \begin{aligned} (E l) &= (J A) \left( \rho \frac{l}{A} \right) \\ E &= J \rho \\ J &= \frac{1}{\rho} E = \sigma E \end{aligned} $$ Where $\sigma = 1/\rho$ is the conductivity. In vector notation: $$ \begin{aligned} \boxed{\vec{J} = \sigma \vec{E}} \end{aligned} $$ This is the fundamental, microscopic form of Ohm's law valid at every point in a conductor.

3.3 Limitations of Ohm’s Law (Non-Ohmic Devices)

Although Ohm's law is extremely useful, it is not a fundamental law of nature. For many materials and devices used in practical circuits, the relation $V \propto I$ ceases to hold. These are known as Non-Ohmic devices.

The deviations from Ohm's law broadly fall into three categories:

  1. Non-linear V-I dependence: The value of $V$ ceases to be proportional to $I$. The slope of the V-I graph is not constant. (e.g., heating of a filament bulb).
  2. Dependence on the sign of V: The relation between $V$ and $I$ depends on the sign of $V$. Reversing the polarity of the voltage changes the magnitude of the current. (e.g., Semiconductor P-N Junction Diode).
  3. Non-unique relationship: The relation between $V$ and $I$ is not unique. There is more than one value of $V$ for the same current $I$. The graph contains a region of negative resistance. (e.g., Gallium Arsenide - GaAs).

3.4 Drift of Electrons

In the absence of an electric field, free electrons move randomly with high thermal velocities ($10^5$ m/s), but their average velocity is zero. When an electric field is applied, they experience a force and acquire a small net velocity in the direction opposite to $\vec{E}$, called Drift Velocity ($v_d$).

Derivation of Drift Velocity (VVIP for Boards)

Force on an electron: $\vec{F} = -e\vec{E}$.
Acceleration: $\vec{a} = \frac{-e\vec{E}}{m}$.
Using $v = u + at$ for an electron between two successive collisions, and averaging over all electrons: $$ \begin{aligned} \vec{v}_d = \langle \vec{u} \rangle + \vec{a} \langle t \rangle \end{aligned} $$ Since average thermal velocity $\langle \vec{u} \rangle = 0$, and average time between collisions is the Relaxation Time ($\tau$): $$ \begin{aligned} \boxed{\vec{v}_d = -\frac{e\vec{E}}{m}\tau} \end{aligned} $$ Magnitude: $v_d = \frac{eE\tau}{m}$. (Order of $v_d$ is ~ $10^{-4}$ m/s).

Relation between Current and Drift Velocity

Consider a conductor of area $A$, length $l$, and free electron density $n$ (electrons per unit volume).
Total number of electrons $N = n \times (A l)$.
Total charge $q = N e = nA l e$.
Time taken by electrons to cross the length $l$ is $t = \frac{l}{v_d}$.
Current $I = \frac{q}{t} = \frac{nA l e}{l / v_d} \implies \boxed{I = neA v_d}$

Mobility ($\mu$): The magnitude of drift velocity per unit electric field. $$ \begin{aligned} \mu = \frac{v_d}{E} = \frac{e\tau}{m} \end{aligned} $$ Mobility is always positive. SI unit: $m^2 V^{-1} s^{-1}$.

3.5 Temperature Dependence of Resistivity

The resistivity of a material depends on temperature according to the empirical relation: $$ \begin{aligned} \rho_T = \rho_0 [1 + \alpha(T - T_0)] \end{aligned} $$ Where $\alpha$ is the Temperature Coefficient of Resistivity.

  • Metals (Conductors): $\alpha$ is positive. As temperature increases, thermal vibrations of lattice ions increase, causing more frequent collisions (relaxation time $\tau$ decreases). Since $\rho = \frac{m}{ne^2\tau}$, $\rho$ increases nonlinearly.
  • Alloys (Nichrome, Manganin): $\alpha$ is very small but positive. Their resistivity is high and nearly independent of temperature, making them ideal for standard wire-bound resistors.
  • Semiconductors & Insulators: $\alpha$ is negative. As temperature increases, more covalent bonds break, exponentially increasing the charge carrier density ($n$). This dominates the decrease in $\tau$, so overall resistivity decreases.

3.6 Commercial Resistors & Colour Coding

Commercially produced resistors for domestic use or in laboratories are of two major types:

  • Wire bound resistors: Made by winding the wires of an alloy, viz., manganin, constantan, nichrome. These alloys are relatively insensitive to temperature.
  • Carbon resistors: Made from a mixture of carbon black, clay, and resin binder. They are compact, inexpensive, and therefore extensively used in electronic circuits.

3.7 Combination of Resistors

Like capacitors, resistors can be combined to achieve a desired equivalent resistance in a circuit.

1. Resistors in Series

Two or more resistors are in series if the same current ($I$) flows through all of them when a potential difference is applied. The total potential difference $V$ across the combination is the sum of potential differences across individual resistors. $$ \begin{aligned} V &= V_1 + V_2 + V_3 \\ I R_{eq} &= I R_1 + I R_2 + I R_3 \\ \boxed{R_{eq} &= R_1 + R_2 + R_3 + \dots} \end{aligned} $$ The equivalent resistance is always greater than the greatest individual resistance in the series combination.

2. Resistors in Parallel

Two or more resistors are in parallel if the same potential difference ($V$) exists across all of them. The total current $I$ drawn from the source is the sum of currents through individual resistors. $$ \begin{aligned} I &= I_1 + I_2 + I_3 \\ \frac{V}{R_{eq}} &= \frac{V}{R_1} + \frac{V}{R_2} + \frac{V}{R_3} \\ \boxed{\frac{1}{R_{eq}} &= \frac{1}{R_1} + \frac{1}{R_2} + \frac{1}{R_3} + \dots} \end{aligned} $$ The equivalent resistance is always less than the smallest individual resistance in the parallel combination.

3.8 Electrical Energy and Power

When a current $I$ flows through a resistor $R$ for time $t$ under a potential difference $V$, the electrical energy consumed (and dissipated as heat) is: $$ \begin{aligned} E = W = V I t = I^2 R t = \frac{V^2}{R} t \quad \text{(Joule's Law of Heating)} \end{aligned} $$

Electrical Power ($P$): The rate at which electrical energy is dissipated. $$ \begin{aligned} P = VI = I^2R = \frac{V^2}{R} \end{aligned} $$ SI Unit: Watt (W). Commercial unit of energy is the Kilowatt-hour (kWh). $1 \text{ kWh} = 3.6 \times 10^6 \text{ Joules}$.

3.9 Cells, EMF, and Internal Resistance

Electromotive Force (EMF, $E$): The potential difference between the terminals of a cell when no current is drawn from it (open circuit). It is the work done by the cell in driving a unit charge around the complete circuit.

Internal Resistance ($r$): The resistance offered by the electrolyte and electrodes of a cell. Thus, when a current $I$ is drawn, a potential drop $Ir$ occurs inside the cell.

Terminal Potential Difference ($V$): The potential difference across the cell when current is flowing (closed circuit). $$ \begin{aligned} \boxed{V = E - Ir} \quad \text{(During Discharging)} \end{aligned} $$ $$ \begin{aligned} V = E + Ir \quad \text{(During Charging)} \end{aligned} $$

Grouping of Cells 1. Series Grouping: $n$ identical cells ($E, r$) connected in series to external resistor $R$.
$E_{eq} = nE$, $r_{eq} = nr \implies I = \frac{nE}{R + nr}$.

2. Parallel Grouping: $m$ identical cells ($E, r$) connected in parallel to external resistor $R$.
$E_{eq} = E$, $r_{eq} = \frac{r}{m} \implies I = \frac{E}{R + r/m} = \frac{mE}{mR + r}$.

Maximum Power Transfer Theorem: Maximum power is delivered to the external load when external resistance equals total internal resistance ($R = r_{eq}$).

3.10 Kirchhoff’s Rules

For complex circuits where simple series and parallel combinations fail, we use Kirchhoff's Rules.

1. Kirchhoff's Junction Rule (KCL)

The algebraic sum of currents meeting at a junction is zero. $\sum I = 0$. Alternatively, sum of currents entering a junction equals sum of currents leaving the junction.
Principle: Based on the Conservation of Charge. Charges cannot accumulate at a junction.

2. Kirchhoff's Loop Rule (KVL)

The algebraic sum of changes in potential around any closed loop is zero. $\sum \Delta V = 0$.
Principle: Based on the Conservation of Energy. The electrostatic force is conservative, so work done in a closed path is zero.

3.11 Circuit Analysis Techniques (JEE/NEET Focus)

In competitive exams like JEE Mains and NEET, finding equivalent resistance of complex 2D or 3D resistor grids requires advanced shortcuts.

1. Nodal Analysis (KCL Shortcut)

Nodal analysis simplifies circuits by assigning potentials to junctions instead of solving multiple KVL loops.

  1. Assign $0\text{V}$ (Ground) to one junction (usually the negative terminal of the battery).
  2. Assign $V$ to the positive terminal. Assign unknown potentials ($x, y, z$) to other key junctions.
  3. Apply KCL ($\sum I = 0$) at the unknown junction: $\frac{x - V_1}{R_1} + \frac{x - V_2}{R_2} + \dots = 0$.
  4. Solve for $x$ to find the current through any branch!

2. Symmetry Rules for Equivalent Resistance

Symmetry drastically reduces the complexity of resistor grids.

  • Mirror Symmetry (Perpendicular Axis Symmetry): If a circuit is symmetric about an axis perpendicular to the terminals (input/output), the nodes on opposite sides of the mirror are at the same potential. The currents entering the mirror must perfectly match the currents leaving the mirror. Junctions lying exactly on the mirror axis can often be split or detached.
  • Folding Symmetry (Parallel Axis Symmetry): If a circuit is symmetric about an axis parallel to the terminals, points directly opposite each other across the axis are at the exact same potential. These points can be "folded" and joined together, placing their corresponding resistors in parallel ($R/2$).

3.12 Wheatstone Bridge

An arrangement of four resistances $P, Q, R, S$ used to measure an unknown resistance accurately.

Balanced Condition: If the galvanometer connected between the opposite junctions shows zero deflection ($I_g = 0$), the bridge is balanced. In this state, the potentials at the two junctions are equal. $$ \begin{aligned} \frac{P}{Q} = \frac{R}{S} \end{aligned} $$

3.13 Meter Bridge (Slide Wire Bridge)

The Meter Bridge is the simplest practical application of the Wheatstone bridge, used to measure an unknown resistance.

It consists of a uniform wire of exactly 1 meter (100 cm) in length, stretched between two thick metallic strips. An unknown resistance $R$ and a standard known resistance $S$ are connected across the gaps. A galvanometer is connected to a jockey which can slide over the wire.

When the jockey is moved to find the null point (zero deflection in the galvanometer) at a distance $l$ cm from the left end, the bridge is balanced. The lengths $l$ and $(100 - l)$ of the wire act as the resistances $P$ and $Q$. $$ \begin{aligned} \frac{R}{S} = \frac{l}{100 - l} \implies R = S \left( \frac{l}{100 - l} \right) \end{aligned} $$

3.14 Potentiometer

The potentiometer is an ideal device to measure potential difference because it draws zero current from the circuit at the null point (it acts like an ideal voltmeter with infinite resistance).

Principle: For a wire of uniform cross-section and uniform composition carrying a steady current, the potential drop $V$ across any portion of the wire is directly proportional to its length $l$. $$ \begin{aligned} V \propto l \implies V = k l \end{aligned} $$ Where $k$ is the Potential Gradient (V/m).

3.15 Galvanometer Conversion

A galvanometer (resistance $G$) is a sensitive device that gives full-scale deflection for a very small current ($I_g$).

Conversion to Ammeter Conversion to Voltmeter
Measures Current. Connected in Series in the main circuit. Measures Potential Difference. Connected in Parallel to the component.
Connect a small resistance Shunt ($S$) in Parallel to the galvanometer. Connect a high resistance Multiplier ($R$) in Series with the galvanometer.
$S = \frac{I_g \times G}{I - I_g}$ $R = \frac{V}{I_g} - G$
Ideal Ammeter Resistance = $0$ Ideal Voltmeter Resistance = $\infty$

Board / JEE Example Question: A galvanometer with a coil resistance of $120 \, \Omega$ shows full-scale deflection for a current of $2.5 \text{ mA}$. How will you convert it into an ammeter of range $0$ to $7.5 \text{ A}$?
Solution:
Given: $G = 120 \, \Omega$, $I_g = 2.5 \text{ mA} = 2.5 \times 10^{-3} \text{ A}$, $I = 7.5 \text{ A}$.
To convert to an ammeter, connect a shunt resistance $S$ in parallel.
$S = \frac{I_g G}{I - I_g} = \frac{(2.5 \times 10^{-3}) \times 120}{7.5 - 0.0025}$
Since $0.0025$ is very small compared to $7.5$, $I - I_g \approx 7.5$.
$$ \begin{aligned} S \approx \frac{300 \times 10^{-3}}{7.5} = \frac{0.3}{7.5} = \mathbf{0.04 \, \Omega} \end{aligned} $$ Answer: By connecting a shunt resistance of $0.04 \, \Omega$ in parallel.
