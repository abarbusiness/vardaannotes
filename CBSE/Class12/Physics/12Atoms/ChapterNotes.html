Chapter Overview

This chapter explores the internal structure of atoms, moving from Rutherford's planetary model to Bohr's quantized orbits and the spectral series of hydrogen.

12.1 Alpha-particle Scattering Experiment

In 1911, H. Geiger and E. Marsden, under Ernest Rutherford's direction, performed the gold-foil experiment. They bombarded a thin gold foil with high-energy alpha particles ($\text{He}^{2+}$) emitted from a bismuth source.

Observations:

  • Most alpha particles passed straight through the foil.
  • About 0.14% were scattered by more than $1^\circ$.
  • About 1 in 8000 particles were deflected by more than $90^\circ$.
  • Very rarely, a particle was deflected by $180^\circ$ (rebounded).

Impact Parameter ($b$): The perpendicular distance of the initial velocity vector of the alpha particle from the center of the nucleus.
- Small $b \implies$ Large scattering angle.
- Large $b \implies$ Almost no scattering.

12.2 Rutherford's Nuclear Model

Rutherford concluded that the entire positive charge and most of the mass of the atom are concentrated in a tiny central region called the Nucleus. Electrons revolve around it in orbits, like planets around the sun.

Distance of Closest Approach ($d_0$):

At the point of closest approach, the kinetic energy of the alpha particle is completely converted into electrostatic potential energy.

$$ d_0 = \frac{1}{4\pi\varepsilon_0} \frac{2Ze^2}{K} $$

Where $Z$ is atomic number, $e$ is electronic charge, and $K$ is initial kinetic energy.

Drawbacks of Rutherford's Model:

  1. Atomic Instability: An accelerated electron (in circular motion) should continuously radiate energy and spiral into the nucleus.
  2. Line Spectra: It couldn't explain why atoms emit light only at specific discrete frequencies.

12.3 Bohr's Model of Hydrogen Atom

Neils Bohr combined classical and quantum concepts through three postulates:

  1. Stable Orbits: Electrons revolve in specific non-radiating "stationary" orbits.
  2. Angular Momentum Quantization: The angular momentum ($L$) is an integral multiple of $h/2\pi$.
    $$ L = mvr = \frac{nh}{2\pi} $$
  3. Energy Quantization: Radiation is emitted only when an electron jumps from a higher energy state ($E_i$) to a lower energy state ($E_f$).
    $$ h\nu = E_i - E_f $$

12.4 Energy Levels and Spectral Series

The total energy of an electron in the $n^{th}$ orbit of a hydrogen atom is given by:

$$ E_n = -\frac{13.6}{n^2} \text{ eV} $$

The negative sign indicates that the electron is bound to the nucleus.

Spectral Series of Hydrogen:

Series Lower Orbit ($n_f$) Higher Orbit ($n_i$) Region
Lyman 1 2, 3, 4... Ultraviolet
Balmer 2 3, 4, 5... Visible
Paschen 3 4, 5, 6... Infrared
Brackett 4 5, 6, 7... Infrared
Pfund 5 6, 7, 8... Infrared

12.5 De Broglie's Explanation

De Broglie explained Bohr's quantization condition by proposing that an electron in an orbit is a standing wave. For a constructive interference, the circumference of the orbit must be an integral multiple of the wavelength.

$$ 2\pi r = n\lambda $$

Substituting $\lambda = h/mv$, we get $mvr = nh/2\pi$.
