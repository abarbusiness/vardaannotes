Chapter 11: Dual Nature of Radiation and Matter

Dear Class 12 Student! For decades, scientists argued whether light was a wave or a particle. This chapter provides the bridge: Light (and all matter) behaves as both. In Boards, focus on Einstein's derivation and the experimental graphs. In JEE, the De Broglie wavelength transitions and photon counting problems are essential. Let's explore the quantum reality!

1. Electron Emission

In metals, the outer electrons are free to move within the lattice but are prevented from escaping by an attractive force from the positive ions. This creates a surface barrier.

Concept Work Function ($\Phi_0$ or $W$): The minimum energy required by an electron to just escape from the metal surface with zero velocity.

Electron-volt (eV): A smaller unit of energy suitable for atomic physics. It is the kinetic energy gained by an electron when accelerated through a potential difference of 1 Volt.
$1 \text{ eV} = 1.602 \times 10^{-19} \text{ Joules}$

Types of Electron Emission

  • Thermionic Emission: By heating the metal, thermal energy is supplied to the free electrons, allowing them to overcome the surface barrier.
  • Field Emission: By applying an exceptionally strong electric field ($\sim 10^8 \text{ V/m}$) to the metal surface, electrons are literally "pulled out".
  • Photoelectric Emission: When electromagnetic radiation of a suitable high frequency falls on the metal surface, electrons are emitted.

2. Photoelectric Effect: Basic Concepts

The phenomenon of emission of electrons from a metal surface when light of a sufficiently high frequency falls on it is called the Photoelectric Effect.

  • Hertz's Observation: Observed that high voltage sparks across detector loops were enhanced when the emitter plate was illuminated by UV light.
  • Hallwachs' and Lenard's Observations: Lenard noticed that when UV light fell on the emitter plate of an evacuated tube, a current flowed. Hallwachs observed that a zinc plate connected to an electroscope lost its negative charge when illuminated by UV light.

The experimental setup consists of an evacuated glass/quartz tube containing a photosensitive plate (Emitter $C$) and a metal plate (Collector $A$).

Key Experimental Findings

  1. Intensity vs. Photocurrent: For a fixed frequency above the threshold, the photocurrent is directly proportional to the intensity of incident light. (More intensity = more photons = more electrons).
  2. Collector Potential vs. Photocurrent:
    • Saturation Current: The maximum current achieved when all emitted photoelectrons reach the collector.
    • Stopping Potential ($V_0$): The negative (retarding) potential applied to the collector for which the photocurrent becomes zero. It depends on the maximum kinetic energy of the electrons: $K_{max} = e V_0$.
  3. Frequency vs. Stopping Potential:
    • Threshold Frequency ($\nu_0$): The minimum frequency below which no emission occurs, regardless of intensity.
    • For $\nu > \nu_0$, stopping potential increases linearly with frequency.

4. Failure of Wave Theory of Light

Classical wave theory (Maxwell's EM waves) failed to explain three critical aspects of the photoelectric effect:

  1. Kinetic Energy: Wave theory says higher intensity (higher amplitude) should give electrons more energy. Experimentally, $K_{max}$ is independent of intensity.
  2. Threshold Frequency: Wave theory suggests any frequency should cause emission if light is intense enough. Experimentally, no emission occurs below $\nu_0$.
  3. Time Lag: Wave theory implies it should take time (hours) for a single electron to absorb enough continuous wave energy to escape. Experimentally, emission is instantaneous ($\sim 10^{-9} \text{ s}$).

5. Einstein's Photoelectric Equation (Crucial Derivation)

In 1905, Albert Einstein proposed that light is not just a continuous wave, but consists of discrete "packets" of energy called Photons (Quanta). Energy of one photon is $E = h\nu$.

Derivation & Formulas According to Einstein, when a photon hits an electron, it gives its entire energy ($h\nu$) to the electron. This energy is used for two purposes:
1. To overcome the surface barrier (Work Function $\Phi_0$).
2. The remaining energy appears as Maximum Kinetic Energy ($K_{max}$).

$h\nu = \Phi_0 + K_{max}$
$$K_{max} = h\nu - h\nu_0 = h(\nu - \nu_0)$$
Since $K_{max} = eV_0$ and $\nu = c/\lambda$:
$$e V_0 = hc \left( \frac{1}{\lambda} - \frac{1}{\lambda_0} \right)$$

Verification: This equation explains everything! If $\nu < \nu_0$, $K_{max}$ becomes negative (impossible), hence no emission. Higher intensity means more photons, thus more current, but the energy of each photon remains $h\nu$, so $K_{max}$ stays the same.

6. Photon Picture of Electromagnetic Radiation

Light interacts with matter as if it is made of particles called photons.

  • Photons travel at speed $c = 3 \times 10^8 \text{ m/s}$ in vacuum.
  • Energy $E = h\nu = \frac{hc}{\lambda}$ and Momentum $p = \frac{E}{c} = \frac{h}{\lambda}$.
  • Photons are electrically neutral and have zero rest mass.
  • In a collision, energy and momentum are conserved, but the number of photons may change (absorption).
JEE Main Transition: Photon Flux If a light source has power $P$ and wavelength $\lambda$, the number of photons emitted per second ($n$) is:
$$n = \frac{P}{E} = \frac{P \lambda}{hc}$$ Numerical Tip: Use $hc \approx 1240 \text{ eV}\cdot\text{nm}$ or $12400 \text{ eV}\cdot\text{Å}$ for quick calculations.

7. Wave Nature of Matter (De Broglie Hypothesis)

In 1924, Louis de Broglie proposed that since radiation has particle-like properties, material particles (like electrons) should have wave-like properties. This is Matter Waves.

De Broglie Wavelength ($\lambda$): $$\lambda = \frac{h}{p} = \frac{h}{mv}$$

Wavelength of an Accelerated Electron

If an electron is accelerated through a potential difference $V$, its kinetic energy $K = eV$.
Since $p = \sqrt{2mK} = \sqrt{2meV}$:
$$\lambda = \frac{h}{\sqrt{2meV}}$$

Shortcuts By substituting the values of $h, m, e$:
$$\lambda = \frac{1.227}{\sqrt{V}} \text{ nm} = \frac{12.27}{\sqrt{V}} \text{ Å}$$
JEE Main Transition: Thermal & Heavy Particles 1. Thermal Neutrons: For a particle at temperature $T$ (Kelvin), $K = \frac{3}{2} k_B T$.
$$\lambda = \frac{h}{\sqrt{3 m k_B T}}$$
2. Particle Comparisons: For same accelerating potential $V$, $\lambda \propto \frac{1}{\sqrt{mq}}$.
- $\lambda_e$ (Electron) is the largest because mass is smallest.
- Order of wavelength for same $V$: Electron > Proton > Deuteron > Alpha.

8. Davisson-Germer Experiment

The first experimental evidence for matter waves. They scattered a beam of electrons off a Nickel crystal and observed a diffraction pattern similar to X-rays.

  • A sharp peak in intensity was observed at $V = 54 \text{ V}$ and $\theta = 50^\circ$.
  • The wavelength calculated from diffraction ($\approx 0.165 \text{ nm}$) matched the De Broglie wavelength ($\approx 0.167 \text{ nm}$) almost perfectly.
Practice Problem 1 Question: Light of frequency $7.21 \times 10^{14} \text{ Hz}$ is incident on a metal surface. Electrons with a maximum speed of $6.0 \times 10^5 \text{ m/s}$ are ejected. What is the threshold frequency for the metal?
Solution:
1. Apply $h\nu = h\nu_0 + K_{max}$.
2. $K_{max} = \frac{1}{2} m v^2 = 0.5 \times (9.1 \times 10^{-31}) \times (6 \times 10^5)^2 \approx 1.638 \times 10^{-19} \text{ J}$.
3. $h\nu_0 = h\nu - K_{max} = (6.63 \times 10^{-34} \times 7.21 \times 10^{14}) - 1.638 \times 10^{-19}$.
4. $h\nu_0 = 4.78 \times 10^{-19} - 1.638 \times 10^{-19} = 3.142 \times 10^{-19} \text{ J}$.
5. $\nu_0 = \frac{3.142 \times 10^{-19}}{6.63 \times 10^{-34}} \approx \mathbf{4.74 \times 10^{14} \text{ Hz}}$.
Practice Problem 2 Question: Calculate the De Broglie wavelength of an electron accelerated through a potential of $100 \text{ V}$.
Solution:
Using the shortcut formula: $\lambda = \frac{12.27}{\sqrt{V}} \text{ Å}$
$\lambda = \frac{12.27}{\sqrt{100}} = \frac{12.27}{10} = \mathbf{1.227 \text{ Å}}$.
