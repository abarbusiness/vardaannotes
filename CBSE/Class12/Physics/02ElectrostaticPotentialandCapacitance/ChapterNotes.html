1. Electrostatic Potential and Potential Energy Difference

[AI Image Placeholder: Landscape (16:9) - Background: #FFFFFF]
AI Prompt: "A diagram showing the concept of Electrostatic Potential. A large positive source charge $+Q$ is fixed at the origin, producing a radially outward electric field (shown with faint arrows). A small positive test charge $+q_0$ is being moved from point A to point B along an arbitrary curved path against the repulsive electric field. Show an external force vector $\vec{F}_{ext}$ pushing it towards $+Q$, and the electrostatic force $\vec{F}_E$ pushing it away. Pure white background #FFFFFF, clear educational labels."

Conservative Nature of Electrostatic Force: The work done by the electrostatic force in moving a charge from one point to another depends only on the initial and final positions and is completely independent of the path taken. Because of this property, we can define a potential energy for the electrostatic field.

Definitions
  • Electrostatic Potential Difference ($V_B - V_A$): The work done by an external force in bringing a unit positive test charge from point A to point B without any acceleration. $$V_B - V_A = \frac{W_{AB}}{q_0}$$
  • Electrostatic Potential at a Point ($V$): The work done in bringing a unit positive test charge from infinity to that point against the electrostatic force. (Here, reference point A is at infinity, where $V_\infty = 0$). $$V = \frac{W_{\infty \to P}}{q_0}$$
  • SI Unit: Volt (V) or Joule/Coulomb (J/C). It is a Scalar Quantity.

Derivation: Potential due to a Point Charge

Let a source charge $+q$ be at the origin. We want to find potential at point P at a distance $r$.
Work done by external force against electric field $\vec{E}$ to move a test charge $q_0$ through a small distance $d\vec{r}$ is $dW = -\vec{F}_e \cdot d\vec{r} = -q_0 \vec{E} \cdot d\vec{r}$.
Total work done from infinity to $r$: $$W = \int_{\infty}^{r} -q_0 E \, dr = -q_0 \int_{\infty}^{r} \left( \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0} \frac{q}{r^2} \right) dr$$ $$W = -\frac{q q_0}{4\pi\epsilon_0} \left[ -\frac{1}{r} \right]_{\infty}^{r} = \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0} \frac{q q_0}{r}$$ Since $V = W/q_0$, we get:

Formula & Graph $$V = \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0} \frac{q}{r}$$ Comparison: Electric field $E \propto 1/r^2$ (falls off rapidly). Potential $V \propto 1/r$ (falls off slower).

Superposition Principle for Potential

Since potential is a scalar quantity, the net potential at a point due to a system of charges is simply the algebraic sum (taking signs of charges into account) of the individual potentials.

$$V_{net} = V_1 + V_2 + \dots + V_n = \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0} \sum_{i=1}^{n} \frac{q_i}{r_i}$$
JEE Main Transition: Continuous Charge Distribution For a uniformly charged ring of radius $R$ and total charge $Q$:
- Potential at the center: $V = \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0} \frac{Q}{R}$
- Potential on the axis at distance $x$: $V = \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0} \frac{Q}{\sqrt{R^2 + x^2}}$
Practice Problem 1 Question: Four point charges $q, -q, q, -q$ are placed at the corners of a square of side $a$. Find the electrostatic potential at the center of the square.
Solution:
Let the diagonal of the square be $d = a\sqrt{2}$. The distance from each corner to the center is $r = \frac{a\sqrt{2}}{2} = \frac{a}{\sqrt{2}}$.
Since potential is a scalar, we simply add them algebraically:
$V_{net} = V_1 + V_2 + V_3 + V_4$
$V_{net} = \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0} \left( \frac{q}{r} - \frac{q}{r} + \frac{q}{r} - \frac{q}{r} \right) = \mathbf{0 \text{ V}}$.
Even though the electric field at the center might not be zero depending on arrangement, the scalar potential perfectly cancels out!

2. Potential due to an Electric Dipole

[AI Image Placeholder: Landscape (16:9) - Background: #FFFFFF]
AI Prompt: "Diagram for potential due to an electric dipole at a general point. A dipole with charges -q at A and +q at B, separated by distance 2a. Origin O is exactly in the middle. A point P is located at distance r from origin O, such that the line OP makes an angle $\theta$ with the dipole axis (vector p). Draw lines from -q to P (labeled $r_1$) and from +q to P (labeled $r_2$). Show a perpendicular dropped from A to the line OP to indicate path difference. White background #FFFFFF."

A. General Point Derivation ($r, \theta$)

Let point $P$ be at a distance $r$ from the center of the dipole, making an angle $\theta$ with the dipole moment $\vec{p}$.
$V_{net} = V_{+q} + V_{-q} = \frac{q}{4\pi\epsilon_0} \left[ \frac{1}{r_1} - \frac{1}{r_2} \right]$
Using geometry and binomial approximation for a short dipole ($r \gg a$), we find $\frac{1}{r_1} - \frac{1}{r_2} \approx \frac{2a \cos\theta}{r^2}$.
Since $p = q \times 2a$, we get:

$$V = \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0} \frac{p \cos\theta}{r^2}$$

B. Special Cases

  • On the Axial Line: $\theta = 0^\circ$ (towards $+q$) or $180^\circ$ (towards $-q$). $\cos 0^\circ = 1$.
    $V_{axial} = \pm \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0} \frac{p}{r^2}$
  • On the Equatorial Line: $\theta = 90^\circ$. $\cos 90^\circ = 0$.
    $V_{eq} = 0$. (Every point on the equatorial plane is equidistant from $+q$ and $-q$, so their potentials perfectly cancel out).
Practice Problem 2 Question: How much work is done in moving a test charge $q_0$ from one point to another point on the equatorial plane of an electric dipole?
Solution:
The potential everywhere on the equatorial plane of a dipole is exactly $0 \text{ V}$.
Therefore, for any two points A and B on this plane, $V_A = 0$ and $V_B = 0$.
Potential difference $\Delta V = V_B - V_A = 0$.
Work done $W = q_0 \Delta V = q_0 \times 0 = \mathbf{0 \text{ Joules}}$.

3. Equipotential Surfaces

[AI Image Placeholder: Landscape (16:9) - Background: #FFFFFF]
AI Prompt: "Three distinct panels showing Equipotential Surfaces. Panel 1: A point charge (+q) with concentric dashed circles around it representing equipotential surfaces. Electric field lines (solid arrows) radiate outward, perpendicular to the circles. Panel 2: A uniform electric field (horizontal arrows) passing through vertical parallel flat planes (equipotential surfaces). Panel 3: An electric dipole (+ and -). The equipotential surfaces are tight around the charges and spread out further away, with a straight vertical line exactly down the middle representing V=0. White background #FFFFFF."

Definition: Any surface that has same electric potential at every point on it is called an equipotential surface.

Key Properties (Highly Tested in Boards)
  1. No work is done in moving a test charge over an equipotential surface. ($W = q\Delta V$, and since $\Delta V = 0$, $W = 0$).
  2. Electric field is always strictly perpendicular to the equipotential surface. (If it weren't, it would have a parallel component, which would require work to move a charge along the surface—violating property 1).
  3. Two equipotential surfaces can never intersect. (If they did, the point of intersection would have two different values of potential simultaneously, which is impossible).
  4. The spacing between equipotential surfaces indicates the strength of the electric field. They are crowded where the field is strong and further apart where the field is weak.

Relation between Electric Field and Potential

Work done in moving a unit positive charge by distance $dr$ against the field is $dW = -E \cdot dr$.
By definition, this work done is the potential difference $dV$.

$$E = -\frac{dV}{dr}$$

The quantity $\frac{dV}{dr}$ is called the Potential Gradient. The negative sign indicates that the direction of the electric field is always in the direction of decreasing potential.

JEE Main Transition: Partial Differentiation In 3D space, if $V(x,y,z)$ is given, the electric field vector is the negative gradient of V: $$\vec{E} = -\nabla V = -\left(\frac{\partial V}{\partial x}\hat{i} + \frac{\partial V}{\partial y}\hat{j} + \frac{\partial V}{\partial z}\hat{k}\right)$$
Practice Problem 3 Question: The electric potential in a region is given by $V = 2x^2 + 3y \text{ Volts}$. Find the electric field vector $\vec{E}$ at the point $(1, 2)$.
Solution:
Using partial differentiation:
$E_x = -\frac{\partial V}{\partial x} = -\frac{d}{dx}(2x^2 + 3y) = -4x$
$E_y = -\frac{\partial V}{\partial y} = -\frac{d}{dy}(2x^2 + 3y) = -3$
$E_z = -\frac{\partial V}{\partial z} = 0$

So, $\vec{E} = -4x \hat{i} - 3 \hat{j}$.
At point $(1, 2)$, substitute $x = 1$:
$\vec{E} = -4(1)\hat{i} - 3\hat{j} = \mathbf{-4\hat{i} - 3\hat{j} \text{ V/m}}$.

4. Potential Energy of a System of Charges

[AI Image Placeholder: Landscape (16:9) - Background: #FFFFFF]
AI Prompt: "Visualizing Potential Energy of a discrete charge system. Three charges $q_1$, $q_2$, and $q_3$ forming a triangle. Connect them with dashed lines indicating their pair-wise distances: $r_{12}$, $r_{23}$, and $r_{13}$. Show the formula $U = U_{12} + U_{23} + U_{13}$ clearly. White background #FFFFFF."

The potential energy of a system of charges is the total work done by an external agent in assembling those charges from infinity to their present locations.

  • For Two Point Charges: Bringing $q_1$ to an empty space requires no work ($W_1 = 0$). Bringing $q_2$ to a distance $r_{12}$ from $q_1$ requires work against $q_1$'s potential. $$U = W_2 = q_2 \times V_1 = q_2 \left( \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0} \frac{q_1}{r_{12}} \right) = \mathbf{\frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0}\frac{q_1 q_2}{r_{12}}}$$
  • For Multiple Charges: Calculate the potential energy for every possible pair and add them up algebraically. For $n$ charges, there are $\frac{n(n-1)}{2}$ pairs.

Potential Energy in an External Field

  • Single charge $q$: $U = qV(\vec{r})$, where $V$ is the external potential at that point.
  • Two charges $q_1, q_2$: You must account for the external field AND their interaction with each other.
    $U = q_1 V(\vec{r}_1) + q_2 V(\vec{r}_2) + \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0}\frac{q_1 q_2}{r_{12}}$

Potential Energy of a Dipole in an External Field

As derived in Chapter 1, the work done in rotating a dipole is stored as potential energy: $$U = -\vec{p} \cdot \vec{E} = -pE \cos\theta$$

Practice Problem 4 Question: Calculate the work required to completely separate two charges $+3\mu C$ and $-2\mu C$ placed $10 \text{ cm}$ apart.
Solution:
Work required to completely separate them means taking them to infinity, where $U_f = 0$.
Initial Potential Energy ($U_i$):
$U_i = \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0}\frac{q_1 q_2}{r}$
$U_i = (9 \times 10^9) \times \frac{(3 \times 10^{-6}) \times (-2 \times 10^{-6})}{0.10}$
$U_i = 9 \times 10^9 \times \frac{-6 \times 10^{-12}}{0.1} = \frac{-0.054}{0.1} = -0.54 \text{ Joules}$.

Work Done by external agent = $U_{final} - U_{initial} = 0 - (-0.54) = \mathbf{+0.54 \text{ Joules}}$.

5. Electrostatics of Conductors

[AI Image Placeholder: Landscape (16:9) - Background: #FFFFFF]
AI Prompt: "Diagram showing properties of a Conductor in electrostatic equilibrium. An irregularly shaped solid metal object. Inside the object, write 'E = 0' and 'V = Constant'. On the outer surface, draw positive charges. From the positive charges, draw electric field lines pointing OUTWARD, specifically indicating that they exit precisely at a 90-degree (perpendicular) angle to the surface. Next to it, show a hollow shell with a cavity inside, labeling the cavity 'Electrostatic Shielding E=0'. White background #FFFFFF."
Important Properties of Conductors (Must Know)
  1. Net electric field inside a conductor is ZERO. (If E wasn't zero, free electrons would move, creating a current. But we are in electro*statics*!).
  2. Electric field just outside a charged conductor is strictly perpendicular to the surface. (Otherwise, a tangential component would cause surface currents).
  3. Net charge in the interior of a conductor is zero. Any excess charge placed on a conductor entirely resides on its outer surface.
  4. Potential is constant within and on the surface of a conductor. (Since $E = -dV/dr = 0$ inside, $V$ must be constant. The entire conductor is an equipotential volume).
  5. Electric field at the surface is $E = \frac{\sigma}{\epsilon_0}\hat{n}$.

Electrostatic Shielding: The phenomenon of making a region free from any electric field is called electrostatic shielding. Since $E=0$ inside a hollow conductor (a Faraday cage), sensitive instruments are protected by enclosing them in metallic boxes.

6. Dielectrics and Polarization

[AI Image Placeholder: Landscape (16:9) - Background: #FFFFFF]
AI Prompt: "Comparison of Non-polar and Polar dielectrics. Left panel: Non-polar molecules (circles with perfectly overlapping + and - centers) under E=0. Right panel: When E is applied (arrow pointing right), the + and - centers shift slightly in opposite directions, turning the circles into ovals (induced dipoles) aligning with the field. Label this 'Polarization'. White background #FFFFFF."

Dielectrics are non-conducting substances (insulators). They have no free electrons.

  • Polar Molecules: The centers of positive and negative charges do not coincide (e.g., $H_2O, HCl$). They have a permanent intrinsic dipole moment.
  • Non-polar Molecules: The centers coincide (e.g., $H_2, CO_2$). Net dipole moment is zero.

Polarization: When any dielectric is placed in an external electric field ($E_0$), the molecules stretch or align such that a net dipole moment develops in the direction of the field. This induced field ($E_{in}$) opposes the external field, reducing the net field inside the dielectric ($E_{net} = E_0 - E_{in}$).

Polarization Vector ($\vec{P}$): The induced dipole moment developed per unit volume of the dielectric. For linear isotropic dielectrics: $\vec{P} = \chi_e \epsilon_0 \vec{E}$, where $\chi_e$ is the Electric Susceptibility.

7. Capacitors and Capacitance

[AI Image Placeholder: Square (1:1) - Background: #FFFFFF]
AI Prompt: "A diagram of a Parallel Plate Capacitor. Two large parallel metallic plates, area A, separated by distance d. Top plate is charged +Q, bottom plate is -Q. Uniform, straight electric field lines (E) point from the top plate to the bottom plate. White background #FFFFFF."

A capacitor is a system of two conductors separated by an insulator, used to store electrical charge and electrostatic energy.

Capacitance ($C$): The ratio of charge $Q$ given to a conductor to the potential $V$ raised. $$C = \frac{Q}{V}$$ SI Unit is Farad (F). ($1 \text{ F} = 1 \text{ Coulomb/Volt}$).

A. Capacitance of an Isolated Spherical Conductor

For a sphere of radius $R$, potential at the surface is $V = \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0}\frac{Q}{R}$.
$C = Q/V \implies$ $C = 4\pi\epsilon_0 R$.

B. The Parallel Plate Capacitor (Derivation)

Consists of two large plane parallel conducting plates of area $A$, separated by distance $d$. One plate has charge $+Q$ (surface density $+\sigma$), the other has $-Q$ (surface density $-\sigma$).

1. As derived using Gauss's Law, the electric field between two oppositely charged plates is uniform: $E = \frac{\sigma}{\epsilon_0} = \frac{Q}{A\epsilon_0}$.
2. The potential difference $V$ between plates separated by distance $d$ in a uniform field is $V = E \times d$.
$V = \frac{Qd}{A\epsilon_0}$.
3. By definition, $C = Q/V$.
$C = \frac{Q}{\frac{Qd}{A\epsilon_0}} \implies$ $C = \frac{\epsilon_0 A}{d}$.

Practice Problem 5 Question: Calculate the capacitance of the Earth, assuming it to be a spherical conductor of radius $6400 \text{ km}$.
Solution:
Using $C = 4\pi\epsilon_0 R$.
We know $\frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0} = 9 \times 10^9 \implies 4\pi\epsilon_0 = \frac{1}{9 \times 10^9}$.
Radius $R = 6400 \text{ km} = 6.4 \times 10^6 \text{ m}$.
$C = \frac{1}{9 \times 10^9} \times (6.4 \times 10^6) = 0.711 \times 10^{-3} \text{ Farads}$.
$\mathbf{C = 711 \, \mu F}$.
(This proves that 1 Farad is an exceptionally huge unit!)

8. Effect of Dielectric on Capacitance

[AI Image Placeholder: Landscape (16:9) - Background: #FFFFFF]
AI Prompt: "A diagram for the derivation of capacitance with a partial dielectric slab. A parallel plate capacitor with plates separated by distance d. Between the plates, there is a distinct rectangular block (a dielectric slab) of thickness 't' (where t < d) with dielectric constant 'K'. Show the remaining space as vacuum. Clearly label 'd' and 't'. White background #FFFFFF."

A. Completely Filled Dielectric

If the entire space between plates is filled with a dielectric of constant $K$, the new field becomes $E = E_0/K$. This lowers the potential to $V = V_0/K$.
Since $C = Q/V$, the new capacitance is $C = Q / (V_0/K) = K (Q/V_0) = K C_0$.
$C_m = K \frac{\epsilon_0 A}{d}$. (Capacitance increases $K$ times).

B. Partially Filled Dielectric Slab (Important Board Derivation)

Let a dielectric slab of thickness $t$ ($t < d$) be introduced between the plates.
The electric field in the vacuum region (thickness $d-t$) is $E_0 = \frac{\sigma}{\epsilon_0}$.
The electric field inside the dielectric region (thickness $t$) is $E = \frac{E_0}{K}$.
Total potential difference $V$ = Work done in moving unit charge across vacuum + Work done across dielectric.
$V = E_0(d-t) + E(t) = E_0(d-t) + \frac{E_0}{K}t = E_0 \left[ (d-t) + \frac{t}{K} \right]$.
Substitute $E_0 = \frac{Q}{A\epsilon_0}$:
$V = \frac{Q}{A\epsilon_0} \left[ d-t + \frac{t}{K} \right]$.
Since $C = Q/V$:
$$C = \frac{\epsilon_0 A}{d - t + \frac{t}{K}}$$

10. Energy Stored in a Capacitor

[AI Image Placeholder: Square (1:1) - Background: #FFFFFF]
AI Prompt: "A mathematical graph of Potential (V) on the y-axis vs Charge (Q) on the x-axis for a capacitor. The line is a straight diagonal passing through the origin (showing V is directly proportional to Q). Shade the triangular area under this curve. Label the area as 'Work Done / Energy Stored = 1/2 QV'. White background #FFFFFF."

The process of charging a capacitor involves doing work against the repulsive force of the charge already accumulated on the plates. This work is stored as Electrostatic Potential Energy.

Derivation: Suppose at any instant, a charge $q$ is on the capacitor, giving a potential $V = q/C$.
Work done to add a tiny extra charge $dq$ is $dW = V dq = \frac{q}{C} dq$.
Total work done to charge from $0$ to $Q$:
$U = W = \int_{0}^{Q} \frac{q}{C} dq = \frac{1}{C} \left[ \frac{q^2}{2} \right]_0^Q$
$$U = \frac{Q^2}{2C} = \frac{1}{2}CV^2 = \frac{1}{2}QV$$

Energy Density ($u$)

Energy stored per unit volume of the capacitor space.
$u = \frac{U}{\text{Volume}} = \frac{\frac{1}{2}CV^2}{A \times d}$. Substitute $C = \frac{\epsilon_0 A}{d}$ and $V = Ed$.
$u = \frac{1}{2} \epsilon_0 E^2$.

11. Sharing of Charges and Energy Loss

[AI Image Placeholder: Landscape (16:9) - Background: #FFFFFF]
AI Prompt: "Sharing of charges diagram. Left side: Two isolated spherical conductors, C1 charged to V1 and C2 charged to V2. Right side: The same two spheres connected by a thin conducting wire. Show electrons/charge flowing to indicate redistribution until both reach a 'Common Potential V'. Show faint heat waves radiating from the connecting wire indicating energy loss. White background #FFFFFF."

When two charged conductors are connected by a wire, charge flows from the conductor at higher potential to the one at lower potential until their potentials become equal.

Common Potential ($V$): By conservation of total charge.
$V = \frac{\text{Total Charge}}{\text{Total Capacitance}} = \frac{Q_1 + Q_2}{C_1 + C_2} = \mathbf{\frac{C_1 V_1 + C_2 V_2}{C_1 + C_2}}$

Loss of Energy: During the flow of charge, some energy is always dissipated as heat in the connecting wire. The difference between initial and final energy is positive.
$\Delta U = U_{initial} - U_{final} = \left(\frac{1}{2}C_1 V_1^2 + \frac{1}{2}C_2 V_2^2\right) - \frac{1}{2}(C_1+C_2)V^2$
After substituting $V$ and intense algebraic simplification, we get:

Energy Loss Formula $$\Delta U = \frac{1}{2} \frac{C_1 C_2}{C_1 + C_2} (V_1 - V_2)^2$$ Since $(V_1-V_2)^2$ is always positive, $\Delta U$ is always positive, proving that energy is ALWAYS lost during charge sharing.
