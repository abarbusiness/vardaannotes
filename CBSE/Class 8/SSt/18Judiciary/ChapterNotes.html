üèõÔ∏è PART 1: Role and Structure of the Judiciary

Courts interpret laws, settle disputes, and protect the Fundamental Rights of citizens. The work of the judiciary is divided into:

  • Dispute Resolution: Resolves disputes between citizens, between citizens and government, and between state/centre governments.
  • Judicial Review: The judiciary is the final interpreter of the Constitution. It can strike down laws passed by Parliament if they violate the basic structure of the Constitution.
  • Enforcing Fundamental Rights: Citizens can approach the High Court or Supreme Court if their rights are violated.
Independent Judiciary: The legislature and executive cannot interfere with the judiciary. This ensures judges are free from political pressure. Once appointed, removing a judge is extremely difficult.

Structure of Courts

India has an integrated judicial/appellate system: District Courts (lowest) ‚ûî High Courts (state level) ‚ûî Supreme Court (national level). You can appeal to a higher court if you believe the lower court's judgment is incorrect.

üö® PART 2: The Criminal Justice System

While Civil law deals with disputes over individual rights (like rent or divorce), Criminal law deals with offences against society (theft, murder). The criminal justice system has four main pillars:

AI PROMPT FOR IMAGE: A four-part split image illustrating the Criminal Justice System. Top-left: A Police officer writing an FIR. Top-right: A Public Prosecutor pointing at evidence. Bottom-left: A Defence Lawyer speaking to their client. Bottom-right: A Judge banging a gavel.

Role Responsibility in the System
1. The Police Registers an FIR (First Information Report) and investigates the crime. Collects evidence. They file a charge sheet in court but do not decide guilt. Must respect human rights (Article 22).
2. Public Prosecutor Represents the interests of the State (society). Presents the evidence and witnesses objectively against the accused in court.
3. Defence Lawyer Represents the accused person. Cross-examines witnesses and argues to prove the innocence of their client. State provides a lawyer if the accused is poor (Article 39A).
4. The Judge Acts as an impartial umpire. Listens to all sides and decides if the accused is guilty or innocent based only on the evidence presented according to the law.
Rights of the Arrested (Article 22)
An accused person has fundamental rights: to be informed of the reason for arrest, to be presented before a magistrate within 24 hours, to not be tortured, and to access a lawyer.

‚úì PART 3: What is a Fair Trial?

A fair trial is essential to uphold Article 21 (Right to Life and Liberty). It mandates that a person's life or freedom cannot be taken arbitrarily. Key elements include:

  • The trial must be held in an open court in public view.
  • It must be held in the presence of the accused, who has the right to cross-examine prosecution witnesses.
  • The judge must assume the accused is innocent until proven guilty.
  • The decision must rely strictly on the evidence presented.

üôå PART 4: Access to Justice (PIL)

In reality, the poor find it very hard to access courts due to costs and paperwork. The Supreme Court introduced Public Interest Litigation (PIL) in the 1980s. This simplified the process, allowing anyone to file a case on behalf of those whose rights are violated just by sending a letter or telegram.

üìå Chapter Summary

  • Judiciary: An independent, integrated system of courts that interprets laws, settles disputes, and enforces Fundamental Rights.
  • Criminal Justice System: Relies on the Police (investigates/files FIRs), Public Prosecutor (argues for the State), Defence Lawyer (protects the accused), and the Judge (impartial referee).
  • Fair Trial: Upholds Article 21; ensures open, evidence-based trials where guilt must be proven beyond a reasonable doubt.
  • PIL: Public Interest Litigations make justice accessible to the poor and marginalized by lowering barriers to filing a grievance.
