8.1 Deforestation and Its Causes

Deforestation: The clearing of forests and using that land for other non-forest purposes is called deforestation. It means destroying vast areas of green trees at a highly rapid rate.

Natural and Human Causes

Trees in the forest are aggressively cut for some of the following main purposes:

  • Procuring massive land for agriculture and farming to feed the growing population.
  • Building houses, roads, and incredibly massive factories.
  • Making wooden furniture or using precious wood as fuel.
  • Natural Causes: Sometimes, terrible forest fires and severe natural droughts also tragically clear out large patches of forest.

8.2 Consequences of Deforestation

Deforestation severely disrupts the fine ecological balance in nature, leading to catastrophic consequences:

  1. Increase in Temperature (Global Warming): Deforestation reduces the number of trees. Fewer trees mean that less Carbon dioxide ($CO_2$) will be used up for photosynthesis. This leaves an excessive amount of $CO_2$ in the atmosphere, which directly traps heat rays reflected by the earth, causing severe Global Warming.
  2. Droughts: Global warming dangerously disturbs the natural water cycle and deeply reduces rainfall. This directly causes severe droughts.
  3. Desertification: Trees physically hold the fertile soil in place. Without trees, the extremely fertile top layer of soil is blown away by the wind or washed away by water (Soil Erosion). The remaining lower, hard, and rocky layers have less humus and are less fertile. Gradually, the fertile land tragically gets converted into barren deserts. This is called desertification.
  4. Floods: Tree roots drastically help the soil absorb rainwater. Deforestation vastly decreases the water holding capacity of the soil. The rapid movement of water from the soil surface into the ground is heavily reduced, resulting in devastating floods.

8.3 Conservation of Forest and Wildlife

Seeing the dreadful effects of deforestation, the government fiercely laid down strict laws, policies, and rules to protect and deeply conserve our forests and animals. They created massive Protected Areas completely restricting human interference.

The Three Types of Protected Areas:

  • 1. Wildlife Sanctuary: These are heavily protected areas where animals physically live in their absolute natural habitat safely. Here, wildly capturing (poaching) or violently killing wild animals is strictly strictly prohibited by the law. (E.g., Bharatpur Bird Sanctuary)
  • 2. National Park: These are incredibly large, broad reserves tightly structured heavily to actively protect the entire ecosystem—including the native wildlife, local flora, vibrant landscapes, and any historic objects of the area. They are massive enough to protect the whole set of ecosystems. (E.g., Satpura National Park)
  • 3. Biosphere Reserve: These are the largest globally recognised enormous protected areas exclusively meant strictly for the absolute conservation of total biodiversity (plants, animals, and microorganisms). They powerfully also help fiercely deeply strictly protect the highly traditional life of the localized tribal groups peacefully living firmly within the area. (E.g., Pachmarhi Biosphere Reserve)
