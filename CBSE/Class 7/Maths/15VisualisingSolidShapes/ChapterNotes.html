2D (Plane/Flat) shapes have only length and width. Example: square, triangle, circle.
3D (Solid) shapes have length, width, AND height/depth. Example: cube, sphere, cylinder.

üì¶ 3. Nets of 3D Shapes

A net is the 2D flat layout that folds up to form a 3D solid. Multiple nets are possible for some solids.

Net of a Cube: There are 11 different nets of a cube! The classic one is a cross shape with 6 squares.

Net of a Cuboid: Has 6 rectangles in a specific arrangement.
Net of a Cylinder: 2 circles + 1 rectangle (rectangle length = circumference of circle).
Net of a Cone: 1 circle (base) + 1 sector of a bigger circle (curved surface).
Net of a Square Pyramid: 1 square + 4 triangles.

Key Test: A flat shape is a valid net if all faces have correct size and the shape folds into the 3D solid without any overlaps or gaps.

When we look at a 3D solid from different directions, we see different 2D shapes called views:

‚Ä¢ Front View: What you see from the front
‚Ä¢ Side View: What you see from the left or right side
‚Ä¢ Top View: What you see from directly above (also called "Plan")

Example ‚Äî Cube looked at from:
‚Ä¢ Front = Square | Side = Square | Top = Square

Example ‚Äî Cylinder:
‚Ä¢ Front = Rectangle | Side = Rectangle | Top = Circle

Example ‚Äî Cone:
‚Ä¢ Front = Triangle | Side = Triangle | Top = Circle

üìù Quick Revision

  1. 3D solids have Face (F), Edge (E), Vertex (V). 2D shapes are flat.
  2. Euler's Formula for polyhedra: F + V ‚àí E = 2
  3. Cube: F=6, E=12, V=8. Square Pyramid: F=5, E=8, V=5
  4. Cylinder has 2 flat + 1 curved face, 2 edges, 0 vertices
  5. Net = 2D "unfolding" that folds back into 3D solid. Cube has 11 different nets.
  6. Views: Front view / Side view / Top view give 2D cross-sections of 3D solids
  7. Sphere: no edges, no vertices. Cone: 1 vertex, 1 curved face, 1 flat face
