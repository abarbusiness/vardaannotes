ORGANIC CHEMISTRY: BASIC PRINCIPLES & TECHNIQUES

1. Introduction to Organic Chemistry

Organic chemistry is fundamentally the study of carbon compounds, especially hydrocarbons and their derivatives. Carbon is unique due to its position in the periodic table, giving it properties that allow the formation of millions of different compounds.

Concept

Catenation: The unique property of carbon atoms to form covalent bonds with other carbon atoms to form long straight chains, branched chains, and rings of various sizes.

Why Carbon is Special

  • Tetravalency: Carbon has 4 valence electrons and forms 4 covalent bonds.
  • Small Size: Allows strong overlap of orbitals, making C-C bonds highly stable.
  • Multiple Bonding: Carbon can form double (C=C) and triple (C≡C) bonds with itself and elements like O, N, and S.

2. Hybridization of Carbon

The type of bond carbon forms dictates its hybridization, which in turn determines the geometry and bond angle of the molecule.

Fact
  • sp³ Hybridization: Only single bonds. Geometry is Tetrahedral (109.5°). Example: Alkane (Methane).
  • sp² Hybridization: One double bond. Geometry is Trigonal Planar (120°). Example: Alkene (Ethene).
  • sp Hybridization: One triple bond or two double bonds. Geometry is Linear (180°). Example: Alkyne (Ethyne).

3. Representation of Organic Molecules

Organic chemists use various ways to represent complex molecular structures efficiently:

  • Complete Structural Formula: Shows all atoms and every single bond (dash).
  • Condensed Structural Formula: Omits some or all of the dashes indicating covalent bonds. E.g., CH3CH2CH3.
  • Bond-Line Structural Formula: Carbon and hydrogen atoms are not shown. Lines represent carbon-carbon bonds. Intersections and ends of lines represent carbon atoms.

4. IUPAC Nomenclature

The International Union of Pure and Applied Chemistry (IUPAC) system provides a systematic way to name any organic compound based on its structure.

The Golden Rules of Nomenclature

  1. Longest Chain Rule: Identify the longest continuous carbon chain containing the principal functional group, double/triple bonds.
  2. Lowest Number Rule: Number the chain from the end that gives the lowest possible number to the substituents or functional groups.
  3. Alphabetical Order: When two or more different substituents are present, name them in alphabetical order.
Important

Priority Order for Numbering: Principal Functional Group > Double Bond > Triple Bond > Substituents (Alkyl, Halogen, etc.).

5. Isomerism

The phenomenon where two or more compounds have the same molecular formula but different properties (due to different structural arrangements or spatial orientations).

Types of Structural Isomerism

  • Chain Isomerism: Difference in the arrangement of the carbon skeleton (straight vs. branched).
  • Position Isomerism: Difference in the position of a functional group or multiple bond on the carbon chain.
  • Functional Isomerism: Same molecular formula but different functional groups (e.g., Alcohols and Ethers).
  • Metamerism: Arises due to different alkyl chains on either side of a polyvalent functional group (e.g., in Ethers or Ketones).

6. Fundamental Concepts of Organic Reaction Mechanism

Understanding how bonds break and form is crucial for mastering organic chemistry.

Bond Fission (Cleavage)

  • Homolytic Cleavage: The covalent bond breaks symmetrically, and each atom takes one electron. This generates Free Radicals. Requires heat, light, or peroxides.
  • Heterolytic Cleavage: The bond breaks asymmetrically, and one atom takes both bonding electrons. This generates Carbocations (positive) and Carbanions (negative).
Concept

Electrophiles: Electron-loving species. They are electron-deficient and attack areas of high electron density. E.g., H⁺, CH₃⁺, AlCl₃.

Nucleophiles: Nucleus-loving species. They are electron-rich and attack electron-deficient centers. E.g., OH⁻, CN⁻, H₂O, NH₃.

7. Electronic Displacements in Covalent Bonds

Electron density in organic molecules is often shifted, making certain parts reactive.

The Inductive Effect (I-Effect)

The permanent polarization of a covalent bond due to the presence of an electron-withdrawing or electron-donating group down the carbon chain.

  • -I Effect (Electron Withdrawing): Halogens (F, Cl), -NO₂, -CN, -COOH.
  • +I Effect (Electron Donating): Alkyl groups (-CH₃, -C₂H₅). Increases stability of carbocations.

Resonance (Mesomeric) Effect

The polarity produced in a molecule by the interaction of two π-bonds or between a π-bond and a lone pair of electrons present on an adjacent atom. It greatly stabilizes the molecule.
