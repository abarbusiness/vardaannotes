Additive Manufacturing (Mid-Sem Master Guide)

Concept What is Additive Manufacturing (AM)?
Additive Manufacturing is the process of joining materials to make parts from 3D model data, usually layer upon layer, as opposed to subtractive manufacturing methodologies (like CNC machining).

1. Stereolithography (SLA) & Vat Photopolymerization

Working Principle: SLA belongs to the Vat Photopolymerization category. It uses an ultraviolet (UV) laser to cure a liquid photopolymer resin layer-by-layer into a solid 3D part.

Process & Schematic Breakdown

  • Vat: A tank filled with liquid photopolymer resin.
  • Build Platform: Submerges into the vat.
  • UV Laser & Galvanometer: A laser beam is directed by moving mirrors (galvanometers) to trace the cross-section of the part on the resin surface.
  • Mechanism: The laser energy triggers a chemical reaction, solidifying the resin. Once a layer is done, the platform lowers, a sweeper blade recoats the surface, and the process repeats.

Laser Material & The Working Curve of Resin

The Working Curve is a fundamental graph in SLA that shows the relationship between the applied laser exposure (energy) and the resulting Cure Depth of the resin.

  • Energy Distribution: The laser beam usually has a Gaussian profile (strongest in the center, tapering off at edges).
  • Cure Depth (Cd): The actual thickness of the solid layer created. To ensure layers stick together, the cure depth must be slightly greater than the actual slice thickness of the CAD model.
  • Critical Exposure (Ec): The minimum threshold of laser energy required to turn the liquid resin into a solid gel.
Important SLA Variations & Mass Production: The standard SLA uses a point-by-point scanning laser. For faster mass production or larger areas, variations like DLP (Digital Light Processing) are used, which flash the entire layer's image at once using a digital projector, dramatically reducing build time.

2. Material Jetting (MJ)

Working Principle: Similar to a standard 2D inkjet printer, but instead of ink, Material Jetting heads deposit droplets of build material (like photopolymers or wax) onto a build platform. UV lights attached to the print head cure the material instantly as it is deposited.

Jet Formation Mechanisms

The printheads operate via two main droplet generation mechanisms:

  • Continuous Inkjet (CIJ): A continuous stream of droplets is generated. Unwanted droplets are deflected electrically into a recycling gutter. Rarely used in modern AM due to material waste and complexity.
  • Drop-on-Demand (DOD): Droplets are generated only when required. This is the standard for modern Material Jetting.

DOD Controlling Mechanisms

  • Piezoelectric: A voltage applied to a piezo crystal changes its shape, creating pressure in the fluid chamber that forces a droplet out of the nozzle. (Most common in AM).
  • Thermal: A micro-resistor rapidly heats the fluid, creating a vapor bubble that expands and forces a droplet out.
Fact MJ Process Parameters affecting Droplet Size & Rate:
1. Viscosity: Must be low enough to flow through the microscopic nozzles.
2. Temperature: Used to lower the viscosity of materials (like wax) so they can be jetted.
3. Particle Size: If using suspensions, solid particles must be smaller than the nozzle to prevent clogging.
4. Surface Tension (Density): Affects how the droplet forms and breaks off.
5. Pressure Requirement: Crucial to overcome viscosity and eject the drop.

The Physics of Droplet Jetting (Fluid Dynamics)

The successful formation of a jet relies heavily on dimensionless numbers in fluid dynamics:

  • Reynolds Number (Re): Ratio of inertial forces to viscous forces. It tells us if the fluid will flow smoothly.
  • Weber Number (We): Ratio of inertial forces to surface tension forces. It determines if the droplet has enough energy to break away from the nozzle.
  • Note: The Ohnesorge number (Z), derived from Re and We, is the ultimate factor that defines whether a droplet will form cleanly without satellite drops.

3. Difference Between Material Jetting & Binder Jetting

  • Material Jetting: The printhead deposits the actual build material (like liquid plastic or wax) which is then cured via UV light.
  • Binder Jetting: The printhead deposits a liquid adhesive (binder) onto a pre-laid bed of powder (metal, sand, or ceramic). The binder glues the powder together. No UV light is used during printing.

4. Fused Deposition Modeling (FDM)

Basic Working Principle: A thermoplastic filament is pushed through a heated extrusion nozzle. It melts and is deposited layer-by-layer onto a build platform, hardening as it cools down.

Key FDM Terminology

  • Build Time: Depends directly on the nozzle speed, layer height, and the volume of the part.
  • Infill Density: FDM parts are rarely 100% solid. Infill density dictates the internal structure (e.g., 20% honeycomb pattern) to save time and material while retaining strength.
  • Volume Deposition Rate: The amount of material extruded per unit time. Affected by nozzle diameter, feed rate, and filament thickness.
Concept Common AM Defects to remember:
1. Staircase Effect: Because AM builds in discrete layers, curved or angled surfaces look like tiny steps (staircases) rather than smooth curves. Thinner layers reduce this effect.
2. Volumetric Shrinkage: As heated plastics cool (in FDM) or liquid resins chemically cure (in SLA), the material contracts. This can cause the part to warp or curl off the build plate.

5. Design for Additive Manufacturing (DfAM)

Traditional manufacturing relies on limitations (e.g., "can the drill reach this angle?"). DfAM leverages the freedom of 3D printing to create optimized, complex geometries.

Topology Optimization

This is a mathematical method that optimizes material layout within a given space, subjected to specific loads. The goal is usually to maximize strength while minimizing weight.

  • Size Optimization: Modifying the dimensions (thickness) of structural elements.
  • Shape Optimization: Modifying the outer boundary/contours of the part to relieve stress concentrations.
  • Topology Optimization: The most advanced. It can introduce new holes and completely change the internal structure. Often utilizes density-based methods, where the algorithm assigns a "density value" between 0 (void) and 1 (solid material) to elements in a design space to figure out exactly where material is needed.
