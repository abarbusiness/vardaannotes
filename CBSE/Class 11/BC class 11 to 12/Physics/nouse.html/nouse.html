Additive Manufacturing (AM)

Mid-Sem Quick Revision Notes (30 Marks)

1. Basics of Additive Manufacturing

Q: What is AM?

Additive Manufacturing (AM), commonly known as 3D printing, is the process of creating an object by building it one layer at a time from a 3D digital (CAD) model. This is the opposite of subtractive manufacturing (like milling), where you cut material away from a solid block.

Q: Factors considered for common AM processes (w.r.t SLA):

  • Layer Thickness: Thinner layers give better quality but take longer to print.
  • Part Orientation: How the part sits on the build platform affects strength, print time, and the need for support structures.
  • Material Properties: Viscosity, curing time, and melting point.
  • Support Structures: Needed for overhangs so the material doesn't collapse during the print.

2. Stereolithography (SLA)

🔬 The Process in Detail:

  • The Resin: A liquid called "Photopolymer" that is sensitive to UV light.
  • The Laser: A tiny, high-energy UV beam that acts like a "magical pen," drawing the object's shape on the liquid's surface.
  • Layering: Once a layer is hardened, the building platform moves down (or up), and a "wiper" blade ensures a fresh, flat layer of resin is ready for the next "drawing."

Fig 1: Schematic Diagram of SLA Process (Source: ResearchGate)

Q: Laser Material & Working Curve

  • Gaussian Profile: The laser isn't just a flat dot; it's strongest in the middle. Imagine a mountain shape – the peak has the most energy.
  • Working Curve: This is basically a "Recipe Book." It tells you exactly how much laser power you need to reach a certain depth. If you want a deeper cure, you need more "energy dose."
  • Critical Exposure (Ec): The minimum energy needed to start the hardening process. If you stay below this, nothing happens!

Q: SLA Variations (How we point the light)

  • Scanning (Vector): Like using a flashlight to trace a drawing. High accuracy but slow.
  • Mass Production (DLP): Like a movie projector. It shows the whole layer image at once. It's much faster because you don't have to "trace" anything!

🌟 Why SLA?

It produces the smoothest surface finish of all 3D printers. You can barely see the layer lines! It's used for jewelry, dental models, and high-detail prototypes.

3. Material Jetting (MJ)

⚙️ How it works:

  • Print Head: This is the hero. It has thousands of microscopic holes (nozzles).
  • The Material: These are liquid resins or waxes. Unlike SLA, they are stored in cartridges (like your home printer).
  • Multi-Material: A big plus! You can have one nozzle spray rigid plastic and another spray soft rubber, letting you build a "living hinge" or a two-colored part in one go.

Fig 2: Inkjet Droplet Mechanism (Source: ResearchGate)

Q: Piezoelectric vs. Thermal (The "Push")

Piezoelectric:

A crystal inside the nozzle "vibrates" when it gets electricity, physically pushing the drop out. It's very precise and works with many types of fluids.

Thermal:

Uses heat to create a tiny steam bubble. The bubble expands and shoots the drop out like a cannonball. Mostly used for water-based materials.

Q: The Physics of the "Perfect Drop" (PI concept)

Droplet formation depends on the Printability Indicator (PI). Think of it like a "Goldilocks" number:

  • If PI < 1: The liquid is too thick (viscous). No drop will form.
  • If PI > 10: The liquid is too thin (watery). It will splash into tiny satellite drops, making a mess.
  • Stable Jet: When 1 < PI < 10, you get perfect, consistent droplets.

🌟 Why Material Jetting?

It's incredibly precise and colorful. You can print in full color and different textures. However, the machines and materials are very expensive!

4. MJ vs. Binder Jetting

Q: What is the difference?

  • Material Jetting: The printhead sprays the actual building material (liquid plastic/wax) which then hardens.
  • Binder Jetting: The printhead sprays a liquid glue (binder) onto a pre-laid bed of dry powder. The glue sticks the powder together.

5. Fused Deposition Modeling (FDM)

⚙️ How it works:

  • The Filament: Solid plastic on a spool. Usually PLA or ABS.
  • The Extruder: A motor pulls the filament into a heated block.
  • The Nozzle: The plastic is squeezed through a tiny hole (like a cake decorator).
  • Solidification: A fan blows air to cool the molten plastic instantly so it stays in place.

Fig 3: Schematic Diagram of FDM Process (Source: ResearchGate)

Q: Build Time & Infill (Speed vs Strength)

  • Infill Density: 0% means it's hollow. 100% means it's solid. Usually, we use 15-20% with a "Honeycomb" or "Grid" pattern to save time and material.
  • Volume Deposition Rate: This is the "Speed Control." If you print too fast, the plastic might not have time to cool, leading to a messy, "slumped" object.

Q: Common Defects (Things that go wrong)

  • Staircase Effect: On curved surfaces, you see the steps of each layer. Finer (thinner) layers make this less visible.
  • Warping: Because plastic shrinks when it cools, the corners of the part might "lift up" from the build plate. Using a heated bed helps prevent this.

🌟 Why FDM?

It's the cheapest and most common type of 3D printing. The machines are easy to use and the materials are strong. Most "hobbyist" 3D printers you see are FDM!

6. Design for Additive Manufacturing (DfAM)

Q: What is Topology Optimization?

A software technique that analyzes the stress on a part and automatically removes any material that isn't doing any "work." The goal is to make the part as lightweight as possible while keeping it perfectly strong (like bird bones).

  • Three Levels: We can optimize the Size (how big), Shape (the boundary), or Topology (where the holes go).
  • The Density Method (SIMP): This is the most popular way. The software divides the part into tiny cubes (voxels). Each voxel is given a "density" value between 0 and 1.
    • Density 1: Keep it (High Stress).
    • Density 0: Delete it (No Stress).
