Thermodynamics: Second Law & Heat Engines

1. The Second Law of Thermodynamics

While the First Law of Thermodynamics establishes the conservation of energy, it fails to specify the direction in which a thermodynamic process can naturally occur. The Second Law imposes restrictions on the direction of heat transfer and limits the efficiency of heat engines.

1.1 Kelvin-Planck Statement

Concept

Kelvin-Planck Statement: It is impossible to construct an engine which, operating in a cycle, will produce no other effect than the extraction of heat from a single reservoir and the performance of an equivalent amount of work.

  • This means a heat engine cannot have 100% efficiency.
  • Some heat must always be rejected to a lower-temperature body (sink).

1.2 Clausius Statement

Concept

Clausius Statement: It is impossible for a self-acting machine, unaided by any external agency, to transfer heat from a body at a lower temperature to another at a higher temperature.

  • This implies that heat cannot flow naturally from a cold body to a hot body without external work being done (which is the principle of a refrigerator).
  • Note: Both statements are entirely equivalent. A violation of the Kelvin-Planck statement leads to a violation of the Clausius statement, and vice versa.

2. Heat Engine

A heat engine is a device that converts heat energy into mechanical work in a cyclic process.

2.1 Main Components

  • Source (Hot Reservoir): Maintained at a higher temperature (T1). It provides heat energy (Q1) to the working substance.
  • Working Substance: The material (like steam or fuel-air mixture) that performs mechanical work (W).
  • Sink (Cold Reservoir): Maintained at a lower temperature (T2). It absorbs the rejected heat (Q2).
Fact

Work Done: W = Q1 - Q2

Efficiency (η): Defined as the ratio of net work done to the heat absorbed from the source.

Formula: η = W / Q1 = (Q1 - Q2) / Q1 = 1 - (Q2 / Q1)

For a Carnot (Ideal) Engine: η = 1 - (T2 / T1)    (where temperatures must be in Kelvin)

3. Refrigerator and Heat Pump

A refrigerator operates exactly in the reverse direction of a heat engine. It extracts heat (Q2) from a cold reservoir (T2), external work (W) is done on it, and it rejects heat (Q1) to a hot reservoir (T1).

Fact

Coefficient of Performance (COP, designated as α or β):

It is the ratio of heat extracted from the cold reservoir to the external work done.

Formula: α = Q2 / W = Q2 / (Q1 - Q2)

For an Ideal Refrigerator: α = T2 / (T1 - T2)

Important

Relation between η and α: α = (1 - η) / η

4. Reversible and Irreversible Processes

4.1 Reversible Process

A process which can be retraced in the opposite direction such that both the system and the surroundings return to their original states, leaving no net change in the universe.

  • Conditions: Must be extremely slow (quasi-static), and there must be no dissipative forces (friction, viscosity, electrical resistance).
  • Example: Very slow isothermal expansion/compression of an ideal gas. (Theoretical ideal only).

4.2 Irreversible Process

A process that cannot be retraced back precisely. Almost all natural processes are irreversible.

  • Causes: Spontaneous occurrence, presence of dissipative forces.
  • Example: Rusting of iron, diffusion of gases, heat transfer through a finite temperature difference.

5. Concept of Entropy (S)

Entropy is a thermodynamic state variable that measures the degree of randomness or disorder in a system.

Concept

If a small amount of heat dQ is added to a system reversibly at absolute temperature T, the change in entropy is defined as:
dS = dQ / T

SI Unit: J/K (Joules per Kelvin)

Important

Second Law in terms of Entropy: In any spontaneous (irreversible) process, the total entropy of the universe (system + surroundings) always increases. In a reversible process, the total entropy remains constant.
(ΔSuniverse ≥ 0)

6. Practice Numerical Problems (JEE Main & Class 11 Boards Level)

These 16 hand-picked numericals cover various concepts across Thermodynamics to solidify your preparation.

Q1. A Carnot engine operates between temperatures 500 K and 300 K. What is its efficiency?

Solution:
Given: T1 = 500 K, T2 = 300 K.
η = 1 - (T2 / T1) = 1 - (300 / 500) = 1 - 0.6 = 0.4.
Efficiency = 40%.

Q2. A thermodynamic system goes from state A to B, absorbing 100 J of heat and doing 40 J of work. Find the change in its internal energy.

Solution:
By First Law: ΔQ = ΔU + ΔW
100 = ΔU + 40 ⇒ ΔU = 100 - 40 = 60 J.

Q3. A refrigerator has a Coefficient of Performance of 5. If it extracts 250 J of heat per cycle from the cold reservoir, calculate the work done per cycle.

Solution:
COP (α) = Q2 / W
5 = 250 / W ⇒ W = 250 / 5 = 50 J.

Q4. Calculate the work done by 2 moles of an ideal gas during isothermal expansion from 10 L to 20 L at 300 K. (R = 8.31 J/mol·K, ln 2 = 0.693).

Solution:
W = nRT ln(V2 / V1)
W = 2 × 8.31 × 300 × ln(20/10) = 4986 × 0.693 ≈ 3455 J.

Q5. An ideal gas (γ = 1.5) is adiabatically compressed from volume V to V/4. If the initial temperature is 300 K, what is the final temperature?

Solution:
For adiabatic process: T1V1γ-1 = T2V2γ-1
300 × (V)0.5 = T2 × (V/4)0.5
T2 = 300 × (4)0.5 = 300 × 2 = 600 K.

Q6. A Carnot engine whose sink is at 300 K has an efficiency of 40%. By how much should the temperature of the source be increased to increase the efficiency to 50%?

Solution:
Case 1: η = 0.4 = 1 - (300 / T1) ⇒ 300 / T1 = 0.6 ⇒ T1 = 500 K.
Case 2: η' = 0.5 = 1 - (300 / T1') ⇒ 300 / T1' = 0.5 ⇒ T1' = 600 K.
Increase in source temperature = 600 - 500 = 100 K.

Q7. Calculate the change in entropy when 1 kg of ice at 0°C melts into water at 0°C. (Latent heat of fusion of ice = 3.36 × 105 J/kg).

Solution:
Heat absorbed, Q = mL = 1 × 3.36 × 105 J.
Temperature, T = 273 K.
ΔS = Q / T = (3.36 × 105) / 273 ≈ 1230.7 J/K.

Q8. In a cyclic process shown on a P-V diagram, the area enclosed by the cycle is 500 J. If heat absorbed by the system during the cycle is 1200 J, find the heat rejected by the system.

Solution:
For a cyclic process, ΔU = 0. By First Law, net Q = net W.
Qin - Qout = Area of P-V loop
1200 - Qout = 500 ⇒ Qout = 700 J.

Q9. A refrigerator maintains a freezer at -13°C when room temperature is 27°C. If heat leaks into the freezer at 200 J/s, what minimum power is required by the motor to maintain this temperature?

Solution:
T2 = 260 K, T1 = 300 K.
COP (α) = T2 / (T1 - T2) = 260 / 40 = 6.5.
α = Q2 / W ⇒ 6.5 = 200 / Power ⇒ Power = 200 / 6.5 ≈ 30.77 W.

Q10. An ideal diatomic gas is heated at constant pressure. What fraction of the heat supplied is used in doing external work?

Solution:
For a diatomic gas, Cp = 7R/2, Cv = 5R/2.
Total heat Q = nCpΔT. Work done W = nRΔT.
Fraction W/Q = (nRΔT) / (n(7R/2)ΔT) = 2/7. Answer: 2/7.

Q11. Two Carnot engines A and B are operated in series. Engine A receives heat from a reservoir at 600 K and rejects to a reservoir at T. Engine B receives the heat rejected by A and rejects to a sink at 300 K. If the work outputs of both are equal, find T.

Solution:
WA = WB implies Q1 - Q2 = Q2 - Q3.
For Carnot, Q is proportional to T.
T1 - T = T - T2 ⇒ 2T = T1 + T2
T = (600 + 300) / 2 = 450 K.

Q12. A gas is suddenly compressed to 1/8th of its original volume. If γ = 5/3, calculate the ratio of final pressure to initial pressure.

Solution:
Sudden compression means adiabatic. P1V1γ = P2V2γ.
P2 / P1 = (V1 / V2)γ = (8)5/3 = (23)5/3 = 25 = 32.

Q13. Find the change in internal energy when 1 g of water at 100°C is converted to steam at 100°C at 1 atm. (Lv = 2256 J/g, 1 atm = 1.013×105 Pa, Vsteam = 1671 cm3/g, Vwater = 1 cm3/g).

Solution:
Q = mL = 1 × 2256 = 2256 J.
ΔV = 1671 - 1 = 1670 cm3 = 1.67 × 10-3 m3.
W = PΔV = 1.013 × 105 × 1.67 × 10-3 ≈ 169 J.
ΔU = Q - W = 2256 - 169 = 2087 J.

Q14. A Carnot heat engine absorbs 6000 J of heat from a source at 800 K and rejects heat to a sink at 400 K. What is the amount of heat rejected?

Solution:
Q1 / T1 = Q2 / T2
6000 / 800 = Q2 / 400 ⇒ Q2 = (6000 × 400) / 800 = 3000 J.

Q15. 1 mole of monoatomic gas (γ = 5/3) is mixed with 1 mole of diatomic gas (γ = 7/5). What is the equivalent γ for the mixture?

Solution:
Cv(mix) = (n1Cv1 + n2Cv2) / (n1 + n2)
Cv(mix) = (1×3R/2 + 1×5R/2) / 2 = 8R/4 = 2R.
Cp(mix) = Cv(mix) + R = 3R.
γmix = Cp(mix) / Cv(mix) = 3R / 2R = 1.5.

Q16. An engine takes 100 J of heat from a hot reservoir and delivers 20 J of work. If it is operated as a refrigerator between the same reservoirs, what would be its COP?

Solution:
Engine Efficiency, η = W / Q1 = 20 / 100 = 0.2.
Since it operates between the same reservoirs, using the relation α = (1 - η) / η.
α = (1 - 0.2) / 0.2 = 0.8 / 0.2 = 4.
