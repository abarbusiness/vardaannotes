1. Structure: sp Hybridization of Alkynes

General formula: CₙH₂ₙ₋₂ (one triple bond)

Triple bond = 1 σ + 2 π bonds

sp hybridization: Each triple-bond carbon forms 2 sp hybrid orbitals (180° apart → linear). Two unhybridized p orbitals per carbon remain (perpendicular to each other and to the σ-bond axis) → these overlap sideways to form TWO π bonds.

Bond angle: 180° (linear). C≡C bond length: 1.20 Å (shorter than C=C 1.34 Å or C–C 1.54 Å). C≡C bond strength: 839 kJ/mol (strongest C–C bond type).

2. Acidic Nature of Terminal Alkynes

Terminal alkynes (R–C≡C–H) are weakly acidic — the ≡C–H hydrogen can be removed by strong bases.

Why? The sp carbon is the most electronegative type of carbon (50% s-character → holds electrons close to nucleus). The C–H bond is more polarized (Cδ⁺–Hδ⁺) → H is more "acidic".

Acidity order: HC≡CH (pKa ~25) > H₂C=CH₂ (pKa ~44) > CH₄ (pKa ~50)

Key reactions showing acidic nature:

  • HC≡CH + NaNH₂ → HC≡C⁻Na⁺ + NH₃ (sodium amide is a strong enough base)
  • HC≡CH + Na → HC≡C⁻Na⁺ + ½H₂ (sodium metal reacts with terminal alkyne)
  • HC≡CH + Ag(NH₃)₂⁺NO₃⁻ → AgC≡CAg↓ (silver acetylide — white ppt — test for terminal alkyne)
  • HC≡CH + Cu(NH₃)₂⁺Cl⁻ → CuC≡CCu↓ (copper acetylide — red/brown ppt — another test)
These acetylide salts (AgC≡CAg, CuC≡CCu) are shock-sensitive and can EXPLODE when dry — keep wet in lab!

3. Preparation of Alkynes

3.1 From Calcium Carbide (Industrial method for Ethyne)

CaC₂ + H₂O → HC≡CH (ethyne / acetylene) + Ca(OH)₂

Calcium carbide is made by: CaO + 3C →(electric furnace, 2000°C)→ CaC₂ + CO

The carbide ion C₂²⁻ reacts with water to give acetylene. This is the main industrial source of ethyne.

3.2 Dehydrohalogenation of Vicinal Dihalides

Two moles of KOH/alcohol required:

R–CHBr–CHBr–R' + 2KOH (alc.) → R–C≡C–R' + 2KBr + 2H₂O

Mechanism: First elimination gives vinyl halide (R–CBr=CH–R'). Second KOH/alc. removes another HBr from vinyl halide → alkyne. (Vinyl halides are much less reactive in E2 — need strong base and higher temperature.)

3.3 From Gem-Dihalides (same C)

R–CHBr₂ + 2KOH (alc.) → R–C≡CH + 2KBr + 2H₂O (two HBr removed from same C and adjacent C)

Gem-dihalides have both halogens on same carbon.

4. Chemical Reactions

4.1 Addition of H₂ — Controlled Reduction to Cis or Trans Alkene

The remarkable feature of alkynes: you can stop reduction at the alkene stage (do NOT reduce all the way to alkane) using specific catalysts.

Full reduction → Alkane: R–C≡C–R' + 2H₂ →(Ni/Pt, heat)→ R–CH₂–CH₂–R'

Lindlar's Catalyst → cis-Alkene (Z):

R–C≡C–R' + H₂ →(Lindlar's catalyst: Pd/CaCO₃/quinoline)→ cis-R–CH=CH–R' (Z-alkene)

Both H atoms delivered from the same face of the Pd surface → syn addition → cis product.

Birch Reduction (Na/NH₃) → trans-Alkene (E):

R–C≡C–R' + 2Na + 2NH₃(liq.)→ trans-R–CH=CH–R' (E-alkene) + 2NaNH₂

Mechanism: electron addition to alkyne → vinyl radical → vinyl anion → protonation. Anti addition → trans product (bulky groups go to same side for less steric interaction in the vinyl anion step).

4.2 Addition of HX — Markovnikov (Twice)

Step 1: R–C≡CH + HX → R–C(X)=CH₂ (haloalkene — Markovnikov addition)

Step 2 (1 more HX): R–C(X)=CH₂ + HX → R–CX₂–CH₃ (gem-dihalide — Markovnikov again)

Both halogen atoms end up on the SAME carbon (gem-dihalide). This is called gem-addition.

Example: HC≡CH + 2HCl → CH₃–CHCl₂ (1,1-dichloroethane)

4.3 Addition of X₂ (Halogenation)

R–C≡C–R' + Br₂ → R–CBr=CBr–R' (trans adduct, 1,2-dibromo alkene) [first addition]

+ Br₂ again → R–CBr₂–CBr₂–R' (1,1,2,2-tetrabromoalkane)

Alkynes also decolorise bromine water (like alkenes) → test for unsaturation.

4.4 Hydration — Markovnikov → Ketone (or Aldehyde for Ethyne)

Mercuric Sulphate Catalyzed Hydration

Condition: H₂O + H₂SO₄ + HgSO₄ catalyst (or Wacker process with PdCl₂/CuCl₂)

R–C≡CH + H₂O →(H₂SO₄, HgSO₄)→ [R–C(OH)=CH₂] (enol) → tautomerises → R–CO–CH₃ (ketone)

The enol form (vinyl alcohol) is unstable → immediately tautomerises to the more stable keto form.

Markovnikov applies: OH adds to the more substituted C → ketone (not aldehyde, except for acetylene).

Exception — Acetylene: HC≡CH + H₂O →(H₂SO₄, HgSO₄)→ [CH₂=CHOH] (vinyl alcohol) → CH₃CHO (acetaldehyde, ethanal — an aldehyde not a ketone, because the molecule is symmetric)

4.5 Polymerization of Acetylene

Linear trimerization: 3 HC≡CH →(600°C, charcoal)→ C₆H₆ (benzene!) — Berthelot reaction

Polymerization of vinyl acetylene: 2 HC≡CH → CH₂=CH–C≡CH (vinylacetylene) → further addition of HCl → gives neoprene (chloroprene polymer) — a synthetic rubber.

4.6 Ozonolysis of Alkynes

R–C≡C–R' + O₃ → 2 RCOOH (two carboxylic acids by full oxidation)

HC≡CH → 2 CO₂ + H₂O (acetylene gives CO₂)

R–C≡CH → RCOOH + CO₂ (terminal alkyne gives acid + CO₂)

Useful for identifying the structure of unknown alkynes (same backward-reasoning as alkene ozonolysis).

H Q6. Explain why gem-dihalides (not vicinal dihalides) are the product of double HX addition to alkynes, using Markovnikov's rule for each step.

  1. Saying Lindlar's catalyst gives trans-alkene — WRONG! Lindlar's (Pd/CaCO₃/quinoline) gives CIS (Z) alkene by syn-H₂ addition. Birch (Na/NH₃) gives TRANS (E) alkene.
  2. Hydration of a terminal alkyne (other than ethyne) giving an aldehyde — WRONG! Markovnikov → OH to more substituted C → ketone. Only acetylene (HC≡CH) gives aldehyde (acetaldehyde) on hydration.
  3. Forgetting terminal alkynes react with AgNO₃/NH₃ but internal alkynes do NOT — only the terminal ≡C–H is acidic enough.
  4. Saying ozonolysis of alkynes gives aldehydes and ketones — WRONG! Alkynes give CARBOXYLIC ACIDS (or CO₂) on ozonolysis, not aldehydes/ketones (which are alkene ozonolysis products).
