9.1 Classification of Hydrocarbons

Definition

Hydrocarbons are compounds containing only carbon and hydrogen. They are the most important sources of energy (LPG, CNG, petrol, diesel, kerosene) and are used in manufacture of polymers, dyes, drugs and solvents.

LPG = Liquefied Petroleum Gas  ·  CNG = Compressed Natural Gas  ·  LNG = Liquefied Natural Gas

9.2 Alkanes

Definition

Alkanes are saturated open-chain hydrocarbons with only C–C and C–H single bonds. General formula: CnH2n+2. Earlier called paraffins (Latin: parum = little, affinis = affinity) because they are largely inert.

First member: Methane (CH4) — found in coal mines and marshy places.

9.2.1 Nomenclature and Isomerism

Methane, ethane, and propane each have only one possible structure. From butane onwards, multiple structural isomers exist.

9.2.3 Properties of Alkanes

Physical Properties

  • Almost non-polar (very small electronegativity difference between C and H); possess weak van der Waals forces.
  • State at 298 K: C1–C4 = gases; C5–C17 = liquids; C18+ = solids.
  • Boiling point: increases steadily with molecular mass (more surface area → stronger van der Waals forces). Branched chain isomers have lower bp than straight chain (spherical shape → smaller contact area).
  • Colourless and odourless; insoluble in water; like dissolves like (polar in polar, non-polar in non-polar).

Free Radical Chain Mechanism of Chlorination (Detailed)

Mechanism

Three stages — Initiation → Propagation → Termination

Initiation:
Cl–Cl —(hν)→ 2Cl•    (homolysis; Cl–Cl bond weakest)

Propagation:
(a) CH4 + Cl• → •CH3 + HCl
(b) •CH3 + Cl–Cl → CH3Cl + Cl•
(Steps a and b repeat — chain reaction)

Termination:
(a) Cl• + Cl• → Cl2
(b) •CH3 + •CH3 → CH3–CH3  (explains ethane byproduct!)
(c) •CH3 + Cl• → CH3Cl
Why Ethane is a Byproduct?

During termination, two methyl free radicals can combine to form ethane (reaction b above). This is confirmed by the free radical chain mechanism.

9.2.4 Conformations of Ethane

Key Definitions

Conformations (Conformers / Rotamers): Different spatial arrangements of atoms in a molecule that can be interconverted by rotation around a C–C single bond. Alkanes can have infinite conformations, but rotation is NOT completely free — hindered by torsional strain (1–20 kJ/mol).

Torsional strain: Weak repulsive interaction between electron clouds of adjacent C–H bonds that resist rotation.

Dihedral angle (torsional angle): The angle of rotation about the C–C bond.

Eclipsed Conformation

H atoms on adjacent carbons are as close together as possible (0° dihedral angle). Maximum electron cloud repulsion → maximum torsional strain → least stable.

Staggered Conformation

H atoms are as far apart as possible (60° dihedral angle). Minimum repulsion → minimum torsional strain → most stable. Preferred conformation.

Any intermediate conformation = Skew conformation. Energy difference between eclipsed and staggered = 12.5 kJ/mol (small enough that free rotation occurs at room temperature).

Representation Methods

Sawhorse Projection

View along C–C axis. C–C drawn as a long diagonal line. Front carbon at lower end, rear carbon at upper end. Each C has 3 lines for H at 120°.

Newman Projection

View at C–C bond head-on. Front carbon = central point; rear carbon = circle. Each has 3 H-bonds at 120°. Eclipsed: H overlaps; Staggered: H at 60° to each other.

9.3 Alkenes

Definition

Alkenes are unsaturated hydrocarbons containing at least one C=C double bond. General formula: CnH2n. Also called olefins (oil-forming) because ethylene forms an oily liquid with Cl2. First stable member: Ethene (C2H4).

9.3.1 Structure of the Double Bond

  • C=C consists of: one σ bond (sp² head-on overlap, enthalpy ≈ 397 kJ/mol) + one π bond (lateral overlap of 2p orbitals, enthalpy ≈ 284 kJ/mol)
  • C=C bond length = 134 pm (shorter than C–C single bond 154 pm)
  • Total C=C bond enthalpy = 681 kJ/mol > C–C single bond (348 kJ/mol)
  • π bond is weaker → alkenes are electron-rich sources (easily attacked by electrophiles)

Geometrical (cis-trans) Isomerism

Definition

Occurs when rotation about C=C is restricted. If each doubly bonded carbon has two different groups, two spatial arrangements are possible:

  • cis: identical groups on the SAME side of the double bond
  • trans: identical groups on OPPOSITE sides

Condition for cis-trans isomerism: Both doubly bonded carbons must each carry two DIFFERENT substituents. If either carbon carries two identical groups → no cis-trans isomerism.

cis-But-2-ene
μ = 0.33 D · bp 277 K
More polar · Higher mp (solids)
⇌
trans-But-2-ene
μ ≈ 0 D · bp 274 K
Non-polar · Lower bp · Higher mp
cis vs trans — Key Differences
  • Polarity: cis is more polar (bond dipoles don't cancel); trans ≈ non-polar (dipoles cancel)
  • Boiling point: cis > trans (higher polarity → stronger intermolecular forces)
  • Melting point of solids: trans > cis (more symmetric → better packing in crystal)
  • Dipole moment: cis-but-2-ene = 0.33 D; trans-but-2-ene ≈ 0 D

Markovnikov Rule

KEY CONCEPT

Markovnikov's Rule (1869): In the addition of an unsymmetrical reagent (H–X) to an unsymmetrical alkene, the negative part of the addendum (X⁻) attaches to the carbon atom that has the lesser number of hydrogen atoms.

Mechanistic explanation: H⁺ attacks the C=C to form two possible carbocations. The more stable carbocation (2° > 1°) forms faster and predominates → product determined by stability of intermediate carbocation.

CH3–CH=CH2 + HBr →
  H⁺ adds to C3 (more H): forms CH3–CH⁺–CH3 (2° carbocation — more stable)
  Br⁻ attacks C2: → CH3–CHBr–CH3 (2-Bromopropane — MAJOR product ✓)
  H⁺ adds to C2: forms CH3–CH2–CH2⁺ (1° carbocation — less stable)
  Br⁻ attacks C3: → CH3–CH2–CH2Br (1-Bromopropane — MINOR product)

Anti-Markovnikov Addition (Peroxide / Kharash Effect)

Peroxide Effect

In the presence of peroxide, HBr adds to unsymmetrical alkenes in anti-Markovnikov fashion (Br goes to the carbon with MORE H). Observed ONLY with HBr (not HCl or HI). Free radical mechanism (not ionic).

CH3–CH=CH2 + HBr —(peroxide)→ CH3–CH2–CH2Br (1-Bromopropane — anti-Markovnikov)

Why not HCl? H–Cl bond (430.5 kJ/mol) too strong for free radical cleavage.
Why not HI? I• free radicals combine to form I2 instead of adding to double bond (H–I bond = 296.8 kJ/mol, too weak).
HBr (363.7 kJ/mol) = just right.

Ozonolysis — Finding Position of Double Bond

R–CH=CH–R' + O3 → Ozonide —(Zn/H2O)→ R–CHO + R'–CHO (aldehydes/ketones)

Example: Propene + O3 → Propene ozonide → CH3CHO (Ethanal) + HCHO (Methanal)
2-Methylpropene + O3 → (CH3)2C=O (Propan-2-one) + HCHO (Methanal)
Application of Ozonolysis

If ozonolysis gives two specific aldehydes/ketones, we can work backwards to find the original alkene structure. This is the primary use of ozonolysis.

9.4 Alkynes

Definition

Alkynes are unsaturated hydrocarbons with at least one C≡C triple bond. General formula: CnH2n–2. First stable member: Ethyne (C2H2) — commonly called acetylene. Used for oxyacetylene welding.

9.4.2 Structure of the Triple Bond (Ethyne)

  • Each C in ethyne: sp hybridised (2 sp orbitals + 2 unhybridised p orbitals)
  • C≡C: one σ (sp–sp head-on overlap) + two π bonds (lateral overlap of 2px and 2py orbitals)
  • C–H bonds: sp–1s overlap
  • H–C–C bond angle: 180° (linear molecule)
  • C≡C bond length: 120 pm (shorter than C=C 134 pm and C–C 154 pm)
  • C≡C bond enthalpy: 823 kJ/mol > C=C (681) > C–C (348)
  • Electron cloud is cylindrically symmetrical about the internuclear axis

9.4.4 Properties of Alkynes

First 3 = gases; next 8 = liquids; higher = solids. All colourless; ethyne has characteristic odour. Weakly polar. Lighter than water; immiscible with water; soluble in organic solvents.

A. Acidic Character

KEY CONCEPT

Terminal alkynes (H attached to triply bonded C) are acidic — they react with strong bases like Na metal and NaNH2 to liberate H2.

Reason: sp hybridised C (50% s character) is most electronegative → attracts C–H shared electrons more strongly → H is released as H⁺ more easily than in alkenes (sp²) or alkanes (sp³).

HC≡CH + Na → HC≡C⁻Na⁺ + ½H2    (Monosodium ethynide)
HC≡C⁻Na⁺ + Na → Na⁺C≡C⁻Na⁺ + ½H2    (Disodium ethynide)
CH3–C≡C–H + NaNH2 → CH3–C≡C⁻Na⁺ + NH3    (Sodium propynide)

Note: But-2-yne (internal alkyne, CH3–C≡C–CH3) is NOT acidic — no H on triply bonded C!

9.5.2 Structure of Benzene

Historical Facts
  • Benzene isolated by Michael Faraday (1825)
  • Molecular formula C6H6 — indicates high degree of unsaturation
  • Benzene forms a triozonide → 3 double bonds present
  • Benzene gives only one monosubstituted product → all 6 C and H atoms are identical
  • Kekulé (1865): proposed cyclic structure with alternating single and double bonds
  • Problem with Kekulé: predicts 2 ortho-disubstituted isomers, but only ONE is observed → solved by "oscillating double bonds"
  • Modern explanation: resonance (delocalisation)

Electronic Structure of Benzene

  • All 6 C atoms are sp² hybridised
  • Each C forms σ bonds with 2 adjacent C atoms and 1 H atom → hexagonal planar framework
  • Each C has one unhybridised 2p orbital perpendicular to the ring plane
  • Equal probability of p orbitals of each C overlapping with neighbours → 6 π electrons completely delocalised over all 6 C atoms
  • Represented as: two doughnut-shaped electron clouds above and below the ring

KEY CONCEPT

Why benzene prefers substitution over addition:

Benzene's delocalised π electrons are attracted more strongly by the 6 carbon nuclei than localised electrons between 2 carbons. This extra stability (resonance energy) means benzene resists addition reactions (which would destroy delocalisation) and instead undergoes electrophilic substitution (which retains the aromatic ring).

X-Ray data: all C–C = 139 pm (intermediate between single 154 and double 134). No pure double bonds → explains resistance to addition.

9.5.3 Aromaticity

KEY CONCEPT

Hückel's Rule of Aromaticity

A compound is aromatic if it satisfies ALL THREE conditions:

  1. Planarity: the molecule is flat (planar)
  2. Complete delocalisation of π electrons in the ring
  3. (4n + 2) π electrons in the ring where n = 0, 1, 2, 3, … (Hückel Rule)

9.5.5 Electrophilic Substitution Reactions of Benzene

Definition

Arenes undergo electrophilic substitution (SE) reactions in which an electrophile (E⁺) replaces a hydrogen atom on the ring, retaining aromaticity. Three steps: (a) Generation of E⁺, (b) Formation of σ-complex (arenium ion), (c) Removal of H⁺ to restore aromaticity.

ReactionReagents & ConditionsElectrophile GeneratedProductEq. No.
1. Nitration Conc. HNO3 + Conc. H2SO4 (nitrating mixture), 323–333 K NO2⁺ (Nitronium ion) Nitrobenzene (C6H5NO2) 9.72
2. Halogenation Cl2 or Br2, anhydrous FeCl3/FeBr3/AlCl3 (Lewis acid) Cl⁺ or Br⁺ Chlorobenzene / Bromobenzene 9.73
3. Sulphonation Fuming H2SO4 (oleum), heat SO3 (electrophilic) Benzene sulphonic acid (C6H5SO3H) 9.74
4. Friedel-Crafts Alkylation Alkyl halide (R–X) + anhydrous AlCl3 R⁺ (carbocation) Alkylbenzene (e.g. Toluene with CH3Cl) 9.75–9.76
5. Friedel-Crafts Acylation Acyl halide (R–COX) or acid anhydride + AlCl3 RCO⁺ (Acylium ion) Acylbenzene (e.g. Acetophenone with CH3COCl) 9.77–9.78

Mechanism of Electrophilic Substitution (SE) — Detailed

(a) Generation of Electrophile E⁺:
For nitration: HNO3 + H2SO4 → H2NO3⁺ + HSO4⁻ → H2O + ⁺NO2 (Nitronium ion)
For halogenation: Cl–Cl + AlCl3 → Cl⁺ + [AlCl4]⁻
For F-C alkylation: CH3Cl + AlCl3 → CH3⁺ + [AlCl4]⁻

(b) Formation of σ-complex (Arenium ion):
Benzene + E⁺ → Arenium ion (σ-complex) — one C becomes sp³, aromaticity LOST; ion stabilised by resonance over remaining π system.

(c) Removal of H⁺ (Restoration of Aromaticity):
[AlCl4]⁻ or [HSO4]⁻ removes H⁺ from sp³ carbon → aromaticity restored → substituted benzene + HCl/H2SO4 + AlCl3 (Lewis acid regenerated)

Addition Reactions (Under Special Conditions)

Hydrogenation: Benzene + 3H2 —(Ni, Δ, high P)→ Cyclohexane 9.80
Chlorination: Benzene + 3Cl2 —(UV, 500 K)→ BHC (Benzene Hexachloride, C6H6Cl6, Gammaxane) 9.81

9.5.6 Directive Influence of Substituents in Monosubstituted Benzene

Definition

When monosubstituted benzene undergoes further substitution, the incoming group is directed to specific positions (ortho, meta or para) based on the nature of the substituent already present — not on the nature of the incoming group.

Ortho & Para Directing Groups (Activating)

Mechanism: These groups donate electrons into the ring via +R or +I effect → electron density increases at o- and p-positions → electrophile attacks o- and p-positions.

Examples: –OH, –NH2, –NHR, –NR2, –NHCOCH3, –OCH3, –CH3, –C2H5

Halogens are o- and p-directing but moderately deactivating (strong –I but +R effect increases density at o/p)

These groups make benzene ring MORE reactive toward electrophilic substitution.

Meta Directing Groups (Deactivating)

Mechanism: These groups withdraw electrons from ring via –R or –I effect → electron density decreases more at o- and p-positions than at m-position → electrophile attacks the relatively electron-rich meta position.

Examples: –NO2, –CN, –CHO, –COR, –COOH, –COOR, –SO3H

These groups make benzene ring LESS reactive toward electrophilic substitution.

📝 Examples of Directive Effects

Phenol (–OH group): Resonance structures show high electron density at C-2, C-4, C-6 (ortho and para positions) → attack at o- and p-positions → o-nitrophenol + p-nitrophenol (major products)

Nitrobenzene (–NO2 group): –NO2 withdraws electrons strongly (–I and –R) → electron density decreases at o- and p-positions more than m → electrophile attacks m-position → m-dinitrobenzene (major)

Toluene (–CH3 group): +I effect of alkyl group + hyperconjugation → increased electron density at o- and p-positions → o-nitrotoluene + p-nitrotoluene (major)

9.6 Carcinogenicity and Toxicity

Important

Benzene and polynuclear hydrocarbons (containing more than two fused benzene rings) are toxic and possess carcinogenic (cancer-causing) properties.

They are formed during incomplete combustion of organic materials like tobacco, coal and petroleum. They enter the human body, undergo biochemical reactions, damage DNA and cause cancer.

✏️ Practice Questions

Q1
How do you account for the formation of ethane as a by-product during chlorination of methane?
During the termination step of free radical chain mechanism, two methyl free radicals (•CH₃) combine to form ethane: •CH₃ + •CH₃ → CH₃–CH₃. This is one of the chain-terminating steps which consumes the free radicals and produces ethane as a by-product.
Q2
An alkene A on ozonolysis gives a mixture of ethanal and pentan-3-one. Write the structure and IUPAC name of A.
Ethanal = CH₃CHO (from C1=C2 part), Pentan-3-one = CH₃CH₂COCH₂CH₃ (from C2=C part with ethyl groups). Working back: A = CH₃CH=C(CH₂CH₃)₂ → actually CH₃CH=CHCOCH₂CH₃... Let's think: ethanal gives –CH=, pentan-3-one gives =C(CH₂CH₃)– side. So A = CH₃–CH=C(CH₂CH₃)–CH₂CH₃ ... IUPAC: The alkene is 3-ethylpent-2-ene: CH₃–CH=C(C₂H₅)–CH₂CH₃. On ozonolysis: CH₃CHO + CH₃CH₂CO–CH₂CH₃ (pentan-3-one). ✓
Q3
An alkene A contains three C–C bonds, eight C–H σ bonds and one C–C π bond. On ozonolysis it gives two moles of an aldehyde of molar mass 44 u. Write the IUPAC name of A.
Aldehyde with molar mass 44 u = CH₃CHO (ethanal, MW = 44). Two moles of same aldehyde → A is symmetrical alkene. Both sides give CH₃CHO → A is CH₃–CH=CH–CH₃. IUPAC name: But-2-ene.
Q4
Addition of HBr to propene gives 2-bromopropane, while in the presence of benzoyl peroxide, the same reaction gives 1-bromopropane. Explain.
Without peroxide: Electrophilic addition via Markovnikov rule. H⁺ attacks C1 (more H), forming more stable 2° carbocation at C2. Br⁻ attacks C2 → 2-bromopropane. With peroxide (Kharash effect): Free radical mechanism. Peroxide generates Br• radical which attacks C1 (less substituted) forming more stable 2° carbon radical at C2. H• then attacks C2 from HBr → 1-bromopropane (anti-Markovnikov).
Q5
Why is benzene extraordinarily stable though it contains three double bonds?
Benzene is a resonance hybrid of two Kekulé structures. All six π electrons are completely delocalised over all 6 carbon atoms forming a stable aromatic ring. The delocalised electrons are attracted more strongly by the 6 carbon nuclei than localised electrons between 2 carbons would be. This resonance stabilisation (resonance energy) makes benzene much more stable than the hypothetical cyclohexatriene. X-ray data confirms: all C–C bonds are equal at 139 pm — no pure double bonds exist.
Q6
What are the necessary conditions for any system to be aromatic?
(i) Planarity — the molecule must be flat. (ii) Complete delocalisation of π electrons in the ring — continuous p-orbital overlap. (iii) Presence of (4n + 2) π electrons where n = 0, 1, 2, 3,... (Hückel Rule). Examples: Benzene (n=1, 6π), Naphthalene (n=2, 10π), Cyclopentadienyl anion (n=1, 6π).
Q7
Draw the cis and trans structures of hex-2-ene. Which isomer will have higher boiling point and why?
cis-Hex-2-ene: CH₃ and CH₂CH₂CH₃ on same side of C=C. trans-Hex-2-ene: on opposite sides. cis isomer has higher boiling point because it is more polar (dipole moments of the C-C bonds don't fully cancel), resulting in stronger intermolecular dipole-dipole interactions compared to the non-polar trans isomer.
Q8
Arrange benzene, n-hexane and ethyne in decreasing order of acidic behaviour. Give reason.
Ethyne > Benzene > n-Hexane. Reason: C–H acidity depends on electronegativity of the C atom (i.e., s-character). Ethyne C is sp hybridised (50% s) → most electronegative → H is most acidic. Benzene C is sp² (33% s) → intermediate. n-Hexane C is sp³ (25% s) → least electronegative → H is least acidic.
Q9
Why is the Wurtz reaction not preferred for preparation of alkanes with an odd number of carbon atoms?
Wurtz reaction: 2R–X + 2Na → R–R + 2NaX. Using the same alkyl halide always gives a product with an even number of carbons. Using two different alkyl halides (R–X + R'–X) gives a mixture of THREE products: R–R, R–R', and R'–R'. Separation of the desired product from this mixture is impractical. Therefore Wurtz reaction cannot be used for specific synthesis of odd-carbon alkanes.
Q10
Out of benzene, m-dinitrobenzene and toluene, which will undergo nitration most easily and why?
Toluene undergoes nitration most easily. –CH₃ group is an electron-donating (+I effect) ortho/para director — it increases electron density in the ring, activating it for electrophilic attack. Benzene has no substituent (reference). m-Dinitrobenzene has two –NO₂ groups (strongly deactivating, –I and –R effects) → ring electron density greatly decreased → most resistant to further nitration. Order: Toluene > Benzene > m-Dinitrobenzene.
Q11
Write all the alkenes which on hydrogenation give 2-methylbutane.
2-Methylbutane = (CH₃)₂CHCH₂CH₃. The alkenes that give this on hydrogenation must have the same carbon skeleton with one double bond at different positions: (i) 2-Methylbut-1-ene: CH₂=C(CH₃)CH₂CH₃ (ii) 2-Methylbut-2-ene: CH₃C(CH₃)=CHCH₃ (iii) 3-Methylbut-1-ene: CH₂=CHCH(CH₃)CH₃
Q12
Methane cannot be prepared by Kolbe's electrolytic method. Why?
In Kolbe's method, at the anode: 2RCOO⁻ → 2RCOO• → 2R• + 2CO₂ → R–R. To prepare methane (CH₄), we would need •CH₃ radical to pair with another •CH₃ to give C₂H₆ (ethane), not CH₄. To get CH₄, we would need •H radical — but formate (HCOO⁻) would give CO₂ only at the anode. There is no carboxylic acid that can produce •CH₃ which can combine to give methane directly. So methane (C₁) cannot be made by this method which always gives even-numbered alkanes.
