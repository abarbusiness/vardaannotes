Mechanical Properties of Solids

CBSE Class 11 Physics • Chapter 08

Chapter Overview: This chapter deals with the property of elasticity of solids. We will understand Stress, Strain, Hooke's Law, and different Moduli of Elasticity (Young's, Bulk, Shear). We will also analyze the Stress-Strain curve.

8.1 Elasticity and Plasticity

Deforming Force: A force that changes the size or shape of a body.

Restoring Force: An internal force that tends to restore the body to its original size and shape.

Elasticity: The property of a body by virtue of which it tends to regain its original size and shape after the removal of deforming force (e.g., Steel, Rubber).

Plasticity: The property by virtue of which a body does not regain its original shape and gets permanently deformed (e.g., Putty, Mud).

Practice Problem 1

Q: Which is more elastic: Steel or Rubber? Why?

Ans: Steel is more elastic than rubber. In physics, elasticity is measured by the resistance to deformation (Young's Modulus). For the same applied force, steel deforms much less than rubber, meaning it generates a stronger restoring force.

8.2 Stress and Strain

Stress ($\sigma$)

The restoring force per unit area is called stress.

$$ \sigma = \frac{F}{A} $$

Unit: $N/m^2$ or Pascal (Pa). Dimension: $[ML^{-1}T^{-2}]$.

Types:

  • Longitudinal Stress: Tensile (stretching) or Compressive (squeezing).
  • Tangential/Shear Stress: Force acts parallel to the surface.
  • Hydraulic/Volume Stress: Force acts perpendicular to the surface from all sides (pressure).

Strain ($\varepsilon$)

The ratio of change in dimension to the original dimension.

$$ \text{Strain} = \frac{\text{Change in dimension}}{\text{Original dimension}} $$

Unit: No Unit (It is a ratio).

Types:

  • Longitudinal Strain: $\varepsilon_l = \Delta L / L$
  • Shearing Strain: $\theta = \Delta x / L$
  • Volume Strain: $\varepsilon_v = \Delta V / V$

Practice Problem 2 (Stress Calculation)

Q: A load of 4.0 kg is suspended from a ceiling through a steel wire of radius 2.0 mm. Find the tensile stress developed in the wire. ($g = 3.1\pi \text{ ms}^{-2}$)

Ans: $F = mg = 4 \times 3.1\pi$. Area $A = \pi r^2 = \pi (2 \times 10^{-3})^2$.
$\sigma = \frac{F}{A} = \frac{4 \times 3.1\pi}{\pi \times 4 \times 10^{-6}} = \frac{12.4}{4 \times 10^{-6}} = 3.1 \times 10^6 \text{ N/m}^2$.

8.3 Hooke's Law

Statement: For small deformations (within the elastic limit), stress is directly proportional to strain.

$$ \text{Stress} \propto \text{Strain} $$

$$ \text{Stress} = k \times \text{Strain} $$

Where $k$ is the Modulus of Elasticity.

8.4 Stress-Strain Curve

A graph showing the variation of stress with strain for a material (typically a metal wire).

  • Proportional Limit (A): Hooke's law is obeyed.
  • Elastic Limit / Yield Point (B): The material returns to original shape if unloaded.
  • Permanent Set: If deformed beyond B, it won't regain original shape entirely.
  • Fracture Point (E): The material breaks.

Practice Problem 3 (Conceptual)

Q: How do you distinguish between ductile and brittle materials using a Stress-Strain curve?

Ans:

  • Ductile: Large plastic region between yield point and fracture point (e.g., Copper, Steel). able to be drawn into wires.
  • Brittle: Very small plastic region; fracture point is close to yield point (e.g., Glass, Cast iron).

8.5 Moduli of Elasticity

1. Young's Modulus (Y)

Ratio of Longitudinal Stress to Longitudinal Strain.

$$ Y = \frac{\text{Longitudinal Stress}}{\text{Longitudinal Strain}} = \frac{F/A}{\Delta L/L} = \frac{FL}{A\Delta L} $$

For a wire loaded with mass $M$: $Y = \frac{MgL}{\pi r^2 \Delta L}$

Practice Problem 4 (Young's Modulus)

Q: A structural steel rod has a radius of 10 mm and a length of 1.0 m. A 100 kN force stretches it along its length. Calculation the stress and elongation. ($Y_{\text{steel}} = 2.0 \times 10^{11} \text{ Pa}$)

Ans: $A = \pi (0.01)^2 = 3.14 \times 10^{-4} \text{ m}^2$, $F = 10^5 \text{ N}$.
Stress $\sigma = F/A = 10^5 / (3.14 \times 10^{-4}) = 3.18 \times 10^8 \text{ Pa}$.
$\Delta L = \frac{FL}{AY} = \frac{\sigma L}{Y} = \frac{3.18 \times 10^8 \times 1}{2.0 \times 10^{11}} = 1.59 \times 10^{-3} \text{ m} = \mathbf{1.59 \text{ mm}}$.

2. Shear Modulus / Modulus of Rigidity (G or $\eta$)

Ratio of Shearing Stress to Shearing Strain.

$$ G = \frac{\text{Shearing Stress}}{\text{Shearing Strain}} = \frac{F/A}{\Delta x/L} = \frac{F/A}{\theta} $$

3. Bulk Modulus (B)

Ratio of Hydraulic Stress to Volume Strain.

$$ B = \frac{-P}{\Delta V/V} = - \frac{PV}{\Delta V} $$

Negative sign indicates that an increase in pressure leads to a decrease in volume.

Compressibility (k): The reciprocal of Bulk Modulus. $k = 1/B$. Gases are much more compressible than solids.

Practice Problem 5 (Bulk Modulus)

Q: The average depth of the Indian Ocean is 3000 m. Calculate the fractional compression $\Delta V/V$ of water at the bottom. ($B_{\text{water}} = 2.2 \times 10^9 \text{ Pa}$, $g = 10$)

Ans: Pressure $P = h\rho g = 3000 \times 1000 \times 10 = 3 \times 10^7 \text{ Pa}$.
$\frac{\Delta V}{V} = \frac{P}{B} = \frac{3 \times 10^7}{2.2 \times 10^9} = \mathbf{1.36 \times 10^{-2}}$.

8.6 Poisson's Ratio ($\sigma$)

When a wire is stretched, it becomes longer but thinner. The ratio of lateral strain to longitudinal strain is called Poisson's Ratio.

$$ \sigma = \frac{\text{Lateral Strain}}{\text{Longitudinal Strain}} = - \frac{\Delta d/d}{\Delta L/L} $$

Theoretical value lies between -1 and 0.5. Practical value lies between 0 and 0.5.

8.7 Elastic Potential Energy in a Stretched Wire

Work is done against the restoring force to stretch a wire. This work is stored as potential energy ($U$).

$$ U = \frac{1}{2} \times \text{Stress} \times \text{Strain} \times \text{Volume} $$

Energy Density ($u$) = Energy per unit Volume:

$$ u = \frac{1}{2} \times \text{Stress} \times \text{Strain} = \frac{1}{2} Y (\text{Strain})^2 $$

Practice Problem 6 (Energy)

Q: A steel wire of length 4 m and area $3 \times 10^{-6} \text{ m}^2$ is stretched by 2 mm. Calculate the energy stored. ($Y = 2 \times 10^{11} \text{ N/m}^2$)

Ans: $\Delta L = 2 \times 10^{-3} \text{ m}$, $L = 4 \text{ m}$.
Strain = $\frac{2 \times 10^{-3}}{4} = 0.5 \times 10^{-3}$.
$U = \frac{1}{2} Y (\text{strain})^2 \times V = \frac{1}{2} \times (2 \times 10^{11}) \times (0.5 \times 10^{-3})^2 \times (3 \times 10^{-6} \times 4)$
$U = 10^{11} \times 0.25 \times 10^{-6} \times 12 \times 10^{-6} = 3 \times 10^{-1} = \mathbf{0.3 \text{ J}}$.

Important Question Bank

Q7. Hots

Question: Why are springs made of steel and not of copper?

Ans: Steel is more elastic than copper (Young's modulus of steel is greater). It returns to its original shape more effectively after the deforming force is removed, which is the primary requirement for a spring.

Q8. Application

Question: Why is the upper face of the application of a cantilever made of I-section?

Ans: To reduce the weight of the beam while maintaining high strength. The top and bottom sections bear the maximum stress (compressive and tensile), so more material is needed there. The middle part (neutral axis) has zero stress, so material can be saved there.
