14.1 Introduction to Waves

Wave Motion: It is a mode of energy transfer from one point to another without the permanent transport of matter. Patterns of disturbance move through the medium.

Mechanism: Particles of the medium oscillate about their mean positions. The disturbance is handed over from one particle to the next due to Elasticity and Inertia of the medium.

  • Elasticity: Brings the particle back to mean position.
  • Inertia: Allows the particle to overshoot the mean position and continue oscillation.

Types of Waves based on Medium:

  • Mechanical Waves: Require material medium (Solid, Liquid, Gas). Examples: Sound waves, Water waves, Seismic waves, Strings. governed by Newton's Laws.
  • Electromagnetic Waves: Do not require medium. Can travel in vacuum. Examples: Light, Radio, X-rays. Speed $c = 3 \times 10^8$ m/s.
  • Matter Waves: Associated with moving particles (Quantum nature). Example: Electron microscope.

14.3 Progressive Wave (Travelling Wave)

Equation of a Plane Progressive Harmonic Wave:

Consider a wave traveling along the positive x-axis. The displacement $y$ of a particle at position $x$ and time $t$ is:

$$ y(x, t) = A \sin(kx - \omega t + \phi) $$

Simplest Case ($\phi=0$): $y = A \sin(kx - \omega t)$

Detailed Analysis of Terms:

  • $y(x,t)$: Instantaneous displacement of particle at $x$ at time $t$.
  • $A$ (Amplitude): Maximum displacement from mean positions.
  • $\omega$ (Angular Frequency): Rate of change of phase with time. $\omega = \frac{2\pi}{T}$.
  • $k$ (Angular Wave Number): Rate of change of phase with position. $k = \frac{2\pi}{\lambda}$.
  • $(kx - \omega t + \phi)$: Total Phase. Determines the state of motion.

Relation between Phase Diff ($\Delta \phi$), Path Diff ($\Delta x$), and Time Diff ($\Delta t$):

1. Phase difference for two particles separated by distance $\Delta x$:

$$ \Delta \phi = \frac{2\pi}{\lambda} \Delta x $$

2. Phase difference for the SAME particle after time interval $\Delta t$:

$$ \Delta \phi = \frac{2\pi}{T} \Delta t $$
Practice Problem 1

Q: A wave equation is $y = 0.05 \sin(80x - 3t)$. Find velocity and wavelength. (SI units)

Solution: Compare with standard $y = A \sin(kx - \omega t)$.

  • $k = 80$ rad/m $\Rightarrow \frac{2\pi}{\lambda} = 80 \Rightarrow \lambda = \frac{2\pi}{80} = \frac{\pi}{40}$ m.
  • $\omega = 3$ rad/s.
  • Wave Velocity $v = \frac{\omega}{k} = \frac{3}{80} = 0.0375$ m/s.

14.4 Speed of a Travelling Wave

$$ v = \frac{\omega}{k} = \frac{2\pi\nu}{2\pi/\lambda} = \nu \lambda $$

Wave velocity depends ONLY on the properties of the medium (Elasticity $E$ and Inertia $\rho$).

$$ v = \sqrt{\frac{E}{\rho}} $$

Case 1: Transverse Wave on Stretched String

Speed depends on Tension ($T$) and Linear Mass Density ($\mu = m/L$).

$$ v = \sqrt{\frac{T}{\mu}} $$

Note: $v$ does not depend on frequency or wavelength directly.

Case 2: Longitudinal Wave (Sound) in Fluids

Newton's Formula (Historical):

Newton assumed sound propagation is an Isothermal Process (Temp remains constant). For isothermal change, Bulk Modulus $B = P$ (Pressure).

Formula: $v = \sqrt{\frac{P}{\rho}}$.

At STP ($P=1.01 \times 10^5$ Pa, $\rho = 1.29$ kg/m$^3$), $v \approx 280$ m/s.

Discrepancy: Experimental value is ~331 m/s. Newton's formula failed (15% error).

Laplace's Correction (Accurate):

Laplace argued that sound travels fast; compressions/rarefactions occur rapidly. Heat doesn't have time to exchange. Hence, it is an Adiabatic Process.

For adiabatic change, Bulk Modulus $B_{ad} = \gamma P$, where $\gamma = C_p/C_v$ is the adiabatic index.

$$ v = \sqrt{\frac{\gamma P}{\rho}} $$

For air (diatomic), $\gamma = 1.4$. $v = \sqrt{1.4} \times 280 \approx 331.3$ m/s. Matches perfectly!

Factors Affecting Speed of Sound in Gas:

  1. Temperature: $v \propto \sqrt{T}$. Speed increases by 0.61 m/s for every $1^\circ$C rise.
  2. Molecular Weight: $v \propto \frac{1}{\sqrt{M}}$. (Hydrogen transmits sound faster than Oxygen).
  3. Humidity: Moist air is lighter ($\rho$ decreases) than dry air. Hence speed increases with humidity.
  4. Pressure: NO EFFECT (at constant temperature), because $P/\rho$ remains constant.

14.5 Principle of Superposition

When two waves interfere, the resultant displacement is the vector sum of individual displacements.

$$ \vec{y} = \vec{y}_1 + \vec{y}_2 $$

14.6 Reflection & Standing Waves

Boundary Conditions:

  • Rigid/Fixed End: Wave reflects with $\pi$ phase change (Crest $\to$ Trough). Node forms at boundary.
  • Free/Open End: Wave reflects with 0 phase change (Crest $\to$ Crest). Antinode forms at boundary.

Analytical Treatment of Standing Waves

Two waves of same amplitude and frequency traveling in opposite directions:

Incident: $y_1 = A \sin(kx - \omega t)$

Reflected: $y_2 = A \sin(kx + \omega t)$ (Assumption: reflection from open end for simpler math)

Resultant $y = y_1 + y_2 = A[\sin(kx-\omega t) + \sin(kx+\omega t)]$

Using $\sin C + \sin D = 2 \sin \frac{C+D}{2} \cos \frac{C-D}{2}$:

$$ y = 2A \sin(kx) \cos(\omega t) $$

This is NOT a traveling wave (no $kx \pm \omega t$ term). Amplitudes vary with position $x$.

Nodes (Min Amplitude = 0): $\sin kx = 0 \Rightarrow kx = n\pi \Rightarrow x = \frac{n\lambda}{2}$.

Antinodes (Max Amplitude = 2A): $\sin kx = \pm 1 \Rightarrow x = (n+\frac{1}{2})\frac{\lambda}{2}$.

14.7 Beats

Phenomenon: When two waves of nearly equal frequencies $\nu_1$ and $\nu_2$ moving in same direction interfere, the intensity rises and falls periodically.

Derivation:

$y_1 = A \cos(2\pi\nu_1 t)$, $y_2 = A \cos(2\pi\nu_2 t)$.

$y = y_1 + y_2 = 2A \cos[\pi(\nu_1-\nu_2)t] \cos[\pi(\nu_1+\nu_2)t]$.

Amplitude term is $A_{mod} = 2A \cos[\pi(\nu_1-\nu_2)t]$.

Intensity $I \propto A_{mod}^2$. MaxIMA when $\cos[\dots] = \pm 1$. This happens with frequency $\nu_1 - \nu_2$.

$$ \nu_{beat} = |\nu_1 - \nu_2| $$

Practical Limit: Human ear can detect beats only if difference $\nu_{beat} < 10$ Hz (Persistence of hearing=0.1s).

14.8 Doppler Effect

The apparent change in frequency of sound due to the relative motion between the Source (S) and Observer (O).

General Formula:

$$ \nu' = \nu \left( \frac{v \pm v_o}{v \mp v_s} \right) $$

Where $v$ is speed of sound. Directions taken as positive in direction of sound travel ($S \to O$).

Case Condition Formula Result
1 Source moving towards Observer (at rest) $\nu' = \nu \left( \frac{v}{v - v_s} \right)$ $\nu' > \nu$
2 Source moving away from Observer (at rest) $\nu' = \nu \left( \frac{v}{v + v_s} \right)$ $\nu' < \nu$
3 Observer moving towards Source (at rest) $\nu' = \nu \left( \frac{v + v_o}{v} \right)$ $\nu' > \nu$
4 Observer moving away from Source (at rest) $\nu' = \nu \left( \frac{v - v_o}{v} \right)$ $\nu' < \nu$
5 Both moving towards each other $\nu' = \nu \left( \frac{v + v_o}{v - v_s} \right)$ $\nu' > \nu$ (Max increase)
6 Both moving away from each other $\nu' = \nu \left( \frac{v - v_o}{v + v_s} \right)$ $\nu' < \nu$ (Max decrease)
