Mechanical Properties of Fluids

Chapter Overview: This chapter deals with the properties of fluids (liquids and gases) at rest (Hydrostatics) and in motion (Hydrodynamics). Key topics include Pascal's Law, Bernoulli's Principle, Viscosity, and Surface Tension.

9.1 Introduction to Fluids

Fluid: A substance that can flow is called a fluid. Liquids and gases are fluids. They do not possess a definite shape of their own.

Thrust: The total normal force exerted by a fluid on a surface.

Pressure (P): The thrust acting per unit area.

$$ P = \frac{Thrust}{Area} = \frac{F}{A} $$

  • SI Unit: Pascal (Pa) or $N/m^2$
  • Dimensional Formula: $[ML^{-1}T^{-2}]$
Practice Problem 1

Q: A force of 50 N acts uniformly on an area of $10 \text{ cm}^2$. Calculate the pressure in Pascal.

Ans: $F = 50 \text{ N}$, $A = 10 \text{ cm}^2 = 10 \times 10^{-4} \text{ m}^2$.
$P = \frac{F}{A} = \frac{50}{10^{-3}} = 50,000 \text{ Pa}$.

9.2 Pascal's Law

Statement: If gravity is neglected, the pressure at every point of a liquid in equilibrium of rest is same.

Alternative Statement: Theoretical pressure applied to an enclosed fluid is transmitted undiminished to every portion of the fluid and the walls of the containing vessel.

[Diagram: Demonstration of Pascal's Law showing a container with pistons of different areas being pushed. Or a spherical vessel with holes showing liquid spurting out equally.]

Applications of Pascal's Law (Hydraulic Lift)

A small force $f$ applied on a smaller piston of area $a$ creates a pressure $P = f/a$. This pressure is transmitted to the larger piston of area $A$, creating a large force $F = P \times A$.

$$ P = \frac{f}{a} = \frac{F}{A} $$

$$ \therefore F = f \times \left( \frac{A}{a} \right) $$

Practice Problem 2 (Hydraulic Lift)

Q: In a hydraulic lift, the input piston has a radius of 10 cm and the output piston has a radius of 30 cm. If a force of 100 N is applied to the input piston, calculate the force exerted by the output piston.

Ans: $F_1 = 100N$, $r_1 = 10 cm$, $r_2 = 30 cm$.
Using Pascal's Law: $\frac{F_1}{A_1} = \frac{F_2}{A_2}$
$F_2 = F_1 \times \frac{\pi r_2^2}{\pi r_1^2} = 100 \times \left(\frac{30}{10}\right)^2 = 100 \times 9 = \mathbf{900 N}$

9.3 Variation of Pressure with Depth

Pressure inside a fluid increases with depth due to the weight of the liquid column above.

$$ P = P_a + h\rho g $$

Gauge Pressure: The excess pressure above atmospheric pressure. $P_{gauge} = P - P_a = h\rho g$.

Practice Problem 3

Q: What is the pressure on a swimmer 10 m below the surface of a lake? ($g = 10 \text{ m/s}^2$, $\rho = 1000 \text{ kg/m}^3$, $P_a = 1.01 \times 10^5 \text{ Pa}$)

Ans: $P = P_a + h\rho g = 1.01 \times 10^5 + 10 \times 1000 \times 10 = 1.01 \times 10^5 + 1 \times 10^5 = \mathbf{2.01 \times 10^5 \text{ Pa}}$

9.4 Streamline Flow (Hydrodynamics)

Streamline Flow: The flow of a fluid is said to be streamline or steady if the velocity of every fluid particle at any given point remains constant with time.

Equation of Continuity: $A_1 v_1 = A_2 v_2 = \text{constant}$ (based on Conservation of Mass)

[Diagram: Comparison of Streamline flow (parallel smooth lines) and Turbulent flow (chaotic eddies/swirls).]
Practice Problem 4 (Continuity)

Q: Water flows through a horizontal pipe of varying cross-section. If the speed of water is $2 \text{ m/s}$ where the area is $10 \text{ cm}^2$, find the speed where the area is $5 \text{ cm}^2$.

Ans: $A_1 v_1 = A_2 v_2 \Rightarrow 10 \times 2 = 5 \times v_2 \Rightarrow v_2 = \mathbf{4 \text{ m/s}}$.

9.5 Bernoulli's Principle

Important: Bernoulli's principle is based on the Law of Conservation of Energy.

Statement: For the streamline flow of an ideal (incompressible and non-viscous) fluid, the sum of pressure energy, kinetic energy, and potential energy per unit volume remains constant.

$$ P + \frac{1}{2}\rho v^2 + \rho gh = \text{constant} $$

Applications of Bernoulli's Principle

  1. Venturi-meter: Device to measure the rate of flow of liquid through a pipe.
  2. Torricelli's Law: Speed of efflux $v = \sqrt{2gh}$.
  3. Dynamic Lift: Lift of an aircraft wing or Magnus effect in spinning balls.
Practice Problem 5 (Bernoulli)

Q: Water flows through a horizontal pipe. At a point where pressure is $P$, velocity is $v$. At another point pressure is $P/2$. What is the velocity at this point?

Ans: Using Bernoulli's equation for horizontal pipe ($h_1=h_2$):
$P + \frac{1}{2}\rho v^2 = \frac{P}{2} + \frac{1}{2}\rho v_2^2 \Rightarrow P + \rho v^2 = \rho v_2^2 \Rightarrow v_2 = \sqrt{v^2 + P/\rho}$.

Practice Problem 6 (Conceptual)

Q: Why do roofs of huts get blown off during storms?

Ans: High wind speed over the roof creates low pressure (Bernoulli's Principle). The pressure inside the hut is atmospheric (high). This pressure difference creates an upward thrust that lifts the roof.

9.6 Viscosity

Viscosity: The property of a fluid due to which it opposes the relative motion between its different layers.

Stokes' Law: $F = 6\pi \eta r v$

Terminal Velocity: $v_T = \frac{2r^2 (\rho - \sigma)g}{9\eta}$

Practice Problem 7 (Viscosity)

Q: A rain drop of radius $0.3 \text{ mm}$ falls through air with a terminal velocity of $1 \text{ m/s}$. The viscosity of air is $1.8 \times 10^{-5} \text{ N s m}^{-2}$. Find the viscous force on the rain drop.

Ans: $F = 6\pi \eta r v = 6 \times 3.14 \times 1.8 \times 10^{-5} \times 0.3 \times 10^{-3} \times 1 \approx \mathbf{1.02 \times 10^{-7} \text{ N}}$.

9.7 Surface Tension

Surface Tension (S): Property of liquid surface to behave like a stretched elastic membrane. $S = F/l$.

Excess Pressure: Inside a drop $P_{ex} = 2S/R$; Inside a bubble $P_{ex} = 4S/R$.

Practice Problem 8 (Excess Pressure)

Q: Calculate the excess pressure inside a soap bubble of radius $5 \text{ mm}$ if surface tension of soap solution is $2.5 \times 10^{-2} \text{ N/m}$.

Ans: For soap bubble (2 surfaces), $P_{ex} = \frac{4S}{R} = \frac{4 \times 2.5 \times 10^{-2}}{5 \times 10^{-3}} = \frac{10 \times 10^{-2}}{5 \times 10^{-3}} = \mathbf{20 \text{ Pa}}$.

Capillarity

Ascent Formula: $h = \frac{2S \cos \theta}{r \rho g}$

Practice Problem 9 (Capillarity)

Q: Water rises to a height of 5 cm in a capillary tube. If the tube is cut at a height of 3 cm, what happens?

Ans: Water will rise to the top of the 3 cm tube and stay there with a changed radius of curvature ($R'$). It will NOT overflow. The new radius of curvature adjusts such that $h'R' = hR$.

Additional Important Questions

Q10. Derivation (Exam Favorite)

Question: Derive the expression for the excess pressure inside a liquid drop.

Ans: Consider a drop of radius $R$. Let radius increase by $dR$.
Work done = $P_{ex} \times 4\pi R^2 \times dR$.
Change in Energy = $S \times 8\pi R dR$.
Equating: $P_{ex} = \frac{2S}{R}$.

Q11. Conceptual (Application)

Question: Why is it difficult to stop bleeding from a cut in the body at high altitudes?

Ans: At high altitudes, atmospheric pressure is low. The internal blood pressure is much higher than external pressure, forcing blood out rapidly.
