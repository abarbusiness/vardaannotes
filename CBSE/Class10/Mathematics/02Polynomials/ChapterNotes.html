Polynomials

Class 10 Mathematics • Chapter 02 (Rationalized Syllabus)

1. Introduction

Polynomial $p(x)$: An algebraic expression in variable $x$ where the exponents of the variable are non-negative integers (Whole numbers).

Degree of a Polynomial

The highest power of $x$ in $p(x)$ is called the degree of the polynomial.

  • Linear Polynomial (Degree 1): $ax + b$, where $a \neq 0$. Example: $2x - 3$.
  • Quadratic Polynomial (Degree 2): $ax^2 + bx + c$, where $a \neq 0$. Example: $2x^2 + 3x - 5$.
  • Cubic Polynomial (Degree 3): $ax^3 + bx^2 + cx + d$, where $a \neq 0$. Example: $x^3 - 2$.

2. Geometrical Meaning of Zeroes

A zero of a polynomial $p(x)$ is the $x$-coordinate of the point where the graph of $y = p(x)$ intersects the x-axis.

KEY CONCEPT A polynomial of degree $n$ has at most $n$ zeroes.

Case 1: Two Distinct Zeroes

The graph of $ax^2+bx+c$ cuts the x-axis at two distinct points. This happens when the discriminant $D > 0$.

3. Relationship between Zeroes and Coefficients

This is the most critical part for board exams. We typically deal with Quadratic Polynomials.

Quadratic Polynomial ($ax^2 + bx + c$)

Let $\alpha$ (alpha) and $\beta$ (beta) be the zeroes.

FORMULA $$ \text{Sum of zeroes } (\alpha + \beta) = -\frac{b}{a} = -\frac{\text{Coefficient of } x}{\text{Coefficient of } x^2} $$
$$ \text{Product of zeroes } (\alpha \beta) = \frac{c}{a} = \frac{\text{Constant term}}{\text{Coefficient of } x^2} $$

Forming a Quadratic Polynomial

If we know the Sum ($S$) and Product ($P$) of zeroes, the polynomial is given by:

FORMULA $$ p(x) = k[x^2 - (\text{Sum})x + \text{Product}] $$ $$ p(x) = k[x^2 - (\alpha + \beta)x + \alpha\beta] $$

Note: $k$ is any non-zero real number (constant).

4. Important Exam Questions (PYQ Trends)

Type 1: Finding Zeroes and Verifying Relationship

Question: Find the zeroes of $x^2 + 7x + 10$ and verify the relationship between zeroes and coefficients.

Solution:

  1. Factorize: $x^2 + 5x + 2x + 10 = x(x+5) + 2(x+5) = (x+5)(x+2)$.
  2. Zeroes: $x+5=0 \Rightarrow x=-5$ and $x+2=0 \Rightarrow x=-2$. So, $\alpha = -5, \beta = -2$.
  3. Verification:
    • Sum = $\alpha + \beta = -5 + (-2) = -7$. AND $-b/a = -7/1 = -7$. (Matched)
    • Product = $\alpha \beta = (-5)(-2) = 10$. AND $c/a = 10/1 = 10$. (Matched)

Type 2: Forming Polynomial from Zeroes

Question: Find a quadratic polynomial whose zeroes are $-3$ and $2$.

Solution:

  1. Sum ($\alpha + \beta$) = $-3 + 2 = -1$.
  2. Product ($\alpha \beta$) = $-3 \times 2 = -6$.
  3. Formula: $k[x^2 - (\text{Sum})x + \text{Product}]$.
  4. Polynomial: $k[x^2 - (-1)x + (-6)] \Rightarrow k(x^2 + x - 6)$.

Type 3: Advanced Relationships ($\alpha, \beta$)

Question: If $\alpha$ and $\beta$ are zeroes of $3x^2 + 5x - 2$, find the value of $\alpha^2 + \beta^2$.

Solution:

  1. From polynomial: $a=3, b=5, c=-2$.
  2. $\alpha + \beta = -5/3$ and $\alpha\beta = -2/3$.
  3. Identity: $\alpha^2 + \beta^2 = (\alpha + \beta)^2 - 2\alpha\beta$.
  4. Substitute: $(-5/3)^2 - 2(-2/3) = 25/9 + 4/3$.
  5. LCM is 9: $(25 + 12)/9 = 37/9$.
EXAM TRICK Often questions give "Sum and Product" directly (e.g., Sum=1/4, Product=-1). Students mistake these for $\alpha$ and $\beta$. Read carefully if it says "Zeroes are..." or "Sum and Product of zeroes are...".
