SECTION 1: INTRODUCTION TO REAL NUMBERS

1.1 Number System Classification

Before studying the theorems of Class 10, we must perfectly understand the hierarchy of the number system. Think of the number system as boxes fitting inside larger boxes:

$$ \mathbb{N} \subset \mathbb{W} \subset \mathbb{Z} \subset \mathbb{Q} \subset \mathbb{R} $$
  • Natural Numbers ($\mathbb{N}$): The counting numbers starting from $1$.
    Example: $\{1, 2, 3, 4, \dots\}$
  • Whole Numbers ($\mathbb{W}$): Natural numbers plus zero.
    Example: $\{0, 1, 2, 3, \dots\}$
  • Integers ($\mathbb{Z}$): All positive and negative whole numbers.
    Example: $\{\dots, -3, -2, -1, 0, 1, 2, 3, \dots\}$
Definition Rational Numbers ($\mathbb{Q}$): Numbers that can be expressed in the form $\frac{p}{q}$, where $p$ and $q$ are integers and $q \neq 0$.

Key Property: Their decimal expansions are either terminating (like $0.5$) or non-terminating but repeating (like $0.333\dots$).
Examples: $\frac{1}{2}, \frac{-5}{7}, 4$ (since $4 = \frac{4}{1}$), $0, \sqrt{25}$.
Definition Irrational Numbers: Numbers that cannot be expressed in the form $\frac{p}{q}$.

Key Property: Their decimal expansions are strictly non-terminating and non-repeating.
Examples: $\sqrt{2}, \sqrt{3}, \pi, 0.101101110\dots$
Important What are Real Numbers ($\mathbb{R}$)?
A Real Number is any number that can be plotted on the number line. It is the complete collection of all Rational AND Irrational numbers together. Every real number is either rational OR irrational (it can never be both).

SECTION 2: EUCLID'S DIVISION ALGORITHM

2.1 Euclid's Division Algorithm - Step by Step Method

Definition: An algorithm is a series of well-defined steps. Euclid's Division Algorithm is a technique to compute the Highest Common Factor (HCF) of two given positive integers, say $a$ and $b$ (where $a > b$).

Algorithm Steps Step 1: Divide $a$ by $b$ and write it in the form $a = bq + r$, where $0 \leq r < b$.
Step 2: If $r = 0$, then $b$ is the HCF of $a$ and $b$.
Step 3: If $r \neq 0$, repeat the division step for $b$ (the old divisor) and $r$ (the new remainder).
Step 4: Continue the process till the remainder is zero. The divisor at this final stage will be the required HCF.

2.2 Finding HCF Using Euclid's Division Algorithm

Example 1: Find the HCF of 135 and 225.
Solution: Since $225 > 135$, we apply repeated division steps:
Step 1: $225 = 135 \times 1 + 90$ (Since remainder $90 \neq 0$, divide $135$ by $90$)
Step 2: $135 = 90 \times 1 + 45$ (Since remainder $45 \neq 0$, divide $90$ by $45$)
Step 3: $90 = 45 \times 2 + 0$
The remainder has now become zero. The divisor at this stage is $45$.
Therefore, HCF(135, 225) = 45.
Example 2: Find the HCF of 867 and 255.
Solution: Since $867 > 255$, apply repeated division:
Step 1: $867 = 255 \times 3 + 102$ ($r \neq 0$)
Step 2: $255 = 102 \times 2 + 51$ ($r \neq 0$)
Step 3: $102 = 51 \times 2 + 0$
The remainder is zero. The last divisor is $51$.
Therefore, HCF(867, 255) = 51.
Example 3: Find the HCF of 4052 and 12576. (NCERT Example)
Solution: Since $12576 > 4052$:
Step 1: $12576 = 4052 \times 3 + 420$
Step 2: $4052 = 420 \times 9 + 272$
Step 3: $420 = 272 \times 1 + 148$
Step 4: $272 = 148 \times 1 + 124$
Step 5: $148 = 124 \times 1 + 24$
Step 6: $124 = 24 \times 5 + 4$
Step 7: $24 = 4 \times 6 + 0$
Therefore, HCF(4052, 12576) = 4.
Example 4: Find the HCF of 196 and 38220.
Solution: Since $38220 > 196$:
Step 1: $38220 = 196 \times 195 + 0$
The remainder is zero in the very first step!
Therefore, HCF(196, 38220) = 196.
Example 5: Find the HCF of 510 and 92.
Solution: Since $510 > 92$:
Step 1: $510 = 92 \times 5 + 50$
Step 2: $92 = 50 \times 1 + 42$
Step 3: $50 = 42 \times 1 + 8$
Step 4: $42 = 8 \times 5 + 2$
Step 5: $8 = 2 \times 4 + 0$
Therefore, HCF(510, 92) = 2.
Example 6 (Three Numbers): Find the HCF of 441, 567 and 693.
Solution: To find HCF of 3 numbers, first find the HCF of any two numbers, then find the HCF of that result and the third number. Let's pick $441$ and $567$.
Step 1: $567 = 441 \times 1 + 126$
Step 2: $441 = 126 \times 3 + 63$
Step 3: $126 = 63 \times 2 + 0$ $\implies$ HCF(441, 567) = $63$.
Now, find HCF of $63$ and the third number $693$:
Step 4: $693 = 63 \times 11 + 0$
Since remainder is zero, the HCF is $63$.
Therefore, HCF(441, 567, 693) = 63.

2.3 Word Problems on HCF Using Euclid's Algorithm

Examiner's Tip: In word problems, whenever you are asked to find the "maximum capacity", "longest rod", "maximum number of groups", or divide things into the "largest equal size", you must calculate the HCF.

Word Problem 1: The Sweet Seller
A sweet seller has $420$ kaju barfis and $130$ badam barfis. She wants to stack them in such a way that each stack has the same number, and they take up the least area of the tray. What is the maximum number of barfis that can be placed in each stack?
Solution: To minimize the area, we need to maximize the number of barfis in each stack. So, we need to find HCF(420, 130).
$420 = 130 \times 3 + 30$
$130 = 30 \times 4 + 10$
$30 = 10 \times 3 + 0$
The HCF is 10. Therefore, the sweet seller can make stacks of 10 for both kinds of barfi.
Word Problem 2: Army Contingent
An army contingent of $616$ members is to march behind an army band of $32$ members in a parade. The two groups are to march in the same number of columns. What is the maximum number of columns in which they can march?
Solution: We must find HCF(616, 32).
$616 = 32 \times 19 + 8$
$32 = 8 \times 4 + 0$
The HCF is $8$. Therefore, they can march in a maximum of 8 columns.
Word Problem 3: Remainders Problem
Find the largest number which divides $70$ and $125$, leaving remainders $5$ and $8$ respectively.
Solution: Since the number leaves remainders $5$ and $8$, the numbers that are exactly divisible are:
$70 - 5 = 65$
$125 - 8 = 117$
Now, find HCF(117, 65).
$117 = 65 \times 1 + 52$
$65 = 52 \times 1 + 13$
$52 = 13 \times 4 + 0$
The largest number is 13.
Word Problem 4: Tiling the Floor
The length and breadth of a room are $8 \text{m } 25 \text{cm}$ and $6 \text{m } 75 \text{cm}$ respectively. Find the length of the longest measuring rod that can measure the dimensions of the room exactly.
Solution: Convert everything to cm.
Length $= 825$ cm. Breadth $= 675$ cm.
We need the HCF of $825$ and $675$.
$825 = 675 \times 1 + 150$
$675 = 150 \times 4 + 75$
$150 = 75 \times 2 + 0$
The longest rod is 75 cm long.

SECTION 3: FUNDAMENTAL THEOREM OF ARITHMETIC

3.1 Statement of Fundamental Theorem of Arithmetic

Theorem Statement: Every composite number can be expressed (factorised) as a product of prime numbers, and this factorisation is unique, apart from the order in which the prime factors occur.

Explanation: This means that if you break down a number into primes, there is only one specific set of prime numbers that multiplies to give that number. For example, $30 = 2 \times 3 \times 5$. Whether you write it as $5 \times 3 \times 2$ or $3 \times 2 \times 5$, the set of primes $\{2, 3, 5\}$ is unique to $30$.
Quick Revision: Prime Numbers only have two factors: $1$ and themselves ($2, 3, 5, 7, 11\dots$). Composite Numbers have more than two factors ($4, 6, 8, 9\dots$). Note: $1$ is neither prime nor composite.

3.2 Prime Factorisation

You can find prime factors using the Factor Tree Method or the standard Division Method (ladder method).

Examples of Prime Factorisation:
  • 140: $140 = 2 \times 70 = 2 \times 2 \times 35 = 2 \times 2 \times 5 \times 7 \implies \mathbf{2^2 \times 5 \times 7}$
  • 156: $156 = 2 \times 78 = 2 \times 2 \times 39 = 2 \times 2 \times 3 \times 13 \implies \mathbf{2^2 \times 3 \times 13}$
  • 3825: (Ends in 5, divisible by 5) $3825 = 5 \times 765 = 5 \times 5 \times 153 = 5 \times 5 \times 3 \times 51 = 5 \times 5 \times 3 \times 3 \times 17 \implies \mathbf{3^2 \times 5^2 \times 17}$
  • 5005: $5005 = 5 \times 1001 = 5 \times 7 \times 143 = 5 \times 7 \times 11 \times 13 \implies \mathbf{5 \times 7 \times 11 \times 13}$
  • 7429: (A tricky one, test larger primes) $7429 = 17 \times 437 = 17 \times 19 \times 23 \implies \mathbf{17 \times 19 \times 23}$

3.3 HCF by Prime Factorisation Method

Rule HCF Rule: The HCF is the product of the SMALLEST powers of each COMMON prime factor present in the numbers.
Examples (HCF):
1. Find HCF(6, 20): $6 = 2^1 \times 3^1$. $20 = 2^2 \times 5^1$. Common factor is 2. Smallest power is 1. HCF = 2.
2. Find HCF(96, 404): $96 = 2^5 \times 3^1$. $404 = 2^2 \times 101$. Common factor is 2. Smallest power is 2. HCF = $2^2$ = 4.
3. Find HCF(26, 91): $26 = 2 \times 13$. $91 = 7 \times 13$. Common factor is 13. HCF = 13.
4. Find HCF(12, 15, 21): $12 = 2^2 \times 3$. $15 = 3 \times 5$. $21 = 3 \times 7$. The only common prime factor is 3. HCF = 3.
5. Find HCF(8, 9, 25): $8=2^3$. $9=3^2$. $25=5^2$. There are NO common prime factors. Thus, the only common factor is 1. HCF = 1.

3.4 LCM by Prime Factorisation Method

Rule LCM Rule: The LCM is the product of the GREATEST powers of ALL prime factors involved in the numbers. (Take every prime number you see, with its highest power).
Examples (LCM):
1. Find LCM(6, 20): $6 = 2^1 \times 3^1$. $20 = 2^2 \times 5^1$. All primes: 2, 3, 5. Greatest powers: $2^2, 3^1, 5^1$. LCM = $4 \times 3 \times 5$ = 60.
2. Find LCM(96, 404): $96 = 2^5 \times 3^1$. $404 = 2^2 \times 101$. Greatest powers: $2^5, 3^1, 101^1$. LCM = $32 \times 3 \times 101$ = 9696.
3. Find LCM(26, 91): $26 = 2 \times 13$. $91 = 7 \times 13$. LCM = $2 \times 7 \times 13$ = 182.
4. Find LCM(12, 15, 21): $12 = 2^2 \times 3$. $15 = 3 \times 5$. $21 = 3 \times 7$. LCM = $2^2 \times 3 \times 5 \times 7$ = 420.
5. Find LCM(8, 9, 25): $8=2^3$. $9=3^2$. $25=5^2$. LCM = $2^3 \times 3^2 \times 5^2 = 8 \times 9 \times 25$ = 1800.

3.5 Relationship: HCF x LCM = Product of Two Numbers

Formula For any TWO positive integers $a$ and $b$:
$$ \text{HCF}(a, b) \times \text{LCM}(a, b) = a \times b $$ CRITICAL WARNING: This formula works strictly for TWO numbers only. It is FALSE for three numbers. $HCF(p,q,r) \times LCM(p,q,r) \neq p \times q \times r$.
Example 1: Verify the formula for 26 and 91.
We found $HCF = 13$, $LCM = 182$.
LHS: $HCF \times LCM = 13 \times 182 = 2366$.
RHS: $a \times b = 26 \times 91 = 2366$.
LHS = RHS. Verified.
Example 2: Given that $HCF(306, 657) = 9$, find $LCM(306, 657)$.
We know: $LCM \times HCF = a \times b$
$LCM \times 9 = 306 \times 657$
$LCM = \frac{306 \times 657}{9} = 34 \times 657 = \mathbf{22338}$.
Example 3: The LCM of two numbers is 182 and their HCF is 13. If one of the numbers is 26, find the other.
$HCF \times LCM = a \times b \implies 13 \times 182 = 26 \times b \implies b = \frac{13 \times 182}{26} = \frac{182}{2} = \mathbf{91}$.
Example 4: Can two numbers have 16 as their HCF and 380 as their LCM?
Reasoning: A crucial property is that the HCF must always be a perfect factor of the LCM. Let's check if 380 is perfectly divisible by 16.
$380 / 16 = 23.75$ (Not perfectly divisible).
No, they cannot, because HCF must be a factor of LCM.
Example 5: The product of two numbers is 3200 and their LCM is 400. Find their HCF.
$HCF = \frac{\text{Product}}{LCM} = \frac{3200}{400} = \mathbf{8}$.

3.6 Word Problems on HCF and LCM

Examiner's Tip: Use LCM when events repeat over time (meeting points, ringing bells) or when you need a number "divisible by" others. Use HCF when dividing items into identical maximum groups.

Problem 1 (Circular Track - LCM): There is a circular path around a sports field. Sonia takes 18 minutes to drive one round, while Ravi takes 12 minutes. Suppose they both start at the same point and at the same time, and go in the same direction. After how many minutes will they meet again at the starting point?
Solution: They will meet again after a time which is a multiple of both 18 and 12. We need the LCM(18, 12).
$18 = 2 \times 3^2$, $12 = 2^2 \times 3$
$LCM = 2^2 \times 3^2 = 4 \times 9 = 36$.
Answer: They will meet again after 36 minutes.
Problem 2 (Bells Ringing - LCM): Six bells commence tolling together and toll at intervals of 2, 4, 6, 8, 10 and 12 seconds respectively. In 30 minutes, how many times do they toll together?
Solution: Find LCM of 2, 4, 6, 8, 10, 12.
$LCM = 120$ seconds = $2$ minutes.
So, they toll together every 2 minutes.
In 30 minutes, they will toll $30/2 = 15$ times. BUT we must add 1 because they tolled together at the very start ($0$th minute).
Answer: 15 + 1 = 16 times.
Problem 3 (Smallest Number + Remainder - LCM): Find the smallest number which when divided by 28 and 32 leaves remainders 8 and 12 respectively.
Solution: Notice the difference between divisors and remainders: $28 - 8 = 20$ and $32 - 12 = 20$. The difference is constant (20).
Required number = $LCM(28, 32) - (\text{Constant Difference})$.
$LCM(28, 32)$: $28=2^2 \times 7$, $32=2^5$. $LCM = 2^5 \times 7 = 32 \times 7 = 224$.
Number = $224 - 20 = \mathbf{204}$.
Problem 4 (Distributing Items - HCF): Three sets of English, Hindi and Mathematics books have to be stacked such that all books are stored topic-wise and the height of each stack is the same. The number of books are 96, 240 and 336. Find the maximum height of the stack (number of books).
Solution: We need equal stacks of maximum size. Find HCF(96, 240, 336).
$96 = 2^5 \times 3$
$240 = 2^4 \times 3 \times 5$
$336 = 2^4 \times 3 \times 7$
$HCF = 2^4 \times 3 = 16 \times 3 = \mathbf{48 \text{ books per stack}}$.
Problem 5 (Morning Walk - LCM): Three persons step off together for a morning walk. Their steps measure 80 cm, 85 cm and 90 cm respectively. What is the minimum distance each should walk so that all can cover the same distance in complete steps?
Solution: Minimum distance = LCM of 80, 85, 90.
$80 = 2^4 \times 5$
$85 = 5 \times 17$
$90 = 2 \times 3^2 \times 5$
$LCM = 2^4 \times 3^2 \times 5 \times 17 = 16 \times 9 \times 5 \times 17 = \mathbf{12240 \text{ cm (or 122.4 m)}}$.

SECTION 4: IRRATIONAL NUMBERS - PROOFS

4.1 What is an Irrational Number (Detailed)

An irrational number cannot be written as a simple fraction $\frac{p}{q}$. On a number line, they occupy positions between rational numbers, but their exact decimal value goes on forever without repeating.
Algebra of Irrational Numbers:

  • Rational + Irrational = Irrational (e.g., $2 + \sqrt{3}$)
  • Rational - Irrational = Irrational (e.g., $5 - \sqrt{2}$)
  • Non-zero Rational $\times$ Irrational = Irrational (e.g., $3\sqrt{5}$)
  • Irrational + Irrational = Can be rational OR irrational (e.g., $(2+\sqrt{3}) + (2-\sqrt{3}) = 4$ Rational)
  • Irrational $\times$ Irrational = Can be rational OR irrational (e.g., $\sqrt{2} \times \sqrt{2} = 2$ Rational)

4.2 Method of Contradiction

This is the standard proof technique required in boards. It works like this:
1. Assume the exact opposite of what you want to prove (Assume the number is Rational).
2. Express it as $\frac{a}{b}$ where $a$ and $b$ are co-prime integers.
3. Perform mathematical operations that lead to a logical impossibility (a contradiction).
4. Conclude that the original assumption was wrong, therefore the number is Irrational.

Key Theorem Let $p$ be a prime number. If $p$ divides $a^2$, then $p$ divides $a$, where $a$ is a positive integer. (You do not need to prove this theorem, just state it during your $\sqrt{2}$ proof).

4.3 Proof that $\sqrt{2}$ is Irrational (Very Important)

Q: Prove that $\sqrt{2}$ is irrational.
Proof:
Step 1: Let us assume, to the contrary, that $\sqrt{2}$ is a rational number.
Then, there exist two co-prime integers $a$ and $b$ ($b \neq 0$) such that:
$\sqrt{2} = \frac{a}{b}$

Step 2: Squaring both sides, we get:
$2 = \frac{a^2}{b^2}$
$\implies 2b^2 = a^2 \quad \text{--- (Equation 1)}$

Step 3: From Eq 1, it is clear that 2 divides $a^2$.
By the theorem (if a prime $p$ divides $a^2$, then $p$ divides $a$), it follows that 2 divides $a$.

Step 4: Since 2 divides $a$, we can write $a$ as:
$a = 2c$ (for some integer $c$).
Substituting $a = 2c$ in Equation 1:
$2b^2 = (2c)^2$
$2b^2 = 4c^2$
$\implies b^2 = 2c^2$

Step 5: This means that 2 divides $b^2$, and consequently, 2 divides $b$.

Step 6: From Step 3 and Step 5, we conclude that both $a$ and $b$ have 2 as a common factor.
But this contradicts our initial assumption that $a$ and $b$ are co-prime (having no common factor other than 1).
This contradiction has arisen because of our incorrect assumption that $\sqrt{2}$ is rational.
Conclusion: Hence, $\sqrt{2}$ is an irrational number.

4.4 Proof that $\sqrt{3}$ is Irrational

Q: Prove that $\sqrt{3}$ is irrational.
Proof:
Assume $\sqrt{3} = \frac{a}{b}$ where $a, b$ are co-prime integers, $b \neq 0$.
Squaring both sides: $3 = \frac{a^2}{b^2} \implies 3b^2 = a^2$.
Thus, 3 divides $a^2$, which implies 3 divides $a$.
Let $a = 3c$ for some integer $c$. Substitute this back:
$3b^2 = (3c)^2 \implies 3b^2 = 9c^2 \implies b^2 = 3c^2$.
Thus, 3 divides $b^2$, which implies 3 divides $b$.
Therefore, $a$ and $b$ have at least 3 as a common factor, contradicting the fact they are co-prime.
Hence, $\sqrt{3}$ is irrational.

4.5 Proof that $\sqrt{5}$ is Irrational

Q: Prove that $\sqrt{5}$ is irrational.
Proof:
Assume $\sqrt{5} = \frac{a}{b}$ where $a, b$ are co-prime integers, $b \neq 0$.
Squaring both sides: $5 = \frac{a^2}{b^2} \implies 5b^2 = a^2$.
Thus, 5 divides $a^2$, implying 5 divides $a$.
Let $a = 5c$. Substitute this back:
$5b^2 = (5c)^2 \implies 5b^2 = 25c^2 \implies b^2 = 5c^2$.
Thus, 5 divides $b^2$, implying 5 divides $b$.
$a$ and $b$ have 5 as a common factor, contradicting they are co-prime.
Hence, $\sqrt{5}$ is irrational.

4.6 Proofs for Combinations of Irrationals

For these, you must isolate the root part. Assume the basic root ($\sqrt{2}, \sqrt{3}, \sqrt{5}$) is already proven irrational unless stated otherwise.

1. Prove $2\sqrt{3}$ is irrational.
Assume $2\sqrt{3} = \frac{a}{b}$ (where $a, b$ are co-prime, $b \neq 0$).
Isolate the root: $\sqrt{3} = \frac{a}{2b}$.
Since $a, b$ are integers, $\frac{a}{2b}$ is a rational number. This implies $\sqrt{3}$ is rational.
But this contradicts the fact that $\sqrt{3}$ is irrational.
Therefore, our assumption is wrong. $2\sqrt{3}$ is irrational.
2. Prove $3 + 2\sqrt{5}$ is irrational.
Assume $3 + 2\sqrt{5} = \frac{a}{b}$.
$2\sqrt{5} = \frac{a}{b} - 3 \implies 2\sqrt{5} = \frac{a - 3b}{b} \implies \sqrt{5} = \frac{a - 3b}{2b}$.
Since $a, b$ are integers, $\frac{a - 3b}{2b}$ is rational. So $\sqrt{5}$ is rational.
This contradicts the fact that $\sqrt{5}$ is irrational. Hence proved.
3. Prove $5 - \sqrt{3}$ is irrational.
Assume $5 - \sqrt{3} = \frac{a}{b}$.
Rearranging: $5 - \frac{a}{b} = \sqrt{3} \implies \sqrt{3} = \frac{5b - a}{b}$.
Since $a, b$ are integers, $\frac{5b - a}{b}$ is rational. So $\sqrt{3}$ is rational.
Contradiction. Hence $5 - \sqrt{3}$ is irrational.
4. Prove $\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}$ is irrational.
Assume $\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} = \frac{a}{b}$.
Invert both sides: $\sqrt{2} = \frac{b}{a}$.
Since $a, b$ are integers, $\frac{b}{a}$ is rational. So $\sqrt{2}$ is rational.
Contradiction. Hence $\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}$ is irrational.
5. Prove $\sqrt{2} + \sqrt{3}$ is irrational. (High Difficulty)
Assume $\sqrt{2} + \sqrt{3} = \frac{a}{b}$.
Isolate one root: $\sqrt{3} = \frac{a}{b} - \sqrt{2}$.
Squaring both sides:
$3 = \left(\frac{a}{b}\right)^2 + (\sqrt{2})^2 - 2\left(\frac{a}{b}\right)\sqrt{2}$
$3 = \frac{a^2}{b^2} + 2 - \frac{2a}{b}\sqrt{2}$
Rearrange to isolate $\sqrt{2}$:
$\frac{2a}{b}\sqrt{2} = \frac{a^2}{b^2} + 2 - 3 \implies \frac{2a}{b}\sqrt{2} = \frac{a^2 - b^2}{b^2}$
$\sqrt{2} = \frac{b(a^2 - b^2)}{2ab^2} \implies \sqrt{2} = \frac{a^2 - b^2}{2ab}$
Since $a,b$ are integers, the RHS is rational. Thus $\sqrt{2}$ is rational.
Contradiction! Hence $\sqrt{2} + \sqrt{3}$ is irrational.

(Follow the exact same isolation logic for $3\sqrt{2}, 5\sqrt{3}, \sqrt{5}-\sqrt{3}, 2-3\sqrt{5}, 7\sqrt{5}, \sqrt{2}+3$. The template is universally applicable.)

SECTION 5: DECIMAL EXPANSIONS OF RATIONAL NUMBERS

5.1 Types of Decimal Expansions

  • Terminating: The division ends after finite decimal places. e.g., $\frac{1}{2} = 0.5$, $\frac{7}{8} = 0.875$.
  • Non-Terminating Repeating: The division never ends, but a block of digits repeats. e.g., $\frac{1}{3} = 0.333\dots$, $\frac{1}{7} = 0.142857142857\dots$

5.2 Theorem for Terminating Decimals

Theorem Let $x = \frac{p}{q}$ be a rational number, such that $p$ and $q$ are co-prime (lowest terms). Then $x$ has a decimal expansion which terminates if and only if the prime factorisation of the denominator $q$ is of the form $2^n \times 5^m$, where $n, m$ are non-negative integers.

Explanation: If the denominator contains ONLY 2s, ONLY 5s, or BOTH 2s and 5s, it terminates. If ANY other prime number (like 3, 7, 11) is present in the denominator, it will be Non-Terminating Repeating.

5.4 Without Actual Division - Determine Nature of Decimal

Problem 1: $\frac{13}{3125}$
Denominator $q = 3125 = 5 \times 5 \times 5 \times 5 \times 5 = 5^5$.
Since $q$ is of the form $2^0 \times 5^5$, the decimal expansion is Terminating.
Problem 2: $\frac{17}{8}$
Denominator $q = 8 = 2^3$. Form is $2^3 \times 5^0$. Terminating.
Problem 3: $\frac{64}{455}$
Denominator $q = 455 = 5 \times 7 \times 13$.
Since $q$ has prime factors 7 and 13 (other than 2 and 5), the expansion is Non-Terminating Repeating.
Problem 4: $\frac{77}{210}$
Common Mistake Check: Simplify fraction FIRST! $\frac{77}{210} = \frac{11}{30}$.
Denominator $q = 30 = 2 \times 3 \times 5$.
Because of the prime factor 3, it is Non-Terminating Repeating.
Problem 5: $\frac{129}{2^2 \times 5^7 \times 7^5}$
Denominator clearly contains $7^5$. It is Non-Terminating Repeating.

5.5 Number of Decimal Places in Terminating Decimals

If $q = 2^n \times 5^m$, the number of decimal places before it terminates is the maximum of $n$ and $m$.

Q: After how many places will $\frac{43}{2^4 \times 5^3}$ terminate?
Max power between 4 and 3 is 4. It terminates after 4 decimal places.

SECTION 6: IMPORTANT THEOREMS - SUMMARY

  • Fundamental Theorem of Arithmetic: Every composite number can be uniquely expressed as a product of primes, ignoring order.
  • HCF $\times$ LCM relation: $HCF(a,b) \times LCM(a,b) = a \times b$.
  • Rational Decimal Theorem: $\frac{p}{q}$ (lowest terms) terminates if $q=2^n5^m$; otherwise, it is non-terminating repeating.
  • Irrationality Base Theorem: If prime $p$ divides $a^2$, then $p$ divides $a$.

4. The product of a non-zero rational and an irrational number is:
(A) always rational    (B) always irrational    (C) rational or irrational    (D) one
Answer: (B) always irrational

7.2 Very Short Answer (2 Marks)

Q1. Given that HCF(252, 594) = 18, find LCM(252, 594).
Sol: $LCM = (252 \times 594) / 18 = 8316$.
Q2. Check whether $6^n$ can end with the digit 0 for any natural number $n$.
Sol: $6^n = (2 \times 3)^n = 2^n \times 3^n$. For a number to end with 0, its prime factorization must contain both 2 and 5. Since 5 is missing, it can never end with 0.

7.3 Short Answer (3 Marks)

Q1. Prove that $2 + 5\sqrt{3}$ is an irrational number, given that $\sqrt{3}$ is irrational.
Sol: Assume $2 + 5\sqrt{3} = \frac{a}{b}$. Then $5\sqrt{3} = \frac{a}{b} - 2 = \frac{a-2b}{b} \implies \sqrt{3} = \frac{a-2b}{5b}$. Since $a,b$ are integers, the RHS is rational. This implies $\sqrt{3}$ is rational, contradicting the given fact. Hence, $2+5\sqrt{3}$ is irrational.
Q2. Use Euclid's division algorithm to find HCF of 10224 and 9648.
Sol: $10224 = 9648 \times 1 + 576$
$9648 = 576 \times 16 + 432$
$576 = 432 \times 1 + 144$
$432 = 144 \times 3 + 0$. Remainder is zero, HCF is 144.

7.4 Assertion-Reason Questions

Options: (A) Both true, R is correct explanation. (B) Both true, R is NOT correct explanation. (C) A is true, R is false. (D) A is false, R is true.

Q1. Assertion (A): The HCF of two numbers is 5 and their product is 150. Their LCM is 30.
Reason (R): For any two positive integers $a$ and $b$, $HCF(a,b) \times LCM(a,b) = a \times b$.
Answer: (A) Both are true and R perfectly explains A ($5 \times 30 = 150$).

SECTION 8: QUICK REVISION SHEET

8.1 Formulas & Concepts at a Glance

  • $HCF(a,b) \times LCM(a,b) = a \times b$
  • Terminating decimal $\iff q = 2^n \times 5^m$
  • Non-Terminating repeating decimal $\iff q$ has prime factors other than 2 or 5.

8.2 Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Applying the Formula to 3 numbers: Never use $LCM \times HCF = p \times q \times r$.
  • Forgetting to simplify fractions: Before checking for terminating decimals, you MUST cut the fraction to its lowest terms. ($\frac{6}{15}$ looks non-terminating because of 15, but simplifies to $\frac{2}{5}$ which is terminating).
  • Incomplete Proofs: In $\sqrt{2}$ proof, forgetting to state "a and b are co-prime" at the start will cost you marks, because the entire contradiction relies on that statement.

8.3 Examiner's Tips for Full Marks

In word problems, explicitly state why you are choosing HCF or LCM. For example: "Since we need the maximum number of columns, we will find the Highest Common Factor (HCF) of 616 and 32." This guarantees full conceptual marks.
