Real Numbers

Class 10 Mathematics • Chapter 01 (Rationalized Syllabus)

1. Introduction to Real Numbers

Real Numbers ($\mathbb{R}$): The set of all Rational ($\mathbb{Q}$) and Irrational numbers ($\mathbb{Q}'$) combined together form the set of Real Numbers.

  • Natural Numbers ($\mathbb{N}$): $1, 2, 3, \dots$
  • Whole Numbers ($\mathbb{W}$): $0, 1, 2, 3, \dots$
  • Integers ($\mathbb{Z}$): $\dots, -2, -1, 0, 1, 2, \dots$
MASTER TIP Recall that every integer is a rational number because it can be written as $\frac{p}{1}$, where $p$ is an integer and $1 \neq 0$.

2. The Fundamental Theorem of Arithmetic

This theorem essentially tells us that every composite number has a unique "fingerprint" made of prime numbers.

Theorem Statement

Every composite number can be expressed (factorized) as a product of primes, and this factorization is unique, apart from the order in which the prime factors occur.

For example, let's factorize $140$:

$140 = 2 \times 70$
$= 2 \times 2 \times 35$
$= 2 \times 2 \times 5 \times 7$
$= 2^2 \times 5 \times 7$

No matter how you start splitting it, you will always end up with two 2s, one 5, and one 7.

HCF and LCM Relation

For any two positive integers $a$ and $b$:

FORMULA $$ \text{HCF}(a, b) \times \text{LCM}(a, b) = a \times b $$

Definitions:

  • HCF (Highest Common Factor): Product of the smallest power of each common prime factor.
  • LCM (Least Common Multiple): Product of the greatest power of each prime factor, involved in the numbers.

3. Revisiting Irrational Numbers

An irrational number is a number that cannot be written in the form $\frac{p}{q}$, where $p$ and $q$ are integers and $q \neq 0$. Their decimal expansions are non-terminating and non-recurring.

Examples: $\sqrt{2}, \sqrt{3}, \pi$.

Important Theorem

Let $p$ be a prime number. If $p$ divides $a^2$, then $p$ divides $a$, where $a$ is a positive integer.

Proof Strategy: Proving $\sqrt{2}$ is Irrational

We use the method of Contradiction.

  1. Assume $\sqrt{2}$ is rational. So, $\sqrt{2} = \frac{a}{b}$ where $a, b$ are coprime integers.
  2. Squares both sides: $2 = \frac{a^2}{b^2} \Rightarrow 2b^2 = a^2$.
  3. This means $2$ divides $a^2$, so by our theorem, $2$ divides $a$. Let $a = 2c$.
  4. Substitute $a=2c$: $2b^2 = (2c)^2 \Rightarrow 2b^2 = 4c^2 \Rightarrow b^2 = 2c^2$.
  5. This means $2$ divides $b^2$, so $2$ divides $b$.
  6. Contradiction: We showed $2$ divides both $a$ and $b$. But we assumed $a$ and $b$ were coprime (no common factor).
  7. Therefore, our assumption was wrong. $\sqrt{2}$ is irrational.
MASTER TIP This same logic applies to $\sqrt{3}, \sqrt{5}$, etc. Just replace the number 2 with the respective prime.

4. Operations on Rationals & Irrationals

  • The sum or difference of a rational and an irrational number is irrational.
  • The product or quotient of a non-zero rational and an irrational number is irrational.
  • Example: Since $\sqrt{3}$ is irrational, $5 - \sqrt{3}$ is also irrational.

5. Important Exam Questions (PYQ Trends)

Based on CBSE Previous Year Papers and reference books (RD Sharma, RS Aggarwal), these types of questions are strictly part of the syllabus.

Type 1: Proving Reciprocals are Irrational

Question: Prove that $\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}$ is irrational.

Proof:

  1. Assume, to the contrary, that $\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}$ is rational.
  2. That is, we can find coprime integers $a$ and $b$ ($b \neq 0$) such that $\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} = \frac{a}{b}$.
  3. Rearranging, we get $\sqrt{2} = \frac{b}{a}$.
  4. Since $a$ and $b$ are integers, $\frac{b}{a}$ is rational.
  5. This implies $\sqrt{2}$ is rational.
  6. But this contradicts the fact that $\sqrt{2}$ is irrational.
  7. So, our assumption is incorrect. Therefore, $\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}$ is irrational.

Type 2: Proving Products are Irrational

Question: Prove that $7\sqrt{5}$ is irrational.

Proof:

  1. Let us assume, to the contrary, that $7\sqrt{5}$ is rational.
  2. Then we can find coprime integers $a$ and $b$ ($b \neq 0$) such that $7\sqrt{5} = \frac{a}{b}$.
  3. Rearranging, we get $\sqrt{5} = \frac{a}{7b}$.
  4. Since $a$ and $b$ are integers, $\frac{a}{7b}$ is rational.
  5. This implies $\sqrt{5}$ is rational.
  6. But this contradicts the fact that $\sqrt{5}$ is irrational.
  7. So, our assumption is incorrect. Hence, $7\sqrt{5}$ is irrational.

Type 3: Proving Sums are Irrational

Question: Prove that $6 + \sqrt{2}$ is irrational.

Proof:

  1. Assume $6 + \sqrt{2}$ is rational.
  2. So, $6 + \sqrt{2} = \frac{a}{b}$ where $a, b$ are coprime integers.
  3. Rearranging: $\sqrt{2} = \frac{a}{b} - 6$.
  4. $\sqrt{2} = \frac{a - 6b}{b}$.
  5. Since $a$ and $b$ are integers, $\frac{a - 6b}{b}$ is rational.
  6. Therefore, $\sqrt{2}$ is rational.
  7. This contradicts the proved fact that $\sqrt{2}$ is irrational.
  8. Hence, $6 + \sqrt{2}$ is irrational.
EXAM TRICK For questions involving sums or products (like $5-\sqrt{3}$ or $3\sqrt{2}$), you don't need to prove $\sqrt{3}$ or $\sqrt{2}$ is irrational from scratch unless the question explicitly asks or carries high marks (4-5 marks). Usually, you can state "We know that $\sqrt{p}$ is irrational..." and proceed to find the contradiction.
