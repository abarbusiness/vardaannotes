The Rise of Nationalism in Europe

Class 10 History • Chapter 01

1. Frederic Sorrieu's Vision (1848)

A French artist who prepared a series of 4 prints visualizing his dream of a world made up of 'Democratic and Social Republics'.

Key Features of the First Print:

  • Peoples of Europe and America (men/women, all ages/classes) marching in a long train.
  • Offering homage to the Statue of Liberty (Torch of Enlightenment + Charter of Rights of Man).
  • Shattered remains of Absolutist institutions (Monarchy) on the ground.

Utopian Vision: Ideally perfect society that is unlikely to exist.

2. The French Revolution (1789)

The first clear expression of nationalism.

Steps taken to create Collective Identity:

  • La Patrie (the fatherland) and Le Citoyen (the citizen).
  • New French Flag (Tricolour) replaced the Royal Standard.
  • Estates General renamed as National Assembly (elected by active citizens).
  • New hymns composed, oaths taken, martyrs commemorated.
  • Centralized administrative system and uniform laws for all.
  • Internal customs duties abolished. Uniform weights and measures.
  • French became the common language.

3. Napoleon Bonaparte (1799-1815)

Ruled France and conquered parts of Europe. He destroyed democracy but revolutionized administration.

Civil Code of 1804 (Napoleonic Code):

  • Did away with all privileges based on birth.
  • Established equality before the law.
  • Secured the right to property.
  • Abolished feudal system and freed peasants from serfdom.
  • Guild restrictions removed in towns.
  • Transport and communication systems improved.

Reaction: Initially viewed as "Harbingers of Liberty" (Holland, Switzerland). Soon turned to hostility due to: Increased taxation, Censorship, Forced conscription into French armies.

4. The Making of Nationalism in Europe

Mid-18th century Europe had no 'nation-states'. (e.g., Habsburg Empire was a patchwork).

4.1 The Aristocracy and the New Middle Class

  • Aristocracy: Dominant class, owned estates, spoke French, connected by marriage ties. Numerically small.
  • Peasantry: Majority of population.
  • Middle Class (Liberal Bourgeoisie): Emerged due to Industrialization. Industrialists, businessmen, professionals. They wanted national unity and abolition of aristocratic privileges.

4.2 What did Liberal Nationalism Stand for?

  • Political: Concept of Govt by consent. End of autocracy. Constiution and Representative govt. (Note: Did not necessarily mean Universal Suffrage; women/non-propertied men were excluded).
  • Economic: Freedom of markets. Abolition of state-imposed restrictions on movement of goods/capital.
Zollverein (1834): A customs union formed at the initiative of Prussia.
  • Abolished tariff barriers.
  • Reduced number of currencies from 30 to 2.
  • Created a network of railways to stimulate mobility.

4.3 New Conservatism after 1815

After Napoleon's defeat (Battle of Waterloo), European powers (Britain, Russia, Prussia, Austria) wanted to restore Monarchy and Tradition.

TREATY OF VIENNA (1815) Host: Duke Metternich (Austrian Chancellor).
Decisions: 1. Bourbon dynasty restored to power in France.
2. France lost territories annexed under Napoleon.
3. Series of states set up on boundaries of France (like Netherlands, Piedmont, Prussia) to prevent expansion.
4. Main Goal: Restore monarchies overthrown by Napoleon.

4.4 The Revolutionaries

Secret societies formed to oppose monarchies.
Giuseppe Mazzini: Italian revolutionary. Member of Carbonari. Founded Young Italy (Marseilles) and Young Europe (Berne). Believed God intended nations to be natural units of mankind.
Metternich described him as "The most dangerous enemy of our social order".

5. The Age of Revolutions (1830-1848)

  • July Revolution (France 1830): Bourbon Kings overthrown. Louis Philippe installed as constitutional monarch.
    "When France sneezes, the rest of Europe catches cold" - Metternich.
  • Greek War of Independence: Struggle against Ottoman Empire since 1821. Treaty of Constantinople (1832) recognized Greece as an independent nation.
  • Frankfurt Parliament (1848): Creating a German nation. 831 elected representatives marched to St. Paul Church. Drafted constitution for German nation headed by monarchy subject to parliament. King of Prussia (Friedrich Wilhelm IV) rejected it.

6. The Making of Germany and Italy

A. Unification of Germany

  • Architect: Otto Von Bismarck (Prussian Chief Minister).
  • Method: "Blood and Iron". Three wars over 7 years (with Austria, Denmark, France) ended in Prussian victory.
  • Result: Kaiser William I proclaimed German Emperor (Jan 1871) at Versailles.

B. Unification of Italy

  • Italy was divided into 7 states. Only Sardinia-Piedmont ruled by Italian Princely House.
  • Giuseppe Mazzini: The soul. Formed Young Italy.
  • Count Cavour: The brain. CM of Sardinia-Piedmont. Tactful diplomat. Defeated Austria.
  • Giuseppe Garibaldi: The sword. Leader of 'Red Shirts'. Marched to South Italy (Two Sicilies) and won peasant support.
  • Result: Victor Emmanuel II proclaimed king of United Italy (1861).

C. The Strange Case of Britain

No sudden revolution. A long drawn-out process.

  • Primary identities were ethnic (English, Welsh, Scot, Irish).
  • Act of Union (1707): Between England and Scotland. Formation of 'United Kingdom of Great Britain'. Scotland repressed.
  • Ireland: Deeply divided (Catholic vs Protestant). English helped Protestants to dominate. Ireland forcibly incorporated into UK in 1801.
  • Symbols: British Flag (Union Jack), Anthem (God Save Our Noble King), English Language.

7. Visualizing the Nation (Allegory)

Artists used female figures to portray the nation (Allegory).

  • France: Marianne (Red cap, tricolour, cockade). Symbol of Liberty and Republic.
  • Germany: Germania (Wears crown of oak leaves). Oak stands for heroism.

8. Nationalism and Imperialism (The Balkans)

Balkans: Region of geographical/ethnic variation (Romania, Bulgaria, Albania, Greece, etc.). Inhabitants called Slavs.
Controlled by Ottoman Empire.
Conflict: As Ottoman Empire collapsed, nationalities broke away for independence. Big Powers (Russia, Germany, England, Austro-Hungary) jumped in to extend control. This led to series of wars and finally **World War I (1914)**.

IMPORTANT DATES
  • 1789: French Revolution.
  • 1804: Napoleonic Code.
  • 1815: Battle of Waterloo / Treaty of Vienna.
  • 1834: Zollverein formed.
  • 1848: Revolutions in Europe / Frankfurt Parliament.
  • 1861: Unification of Italy.
  • 1871: Unification of Germany.
