1. Reflection of Light

Reflection is the bouncing back of light from a polished surface.

Laws of Reflection:

  • The angle of incidence equals the angle of reflection (âˆ i = âˆ r).
  • The incident ray, reflected ray, and the normal all lie in the same plane.
Plane Mirror Image: Always virtual, erect, same size as the object, and laterally inverted.

2. Spherical Mirrors

These are mirrors with curved reflecting surfaces.

  • Concave Mirror: Reflecting surface is curved inwards. It's a converging mirror.
  • Convex Mirror: Reflecting surface is curved outwards. It's a diverging mirror.

Key Terms:

  • Pole (P): Center of the mirror's surface.
  • Center of Curvature (C): Center of the sphere of which the mirror is a part.
  • Principal Focus (F): The point where parallel rays meet (concave) or appear to diverge from (convex) after reflection.
  • Focal Length (f): Distance between Pole (P) and Focus (F).
Relationship: f = R/2

Image Formation by Concave Mirror

The image type depends on the object's position. This table is crucial.

Position of Object Position of Image Size of Image Nature of Image
At Infinity At Focus (F) Point-sized Real & Inverted
Beyond C Between F and C Diminished Real & Inverted
At C At C Same size Real & Inverted
Between C and F Beyond C Enlarged Real & Inverted
At Focus (F) At Infinity Highly enlarged Real & Inverted
Between P and F Behind mirror Enlarged Virtual & Erect

Image Formation by Convex Mirror

A convex mirror always forms a virtual, erect, and diminished image.

Uses

  • Concave Mirrors: Shaving mirrors, dentist's mirrors, torches, and solar furnaces.
  • Convex Mirrors: Rear-view mirrors in vehicles because they provide an erect image and a wider field of view.

3. Mirror Formula & Sign Convention

To solve numerical problems, follow the New Cartesian Sign Convention.

  • All distances are measured from the Pole (P).
  • Distances to the left of the mirror are negative (-); distances to the right are positive (+).
  • Height upwards is positive (+); height downwards is negative (-).
Quick Reference:
  • Object distance (u) is always negative.
  • Focal length (f) is negative for concave and positive for convex.
Mirror Formula: 1/v + 1/u = 1/f

Magnification (m):

m = h'/h = -v/u
  • If m is negative, the image is real & inverted.
  • If m is positive, the image is virtual & erect.

4. Refraction of Light

Refraction is the bending of light as it passes from one medium to another. It's caused by the change in the speed of light.

  • When light goes from a rarer to a denser medium, it bends towards the normal.
  • When light goes from a denser to a rarer medium, it bends away from the normal.

Laws of Refraction:

  • The incident ray, refracted ray, and normal all lie in the same plane.
  • Snell's Law: sin i / sin r = constant (n), where 'n' is the refractive index.

Refractive Index (n): A measure of how much a medium bends light.

n = Speed of light in vacuum (c) / Speed of light in medium (v)

5. Spherical Lenses

A lens is a transparent material with one or two spherical surfaces.

  • Convex Lens: Thicker at the center; it's a converging lens.
  • Concave Lens: Thinner at the center; it's a diverging lens.

Image Formation

  • Convex Lens: Forms both real and virtual images depending on the object's position (similar to a concave mirror).
  • Concave Lens: Always forms a virtual, erect, and diminished image.

Uses

  • Convex Lenses: Magnifying glasses, spectacles (hypermetropia), cameras, microscopes, telescopes.
  • Concave Lenses: Spectacles (myopia), wide-angle spy holes.

Lens Formula & Power

The sign convention is the same, but distances are measured from the Optical Centre (O).

Quick Reference:
  • Focal length (f) is positive for convex and negative for concave.
Lens Formula: 1/v - 1/u = 1/f
Magnification (m): m = h'/h = v/u

Power of a Lens (P): The ability of a lens to converge or diverge light.

P = 1 / f (where f is in meters)
The SI unit of power is the Dioptre (D).
