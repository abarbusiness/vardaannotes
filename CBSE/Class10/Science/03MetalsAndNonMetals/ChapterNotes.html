1. Physical Properties

Metals ðŸ”©

  • Generally lustrous (shiny), hard, malleable (can be beaten into sheets), and ductile (can be drawn into wires).
  • Good conductors of heat and electricity.
  • Sonorous (make a sound when hit).
  • Usually solid at room temperature.
Exceptions: Mercury (liquid), Sodium/Potassium (soft), Gallium/Caesium (low melting point).

Non-metals ðŸ’¨

  • Generally non-lustrous, brittle, and soft.
  • Poor conductors of heat and electricity.
  • Exist as solids or gases.
Exceptions: Iodine (lustrous), Diamond (hardest natural substance), Graphite (conducts electricity), Bromine (liquid).

3. Ionic Compounds

Formed by the transfer of electrons from a metal (forms cation +) to a non-metal (forms anion âˆ’).

The oppositely charged ions are held by strong electrostatic forces, forming an ionic bond.

Properties:
  • Solid, hard, and brittle.
  • High melting and boiling points.
  • Generally soluble in water.
  • Conduct electricity only in molten state or in solution, not as solids.

4. Extraction of Metals (Metallurgy)

  • Minerals: Naturally occurring elements/compounds in the earth's crust.
  • Ores: Minerals from which metals can be extracted profitably.
  • Gangue: Impurities mixed with the ore.

Extraction based on Reactivity:

Low Reactivity Metals (Ag, Au): Found in free state. Oxides are reduced by heating alone.

  • Reduction: Reducing the metal oxide to metal using Carbon (coke) or a more reactive metal (Thermit reaction).
    ZnO(s) + C(s) → Zn(s) + CO(g)

High Reactivity Metals (K, Na, Ca, Al): Cannot be reduced by carbon. They are extracted by electrolysis of their molten ores.

Refining

The final step to obtain pure metal is refining. The most common method is electrolytic refining.

Setup:
  • Impure metal is the anode.
  • A strip of pure metal is the cathode.
  • A salt solution of the metal is the electrolyte.

5. Corrosion

Corrosion is the gradual destruction of metals by the action of air, moisture, or chemicals.

  • Rusting of Iron: Requires both oxygen and water.
  • Tarnishing of Silver: Forms black silver sulphide (Agâ‚‚S).
  • Corrosion of Copper: Forms a green layer of copper carbonate.

Prevention:

  • Painting, oiling, greasing.
  • Galvanizing: Coating with a layer of zinc.
  • Alloying: Mixing a metal with other metals or non-metals (e.g., Stainless Steel).
