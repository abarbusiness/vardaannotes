1. Introduction to Reproduction

Reproduction is a fundamental process for the continuation of life. Unlike processes such as nutrition or respiration, it is not essential for an individual organism's survival. Its primary purpose is to create new individuals of the same species, ensuring the species does not die out.

At the most basic level, reproduction involves making copies of the blueprints of body design, which are stored in the form of DNA (Deoxyribo Nucleic Acid) inside the chromosomes of a cell's nucleus.

2. DNA Copying and the Importance of Variation

  • The Core Process: The fundamental event in reproduction is the creation of a DNA copy. The cell then divides, creating two new cells.
  • Creating Variation: No biochemical reaction is perfectly accurate, so the process of copying DNA results in some variations each time. The resulting DNA copies are similar, but not identical, to the original.
  • Survival of the Species: This variation is crucial for the survival of species over time. If a species' environment (niche) changes drastically, individuals with certain variations might be able to survive and continue the population.
    Example: If water temperature rises due to global warming, most bacteria might die, but a few heat-resistant variants could survive and multiply.

3. Modes of Asexual Reproduction

Asexual reproduction is the creation of a new generation from a single individual.

1. Fission

In unicellular organisms, fission (cell division) leads to the creation of new individuals.

  • Binary Fission: The parent cell splits into two equal halves. Common in bacteria and protozoa. In Amoeba, this splitting can happen in any plane.
  • Multiple Fission: The parent cell divides into many daughter cells simultaneously. Seen in the malarial parasite, Plasmodium.

2. Fragmentation

In some multicellular organisms with simple body structures, the organism breaks up into smaller pieces upon maturation. Each piece, or fragment, grows into a new individual.

Example: Spirogyra.

3. Regeneration

Many organisms can give rise to new individuals from their body parts. If the individual is cut or broken into pieces, each piece can grow into a complete organism. This is carried out by specialized cells that multiply and differentiate.

Example: Planaria.

4. Budding

An outgrowth, or bud, develops on the parent's body due to repeated cell division at one specific site. This bud grows into a tiny individual and, when mature, detaches to become independent.

Example: Hydra.

5. Vegetative Propagation

In many plants, parts like the root, stem, and leaves can develop into new plants under appropriate conditions.

Advantages:

  • Plants grown this way can bear flowers and fruits earlier than those from seeds.
  • It enables the propagation of plants that have lost the capacity to produce seeds, like bananas and roses.
  • All new plants are genetically similar to the parent plant.
Example: The leaves of Bryophyllum have buds in the notches of their margins. If a leaf falls on moist soil, these buds can grow into new plants.

6. Spore Formation

In the bread mould Rhizopus, tiny blob-on-a-stick structures called sporangia contain cells called spores. These spores are covered by thick walls that protect them until they land on a moist surface and can begin to grow.

4. Sexual Reproduction in Flowering Plants üå∫

Sexual reproduction involves two individuals. Its main advantage is that it allows for more variation by combining DNA from two different parents, increasing survival chances.

The reproductive parts of a flowering plant are located in the flower.

  • Unisexual flowers: Contain either stamens (male) or pistil (female). Examples: Papaya, watermelon.
  • Bisexual flowers: Contain both stamens and pistil. Examples: Hibiscus, mustard.

Parts of a Flower:

  • Stamen: Male reproductive part. Produces yellowish pollen grains in the anther.
  • Pistil: Female reproductive part, found in the center. It has three parts:
    • Stigma: Sticky top part that receives pollen.
    • Style: Middle elongated part.
    • Ovary: Swollen bottom part containing ovules. Each ovule has an egg cell.

The Process:

  1. Pollination: Transfer of pollen from anther to stigma.
  2. Fertilization: After pollen lands on stigma, a tube grows to the ovary. Male germ-cell fuses with female gamete in the ovule to form a zygote.
  3. Post-Fertilization: Zygote develops into an embryo. Ovule develops a tough coat and becomes a seed. Ovary ripens to form a fruit.

5. Reproduction in Human Beings üë∂

Humans use a sexual mode of reproduction. The period of sexual maturation during teenage years is called puberty.

Male Reproductive System

  • Testes: Located in scrotum, produce sperm (male germ-cells) and hormone testosterone.
  • Vas Deferens: Tube that transports sperm.
  • Glands (Seminal vesicles & prostate): Add fluid to sperm for nutrition and easier transport.
  • Urethra: Common passage for sperm and urine.
  • Penis: External organ for transfer of sperm.

Female Reproductive System

  • Ovaries: Produce eggs (female germ-cells). At birth, ovaries contain thousands of immature eggs.
  • Oviduct (Fallopian Tube): Carries egg from ovary to uterus. Fertilization happens here.
  • Uterus (Womb): Bag-like structure where embryo implants and develops.
  • Vagina: Receives sperm during intercourse.

Fertilization and Menstruation

  • If Fertilization Occurs: Fertilized egg (zygote) implants in uterus lining and develops into an embryo. Embryo gets nutrition from mother's blood via the placenta.
  • If Fertilization Does Not Occur: Egg lives for about one day. The uterus lining breaks down and is released through vagina as blood and mucous. This monthly cycle is menstruation.

6. Reproductive Health üè•

Sexually Transmitted Diseases (STDs)

Diseases like gonorrhea, syphilis, warts, and HIV-AIDS can be transmitted during sexual contact. Using a condom can help prevent transmission.

Contraception

Methods to avoid pregnancy include:

  • Mechanical Barriers: Condoms or coverings worn in the vagina.
  • Hormonal Pills: Change hormonal balance so eggs are not released.
  • Intrauterine Devices (IUDs): Devices like Copper-T placed in uterus to prevent pregnancy.
  • Surgical Methods: Blocking vas deferens in males (vasectomy) or fallopian tube in females (tubectomy).
