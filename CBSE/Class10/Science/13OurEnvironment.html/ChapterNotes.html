13.1 Eco-System: What Are Its Components?

An ecosystem is a system formed by all the living organisms in an area interacting with each other, along with the non-living (physical) parts of the environment. In an ecosystem, all these parts work together to maintain a balance in nature.

1.1 Components of an Ecosystem

An ecosystem is made of two main types of components:

  • Biotic Components: These are all the living organisms in an ecosystem.
    Examples include plants, animals, microorganisms, and human beings.
    Example: In a garden ecosystem, biotic components would be grasses, trees, flowering plants, frogs, insects, birds.
  • Abiotic Components: These are all the non-living physical factors of the environment.
    Examples include temperature, rainfall, wind, soil, and minerals.
    The growth, reproduction, and other activities of living organisms are affected by these abiotic components.

1.2 Types of Ecosystems

  • Natural Ecosystems: Exist on their own in nature.
    Examples: Forests, ponds, lakes.
  • Human-made (Artificial) Ecosystems: Created by humans.
    Examples: Gardens, crop-fields, aquariums.
Aquarium Ecosystem: An aquarium needs space (jar), water, oxygen, and food. If aquatic plants and animals are added, it can become a self-sustaining system. Unlike natural ponds, it must be cleaned regularly.

1.3 Groups of Organisms (Trophic Levels)

Organisms are grouped based on the way they get their food (sustenance) from the environment.

  1. Producers: Produce their own food (organic compounds like sugar/starch) from simple inorganic substances using photosynthesis.
    Examples: All green plants and certain bacteria.
  2. Consumers: Depend on producers for food, directly or indirectly. Classified as herbivores, carnivores, omnivores, and parasites.
  3. Decomposers: Microorganisms (bacteria, fungi) that break down dead remains and waste products.
    Role: They break down complex organic substances into simple inorganic substances that go back into the soil for plants to use again (natural replenishment).

13.1.1 Food Chains and Webs

2.1 Food Chains

A food chain is a series of organisms that feed on one another.

Each step or level in a food chain is called a trophic level.

2.2 Trophic Levels

  • First Trophic Level: Producers (Autotrophs). They "fix" solar energy and make it available for consumers.
  • Second Trophic Level: Primary Consumers (Herbivores). Eat producers.
  • Third Trophic Level: Secondary Consumers (Small Carnivores). Eat primary consumers.
  • Fourth Trophic Level: Tertiary Consumers (Larger Carnivores). Eat secondary consumers.

2.3 Flow of Energy

The interactions in an ecosystem involve a flow of energy from one component to another.

Energy flows from producers â†’ consumers â†’ decomposers.

Energy Loss: When energy changes form (e.g., sunlight to food, or food to body heat), some is lost to the environment as heat and cannot be used again.

The 1% Rule: Green plants capture only about 1% of the sunlight falling on their leaves and convert it into food energy.
The 10% Law:

When an organism is eaten, a large amount of energy is lost (heat, digestion, work). Only an average of 10% of the food eaten is turned into its own body (biomass) and made available for the next level of consumers.

Result: Food chains usually have only 3 or 4 steps because very little usable energy remains after the fourth level.

2.5 Key Features of Energy Flow

  • Unidirectional (One-Way): Energy captured by producers does not return to the sun. Energy passed to herbivores does not return to producers.
  • Decreases at Each Level: Available energy gets smaller and smaller as you go up trophic levels.

2.4 Food Webs

In nature, relationships are complex. Each organism is eaten by multiple others. This network of branching, interconnected food chains is called a food web.

2.6 Biological Magnification

This describes how certain harmful chemicals (like pesticides) enter our bodies.

Process:
  1. Pesticides used on crops wash into soil/water.
  2. Plants absorb them from soil/water.
  3. Chemicals enter the food chain. Since they are not degradable, they accumulate at each trophic level.
  4. Result: As humans occupy the top level, the maximum concentration accumulates in our bodies. This is biological magnification.

3.1 Ozone Layer and How It Is Getting Depleted

What is Ozone?

Ozone (O3) is a molecule made of three oxygen atoms. While O2 is essential for life, O3 is poisonous near the ground.

Function

High in the atmosphere, it protects Earth from harmful UV radiation from the Sun, which can cause skin cancer in humans.

Formation of Ozone

Formed naturally when UV radiation reacts with oxygen:

Depletion & Protection

  • Action: In 1987, UNEP created an agreement to freeze CFC production. It is now compulsory to manufacture CFC-free appliances.

3.2 Managing the Garbage We Produce

Biodegradable vs. Non-biodegradable

  • Biodegradable: Substances that can be broken down by biological processes (decomposers).
    Examples: Kitchen waste, paper, cotton.
  • Non-biodegradable: Substances that cannot be broken down by biological processes.
    Examples: Plastics, glass. They persist in the environment for a long time.

Case Study: Disposable Cups in Trains

  • Past: Plastic glasses were used and reused.
  • Change 1: Disposable plastic cups introduced for hygiene. Problem: Environmental impact of waste.
  • Change 2: Kulhads (clay cups) suggested. Problem: Loss of fertile top-soil to make them.
  • Current: Disposable paper cups are used as a better alternative.
