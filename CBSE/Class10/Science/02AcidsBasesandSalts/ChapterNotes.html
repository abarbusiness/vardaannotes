1. Introduction to Acids and Bases

  • Acids: Substances that are sour in taste and turn blue litmus paper red.
  • Bases: Substances that are bitter in taste and turn red litmus paper blue.

Indicators

Indicators are substances that change their color or odor when added to an acidic or a basic solution.

Natural Indicators:

  • Litmus: A purple dye extracted from lichen. It turns red in acidic solutions and blue in basic solutions.
  • Turmeric: A yellow spice that turns reddish-brown in basic solutions.

Synthetic Indicators:

  • Methyl Orange: Turns red in acidic solutions and yellow in basic solutions.
  • Phenolphthalein: Colorless in acidic solutions and pink in basic solutions.

Olfactory Indicators: These are substances whose odor changes in acidic or basic media. Examples include onion, vanilla, and clove oil.

4. Strength of Acid or Base Solutions

The strength of an acid or a base depends on the concentration of H‚ÇÉO‚Å∫ or OH‚Åª ions in the solution.

pH Scale

A scale for measuring hydrogen ion concentration.

  • pH < 7: Acidic solution
  • pH = 7: Neutral solution
  • pH > 7: Basic (alkaline) solution
  • Strong Acids: Give rise to more H‚Å∫ ions (e.g., HCl, H‚ÇÇSO‚ÇÑ).
  • Weak Acids: Give less H‚Å∫ ions (e.g., CH‚ÇÉCOOH).

Human Body Works within a narrow pH range of 7.0 to 7.8.
Acid Rain pH of rainwater < 5.6.
Digestive System Antacids (mild bases like Mg(OH)‚ÇÇ) neutralize excess stomach acid.
Tooth Decay Starts when the pH of the mouth falls below 5.5. Toothpastes are generally basic and help neutralize the acid.

5. More about Salts

Salts are formed from the reaction between acids and bases.

pH of Salts

  • Salts of a strong acid + strong base are neutral (pH = 7).
  • Salts of a strong acid + weak base are acidic (pH < 7).
  • Salts of a strong base + weak acid are basic (pH > 7).
