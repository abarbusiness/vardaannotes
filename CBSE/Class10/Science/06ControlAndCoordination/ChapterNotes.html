1. Introduction: Why is Control and Coordination Necessary?

All living organisms respond to changes in their environment. This response is not random; it is a carefully controlled and coordinated action to ensure survival and advantage. For example, we pull our hand away from a hot object to protect ourselves, and a plant grows towards sunlight to perform photosynthesis.

In animals, this control and coordination are achieved by two systems:

  • The Nervous System (for fast, electrical communication)
  • The Endocrine System (for slower, chemical communication using hormones)

In plants, control and coordination are managed primarily through chemical substances called plant hormones.

2. Control and Coordination in Animals

The Nervous System

The nervous system is a complex network of nerve cells, or neurons, that transmit signals between different parts of the body.

  • Neuron: The structural and functional unit of the nervous system.
  • Dendrite: Branch-like part that receives information from the environment or other neurons.
  • Cell Body: Contains the nucleus and processes the information.
  • Axon: Long fiber that transmits the information away from the cell body as an electrical impulse.
  • Nerve Ending: The end of the axon where the electrical impulse is converted into a chemical signal for transmission to the next neuron.
  • Synapse: This is the microscopic gap between the nerve ending of one neuron and the dendrite of the next. The electrical signal cannot jump this gap. Instead, chemicals called neurotransmitters are released at the nerve ending, which cross the synapse and start a new electrical impulse in the next neuron.

Reflex Action and the Reflex Arc

A reflex action is a sudden, involuntary, and rapid response to a stimulus. It is a protective mechanism that does not involve conscious thought.

Examples: Pulling your hand back from a hot object, blinking when something approaches your eye, sneezing.

The pathway taken by the nerve impulse during a reflex action is called the reflex arc.

The brain is the main coordinating center of the body. It is protected by the bony box of the cranium (skull) and a fluid called cerebrospinal fluid which acts as a shock absorber. The brain and spinal cord make up the Central Nervous System (CNS).

The brain has three major parts:

Fore-brain:

This is the main thinking part of the brain, dominated by the cerebrum.

Functions:

  • Controls voluntary actions (e.g., writing, talking, walking).
  • Processes sensory information from receptors for hearing, smell, sight, taste, and touch.
  • Stores information and is responsible for memory, learning, and intelligence.
  • Contains centers associated with hunger.

Mid-brain:

It acts as a bridge connecting the fore-brain and hind-brain.

Functions: Controls involuntary actions and reflexes involving the head, neck, and eyes (e.g., changing the size of the pupil).

Hind-brain:

  • Cerebellum: Controls posture and balance of the body. It also coordinates the precision of voluntary actions, making them smooth and coordinated (e.g., picking up a pencil, riding a bicycle).
  • Medulla: Controls vital involuntary actions such as blood pressure, heartbeat, breathing, salivation, and vomiting.
  • Pons: Regulates the rhythm of breathing.

3. Control and Coordination in Plants

Plants do not have a nervous system but they still respond to stimuli using hormones and electrical-chemical signals.

Plant Movements

Plants show two types of movements:

Nastic Movements (Independent of Growth)

These are non-directional movements that are independent of the direction of the stimulus.

Example: The folding and drooping of the leaves of the touch-me-not plant (Mimosa pudica) when touched. This happens due to rapid changes in the amount of water (turgor pressure) in the cells of the leaves.

Tropic Movements (Dependent on Growth)

These are directional growth movements in response to a specific environmental stimulus.

  • Phototropism: Movement in response to light. (Shoots grow towards light - positive; Roots grow away from light - negative).
  • Geotropism: Movement in response to gravity. (Roots grow downwards - positive; Shoots grow upwards - negative).
  • Hydrotropism: Movement in response to water. (Roots grow towards water - positive).
  • Chemotropism: Movement in response to chemicals. (Example: The growth of the pollen tube towards the ovule).

Plant Hormones (Phytohormones)

These are the chemical substances that control the growth, development, and responses in plants.

Growth Promoters:

  • Auxin: Synthesized at the shoot tip, it promotes cell elongation and growth. It is responsible for phototropism; when light comes from one side, auxin diffuses to the shady side, causing the cells there to grow longer and the plant to bend towards the light.
  • Gibberellin: Helps in the growth of the stem and breaking seed dormancy.
  • Cytokinin: Promotes cell division and is found in high concentrations in fruits and seeds.

Growth Inhibitor:

  • Abscisic Acid (ABA): This hormone inhibits growth, promotes dormancy in seeds, and causes the wilting and falling of leaves.

4. Hormones in Animals (The Endocrine System)

The endocrine system consists of glands that secrete chemical messengers called hormones directly into the bloodstream. These hormones travel throughout the body and act on specific target organs.

Feedback Mechanism

The body has a built-in feedback mechanism that regulates the timing and amount of hormone secretion. For example, when blood sugar levels rise, the pancreas secretes more insulin. When the sugar level falls back to normal, the insulin secretion is automatically reduced.
