Section A: Diagram Based Questions
  1. Draw the structure of a neuron and label the following parts:
    (a) Where info is acquired.
    (b) Where impulse travels as electricity.
    (c) Where impulse is converted to chemical signal.
  2. Draw a schematic diagram of a Reflex Arc. Clearly mark the direction of flow of impulse. Label 'Receptor' and 'Effector'.
  3. In a diagram of the human brain, identify the parts A, B and C.
    A: Seat of intelligence.
    B: Controls balance.
    C: Controls respiration and heart beat.
  4. Draw a diagram showing positive phototropism in a plant shoot and negative geotropism in a plant root.
  5. Mark the location of the following glands on an outline of the human body: Pituitary, Thyroid, Pancreas, Adrenal.
Section B: Scientific Reasoning
  1. A potted plant kept near a window bends towards sunlight. Explain the mechanism involving Auxin.
  2. Why is the Pituitary gland often called the "Master Gland"?
  3. Why do diabetic patients often take insulin injections? What would happen if they took it orally? (Protein digestion).
  4. Why are chemical signals (hormones) needed in addition to electrical signals (nervous system)? (Reach all cells vs only connected ones).
  5. Why does the stem of a plant bend upwards and root downwards even if the pot is kept horizontally? (Geotropism).
  6. How does the brain know that we have eaten enough? (Satiety center).
  7. Why is the flow of signals in a synapse unidirectional? (Neurotransmitter release only at axon end).
  8. Why do climbing plants like pea plants coil around a support? (Thigmotropism / Differential growth).
Section C: Differentiate & Compare
  1. Compare Nervous Mechanism and Hormonal Mechanism (Speed, Duration, Mode of transmission).
  2. Differentiate between Tropic Movements and Nastic Movements.
  3. Differentiate between Gustatory receptors and Olfactory receptors.
  4. Compare the function of Insulin and Glucagon (though Glucagon is extra, compare broadly as sugar regulation).
  5. Differentiate between Involuntary action and Reflex action.
