1. Introduction to Carbon

Carbon is an element of immense significance to us in both its elemental form and in combined forms.

Abundance:

  • Earth's Crust: Contains only 0.02% carbon in the form of minerals (like carbonates, hydrogen-carbonates, coal, and petroleum).
  • Atmosphere: Contains 0.03% of carbon dioxide.

Versatility: Despite this small amount, carbon is the basis for all living structures, food, clothes, medicines, and books.

Properties of Carbon Compounds:

  • Most carbon compounds are poor conductors of electricity.
  • They have low melting and boiling points compared to ionic compounds.
Conclusion: The forces of attraction between the molecules are not very strong, and the bonding does not give rise to any ions.

2. Bonding in Carbon - The Covalent Bond

Carbon has an atomic number of 6. Its electronic configuration is 2, 4. To achieve a stable noble gas configuration, it needs to gain or lose 4 electrons. However, this presents problems:

  • Gaining 4 electrons (C4- anion): It would be difficult for the nucleus (with 6 protons) to hold on to 10 electrons (4 extra electrons).
  • Losing 4 electrons (C4+ cation): It would require a large amount of energy to remove 4 electrons, leaving a carbon cation with 6 protons holding only 2 electrons.
The Solution: Sharing Electrons
Carbon overcomes this problem by sharing its valence electrons with other atoms of carbon or atoms of other elements. This type of bond formed by the sharing of electron pairs is called a Covalent Bond.

Examples of Covalent Bonding:

  • Hydrogen (H2): Two hydrogen atoms share one electron each to form a single covalent bond (H-H).
  • Oxygen (O2): Two oxygen atoms share two electrons each to form a double bond (O=O).
  • Nitrogen (N2): Two nitrogen atoms share three electrons each to form a triple bond (Nâ‰¡N).
  • Methane (CH4): Carbon shares its 4 valence electrons with 4 hydrogen atoms to satisfy its valency.

Properties of Covalent Molecules:

  • They have strong bonds within the molecule but weak inter-molecular forces.
  • This results in low melting and boiling points.
  • They are generally poor conductors of electricity because no charged particles are formed.

2.1 Allotropes of Carbon

The element carbon occurs in different forms in nature with widely varying physical properties, known as allotropes.

  • Diamond: Each carbon atom is bonded to four other carbon atoms, forming a rigid three-dimensional structure. It is the hardest substance known.
  • Graphite: Each carbon atom is bonded to three other carbon atoms in the same plane, forming a hexagonal array. It is smooth, slippery, and a good conductor of electricity.
  • Fullerenes: Example: C-60. Carbon atoms are arranged in the shape of a football. It looks like a geodesic dome.

3. Versatile Nature of Carbon

Carbon forms millions of compounds. Two main factors make this possible:

1. Catenation:

  • Carbon has the unique ability to form bonds with other atoms of carbon to form large molecules.
  • These compounds may have long chains, branched chains, or rings.
  • The Carbon-Carbon bond is very strong and stable.

2. Tetravalency:

  • Carbon has a valency of 4, meaning it is capable of bonding with 4 other atoms of carbon or atoms of other mono-valent elements.
  • Carbon forms strong bonds because its small size enables the nucleus to hold onto the shared pairs of electrons strongly.

Saturated vs. Unsaturated Carbon Compounds

  • Saturated Compounds (Alkanes): Compounds where carbon atoms are linked by only single bonds. They are normally not very reactive.
    Example: Ethane (C2H6), Propane (C3H8).
  • Unsaturated Compounds (Alkenes/Alkynes): Compounds having double or triple bonds between carbon atoms. They are more reactive than saturated compounds.
    Example: Ethene (C2H4) has a double bond; Ethyne (C2H2) has a triple bond.

Chains, Branches, and Rings

Structural Isomers: Compounds with identical molecular formulas but different structures (carbon skeletons) are called structural isomers.

Example: Butane (C4H10) can be a straight chain or a branched chain.

Ring Structures: Carbon atoms can be arranged in the form of a ring.

  • Cyclohexane (C6H12): A saturated ring structure.
  • Benzene (C6H6): An unsaturated ring structure.

4. Homologous Series

A homologous series is a series of compounds in which the same functional group substitutes for hydrogen in a carbon chain.

Characteristics:

  • Successive members differ by a -CH2- unit.
  • Successive members differ by 14 u in molecular mass.
  • Chemical properties remain similar because they are determined by the functional group.
  • Physical properties show a gradation. For example, melting and boiling points increase as molecular mass increases.

General Formula for Alkenes: CnH2n.

5. Functional Groups and Nomenclature

A heteroatom is an element replacing hydrogen in a hydrocarbon chain (like Cl/Br, Oxygen, Nitrogen, Sulphur). This atom or group of atoms confers specific properties to the compound, regardless of the carbon chain length, and is called a Functional Group.

Common Functional Groups:

  • Halo- (Chloro/Bromo): -Cl, -Br (Substitutes for hydrogen atom).
  • Alcohol: -OH.
  • Aldehyde: -CHO.
  • Ketone: -CO-.
  • Carboxylic Acid: -COOH.

Naming Carbon Compounds (Nomenclature):

  1. Identify the number of carbon atoms (e.g., 3 carbons = Propane).
  2. Indicate the functional group with a prefix or suffix.
  3. If the suffix begins with a vowel (a, e, i, o, u), delete the final 'e' of the carbon chain name (e.g., Propane â†’ Propanone).
  4. For unsaturated chains, change 'ane' to 'ene' (double bond) or 'yne' (triple bond) (e.g., Propene, Propyne).

6. Chemical Properties of Carbon Compounds

1. Combustion

Carbon compounds burn in oxygen to give carbon dioxide, water, heat, and light.

  • Saturated hydrocarbons: Generally give a clean blue flame.
  • Unsaturated hydrocarbons: Give a yellow flame with lots of black smoke (sooty deposit).
Note: Limiting the air supply results in incomplete combustion even for saturated hydrocarbons, giving a sooty flame.

2. Oxidation

Carbon compounds can be easily oxidized on combustion. Alcohols can be converted into carboxylic acids using Oxidising Agents like alkaline potassium permanganate or acidified potassium dichromate.

3. Addition Reaction

Unsaturated hydrocarbons add hydrogen in the presence of catalysts such as palladium or nickel to give saturated hydrocarbons. This reaction is used in the hydrogenation of vegetable oils using a nickel catalyst.

Health Note: Vegetable oils (unsaturated) are 'healthy', while animal fats (saturated) are generally harmful for health.

4. Substitution Reaction

In the presence of sunlight, chlorine is added to hydrocarbons in a very fast reaction. Chlorine replaces hydrogen atoms one by one.

7. Important Carbon Compounds

A. Ethanol (CH3CH2OH)

Properties: Liquid at room temperature, soluble in water in all proportions. Commonly called alcohol, used in alcoholic drinks and medicines (tincture iodine, cough syrups).

Safety: Intake of pure ethanol (absolute alcohol) can be lethal. Methanol intake can cause blindness and death.

Reactions:

  • Reaction with Sodium: Produces hydrogen gas and sodium ethoxide.
    2Na + 2CH3CH2OH â†’ 2CH3CH2O-Na+ + H2.
  • Dehydration: Heating ethanol at 443 K with excess concentrated sulphuric acid results in the dehydration of ethanol to give ethene.

B. Ethanoic Acid (CH3COOH)

Properties: Commonly called acetic acid and belongs to carboxylic acids. A 5-8% solution in water is called vinegar. Pure ethanoic acid is called glacial acetic acid because it freezes during winter (MP = 290 K). It is a weak acid.

Reactions:

  • Esterification: Reacts with absolute ethanol in the presence of an acid catalyst to give an ester. Esters are sweet-smelling substances used in perfumes.
    Ethanoic acid + Ethanol â†’ Ester + Water.
  • Saponification: On treating with sodium hydroxide (alkali), the ester is converted back to alcohol and sodium salt of carboxylic acid (soap).
  • Reaction with a Base: Reacts with a base (like NaOH) to give salt (sodium ethanoate) and water.
  • Reaction with Carbonates/Hydrogencarbonates: Reacts to give salt, carbon dioxide, and water.

8. Soaps and Detergents

Soaps:

Soaps are sodium or potassium salts of long-chain carboxylic acids.

Structure: The molecule has two ends with differing properties:

  • Hydrophilic end: Interacts with water (ionic end).
  • Hydrophobic end: Interacts with hydrocarbons/oil (carbon chain).
Mechanism of Cleaning (Micelles):
  1. When soap is at the surface of water, the hydrophobic tail aligns away from water.
  2. Inside water, clusters of molecules form where hydrophobic tails are in the interior and ionic ends are on the surface. This formation is called a micelle.
  3. The oily dirt collects in the center of the micelle.
  4. The micelles stay in solution as a colloid and the dirt is easily rinsed away.

Problem with Hard Water:

Hard water contains calcium and magnesium salts. Soap reacts with these salts to form an insoluble precipitate called scum. This makes foam formation difficult and wastes soap.

Detergents:

Detergents are sodium salts of sulphonic acids or ammonium salts with chlorides or bromides ions.

  • The charged ends of these compounds do not form insoluble precipitates with calcium and magnesium ions.
  • Thus, they remain effective in hard water and are usually used to make shampoos and products for cleaning clothes.
