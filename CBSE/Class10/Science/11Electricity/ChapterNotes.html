1. Electric Current & Circuit

Figure 3.1: Common Electric Circuit Symbols — Battery, Resistor, Ammeter, Voltmeter, Key, Bulb

Electric Current (I): The rate of flow of electric charge. $$ I = \frac{Q}{t} $$ Unit: Ampere (A). $1 \text{ A} = 1 \text{ C/s}$.

Electric Potential Difference (V): Work done to move a unit charge from one point to another. $$ V = \frac{W}{Q} $$ Unit: Volt (V). $1 \text{ V} = 1 \text{ J/C}$.

Key Electric Quantities

  • Charge (Q): Unit Coulomb (C). $1 \text{ C} = 6 \times 10^{18}$ electrons.
  • Current (I): Measured by Ammeter (connected in series, low resistance).
  • Potential Difference (V): Measured by Voltmeter (connected in parallel, high resistance).
  • Resistance (R): Opposition to current flow. Unit Ohm ($\Omega$).
REMEMBER

Electrons move from negative to positive terminal, but conventional current direction is from positive to negative terminal.

2. Ohm's Law & Resistance

OHM'S LAW $$ V = I R $$

States that the potential difference (V) across a conductor is directly proportional to the current (I) flowing through it, provided its temperature and other physical conditions remain constant.

Figure 3.2: Ohm's Law Experiment — Battery, Key, Rheostat, Ammeter (series), Voltmeter (parallel across resistor) Figure 3.3: V-I Graph (slope = R) & I-V Graph (slope = 1/R)

V-I Graphs

Ohmic Conductors: Show a straight line V-I graph passing through the origin. The SLOPE of V-I graph gives Resistance ($R$).

Non-Ohmic Conductors: Do not obey Ohm's Law (e.g., semiconductor diodes, transistors). Their V-I graph is non-linear.

Factors Affecting Resistance

The resistance (R) of a conductor depends on:

  1. Length (L): $R \propto L$ (longer wire, more resistance).
  2. Area of Cross-section (A): $R \propto 1/A$ (thicker wire, less resistance).
  3. Nature of Material: Represented by Resistivity ($\rho$).
  4. Temperature: Resistance generally increases with temperature for metals.

Combining these, we get: $$ R = \rho \frac{L}{A} $$ Unit of Resistivity ($\rho$): Ohm-meter ($\Omega \cdot \text{m}$).

ADVANCED TRICK: STRETCHING A WIRE If a wire of resistance $R$ is stretched to 'n' times its original length:
  • New Length $L' = nL$
  • New Area $A' = A/n$ (Volume remains constant)
  • New Resistance $R' = \rho \frac{nL}{A/n} = n^2 (\rho \frac{L}{A}) = n^2 R$.
Example: Stretch 2x length $\to$ Resistance becomes $2^2 = 4$ times.

3. Combination of Resistors

Figure 3.4: Series Circuit (top) & Parallel Circuit (bottom) — note current and voltage behaviour
Feature Series Combination Parallel Combination
Current (I) Same through each resistor. Divides among resistors ($I = I_1 + I_2 + \dots$).
Voltage (V) Divides across resistors ($V = V_1 + V_2 + \dots$). Same across each resistor.
Equivalent Resistance ($R_{eq}$) $R_{eq} = R_1 + R_2 + \dots$ (Increases total resistance). $\frac{1}{R_{eq}} = \frac{1}{R_1} + \frac{1}{R_2} + \dots$ (Decreases total resistance).
Application Used when a higher total resistance is needed (e.g., decorative lights). Used in household circuits to provide same voltage to all appliances.
Disadvantage If one component fails, the entire circuit breaks. If one component fails, others continue to work.

4. Heating Effect of Electric Current

JOULE'S LAW OF HEATING $$ H = I^2 R t $$

When current (I) flows through a resistor (R) for time (t), heat (H) is produced. This is known as the heating effect of electric current.

Electric Power (P)

Electric Power: The rate at which electric energy is consumed or dissipated. $$ P = VI = I^2 R = \frac{V^2}{R} $$ Unit: Watt (W). $1 \text{ W} = 1 \text{ J/s}$.

Commercial Unit of Electric Energy: Kilowatt-hour (kWh). $$ 1 \text{ kWh (Unit)} = 3.6 \times 10^6 \text{ Joules} $$

5. Practical Applications of Heating Effect

  • Electric Fuse:
    KEY POINTS

    Made of an alloy of Lead and Tin. Has a low melting point. Connected in series with the live wire. Protects circuits and appliances from high current.

  • Electric Heater/Iron Element:
    KEY POINTS

    Made of Nichrome (an alloy of Nickel, Chromium, Manganese, Iron). Has high resistivity and a high melting point. Does not oxidize easily at high temperatures.

  • Electric Bulb Filament:
    KEY POINTS

    Made of Tungsten. Has a very high melting point (approx. $3380^\circ$C). It glows at high temperatures. Bulbs are filled with inert gases (Nitrogen, Argon) to prolong filament life.

Comparing Bulbs:
In Series: Higher resistance bulb glows brighter (P = I²R, same I).
In Parallel: Lower resistance bulb glows brighter (P = V²/R, same V).
This is the most common exam numerical trap!
