1. The Human Eye

The human eye is a natural optical instrument that enables us to see. It works like a camera, forming a real, inverted image on a light-sensitive screen called the retina.

Structure and Function of Key Parts

  • Cornea: The transparent, bulging front surface of the eyeball. Most of the refraction of light occurs here.
  • Iris: A dark, muscular diaphragm located behind the cornea. Its primary function is to control the size of the pupil.
  • Pupil: The small opening in the center of the iris. It regulates the amount of light entering the eye. In bright light, it contracts; in dim light, it expands.
  • Crystalline Lens: A transparent, flexible, convex lens located behind the pupil. It provides the fine adjustment of focal length to focus objects at different distances.
  • Ciliary Muscles: These muscles hold the eye lens in position. They modify the curvature (and thus the focal length) of the lens.
  • Retina: The light-sensitive screen at the back of the eye. It contains a vast number of light-sensitive cells (rods and cones) that get activated upon illumination and generate electrical signals.
  • Optic Nerve: This nerve transmits the electrical signals from the retina to the brain. The brain then interprets these signals, allowing us to perceive the image as erect and in its actual size.

Power of Accommodation

This is the ability of the eye lens to adjust its focal length to see both distant and nearby objects clearly.

  • For Distant Objects: The ciliary muscles are relaxed, making the eye lens thin. This increases the focal length.
  • For Nearby Objects: The ciliary muscles contract, making the eye lens thicker. This decreases the focal length.

Near Point and Far Point

Near Point: The minimum distance at which an object can be seen clearly without any strain. For a normal young adult eye, it is 25 cm.

Far Point: The farthest point up to which the eye can see objects clearly. For a normal eye, it is infinity.

2. Defects of Vision and their Correction

Sometimes, the eye's power of accommodation is lost, leading to refractive defects.

1. Myopia (Near-sightedness)

  • Problem: A person can see nearby objects clearly but cannot see distant objects distinctly. The far point of the eye is nearer than infinity.
  • Cause: The image of a distant object is formed in front of the retina. This happens due to:
    • Excessive curvature of the eye lens (lens is too converging).
    • Elongation of the eyeball.
  • Correction: A concave lens of suitable power is used. It diverges the light rays before they reach the eye lens, allowing the final image to form on the retina.

2. Hypermetropia (Far-sightedness)

  • Problem: A person can see distant objects clearly but finds it difficult to see nearby objects. The near point of the eye is farther away than 25 cm.
  • Cause: The image of a nearby object is formed behind the retina. This happens due to:
    • The focal length of the eye lens being too long.
    • The eyeball being too short.
  • Correction: A convex lens of suitable power is used. It provides the additional converging power needed to form the image on the retina.

3. Presbyopia

  • Problem: This is the "old age" defect where people find it difficult to see nearby objects. The power of accommodation decreases with age.
  • Cause: Gradual weakening of the ciliary muscles and diminishing flexibility of the eye lens.
  • Correction: Bifocal lenses are often required. The upper part is a concave lens (for distant vision), and the lower part is a convex lens (for near vision).

3. Refraction and Dispersion of Light by a Prism

Refraction through a Prism: When a ray of light passes through a glass prism, it bends towards the base of the prism. The angle between the incident ray and the emergent ray is called the angle of deviation.

Dispersion of White Light:

  • Definition: The splitting of white light into its band of seven constituent colors is called dispersion.
  • Spectrum: The band of seven colors (Violet, Indigo, Blue, Green, Yellow, Orange, Red - VIBGYOR) is called a spectrum.
Cause: This happens because different colors of light bend at slightly different angles when passing through the prism. Violet light bends the most, and red light bends the least.

4. Atmospheric Phenomena

Phenomena due to Atmospheric Refraction

Atmospheric refraction is the bending of light caused by the Earth's atmosphere, which has layers of varying optical densities.

  • Twinkling of Stars: Starlight continuously passes through the changing atmospheric layers. This causes the light to refract randomly, making the apparent position of the star fluctuate and the amount of light entering the eye flicker.
  • Why planets don't twinkle: Planets are much closer to Earth and are seen as extended sources. They can be considered a collection of many point-sized sources of light. The total variation in the amount of light from all these points averages out to zero, nullifying the twinkling effect.
  • Advance Sunrise and Delayed Sunset: The Sun is visible about 2 minutes before the actual sunrise and 2 minutes after the actual sunset due to atmospheric refraction.

Phenomena due to Scattering of Light

Scattering is the process where light rays are redirected in different directions by fine particles (like dust, smoke, or air molecules) in their path.

  • Tyndall Effect: The phenomenon of scattering of light by colloidal particles, which makes the path of the light beam visible.
  • Why the sky is blue: Fine particles in the atmosphere are more effective at scattering light of shorter wavelengths (the blue end of the spectrum) than longer wavelengths (the red end). This scattered blue light enters our eyes, making the sky appear blue.
  • Why the Sun appears red at sunrise and sunset: At these times, sunlight has to travel through a greater distance in the atmosphere. Most of the blue light is scattered away by the particles. The light that reaches our eyes is predominantly of longer wavelengths, like red and orange.
  • Why danger signals are red: Red light is scattered the least by fog or smoke, so it can be seen clearly from a greater distance.
