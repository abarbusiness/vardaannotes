1. What are Life Processes?

Life processes are the essential functions that living organisms perform to maintain and sustain life. Even when an organism appears to be inactive, like when we are asleep, these maintenance processes must continue. The major life processes are:

  • Nutrition: Taking in and using food.
  • Respiration: Breaking down food to release energy.
  • Transportation: Moving substances around the body.
  • Excretion: Removing waste products from the body.

All these processes require a constant supply of energy, which organisms get from the food they consume.

2. Nutrition: The Process of Obtaining Food

Nutrition is the process of taking in food (nutrients) and utilizing it for energy, growth, and repair. The mode of nutrition varies among different organisms.

2.1 Autotrophic Nutrition

This is a mode of nutrition where an organism makes its own food from simple inorganic materials like carbon dioxide (CO2) and water (H2O). Organisms that do this are called autotrophs.

Example: Green plants and some bacteria.

The primary process for autotrophic nutrition in plants is photosynthesis.

Definition: Photosynthesis is the process by which green plants convert light energy into chemical energy. They use carbon dioxide and water to synthesize carbohydrates (food) in the presence of sunlight and chlorophyll.

Three Main Events of Photosynthesis:

  1. Absorption of light energy by chlorophyll.
  2. Conversion of light energy into chemical energy and the splitting of water (photolysis) into hydrogen and oxygen.
  3. Reduction of carbon dioxide to form carbohydrates (glucose).

Stomata: These are tiny pores on the surface of leaves through which the exchange of gases (CO2 and O2) occurs. The opening and closing of stomata are controlled by guard cells. When water flows into the guard cells, they swell and the pore opens; when they lose water, they shrink and the pore closes.

Storage: Unused glucose is stored in plants in the form of starch. In animals, excess energy is stored as glycogen.

2.2 Heterotrophic Nutrition

This is a mode of nutrition where an organism cannot make its own food and depends on other organisms for its energy requirements. These organisms are called heterotrophs.

Examples: Animals, fungi, and most bacteria.

2.3 Nutrition in Human Beings

Humans have a complex digestive system called the alimentary canal, a long tube running from the mouth to the anus. Digestion involves breaking down complex food substances into simpler, soluble molecules with the help of enzymes.

  • Mouth: Digestion begins here. Teeth crush the food, and the salivary glands release saliva, which contains the enzyme salivary amylase. This enzyme starts the breakdown of starch into simpler sugars.
  • Stomach: The food travels through the oesophagus to the stomach. The stomach walls contain gastric glands that secrete:
    • Hydrochloric Acid (HCI): Creates an acidic medium for pepsin to act and kills germs.
    • Pepsin: An enzyme that begins the digestion of proteins.
    • Mucus: Protects the inner lining of the stomach from the corrosive action of HCI.
  • Small Intestine: This is the site of complete digestion of carbohydrates, proteins, and fats.
    It receives secretions from two glands:
    1. Liver: Secretes bile juice, which breaks down large fat globules into smaller ones. This process is called emulsification. Bile also makes the food alkaline.
    2. Pancreas: Secretes pancreatic juice containing enzymes like trypsin (for protein digestion) and lipase (for fat digestion).

    Absorption: The inner lining of the small intestine has numerous finger-like projections called villi, which vastly increase the surface area for the absorption of digested food into the blood.
  • Large Intestine: It absorbs excess water from the undigested food. The remaining waste is then removed from the body through the anus.

3. Respiration: The Process of Releasing Energy

Respiration is the biological process in which food molecules (like glucose) are broken down in cells to release energy in the form of ATP (Adenosine Triphosphate), the energy currency of the cell.

Breakdown of Glucose by Various Pathways

The first step, which occurs in the cytoplasm, is the breakdown of glucose (a 6-carbon molecule) into pyruvate (a 3-carbon molecule). The fate of pyruvate depends on the presence or absence of oxygen.

1. Aerobic Respiration (in the presence of O2):
  • Occurs in the mitochondria.
  • Pyruvate is completely broken down into carbon dioxide, water, and a large amount of energy.

Human Respiratory System

  • Air enters through the nostrils, is filtered by fine hairs and mucus, and passes through the trachea into the lungs. Rings of cartilage in the trachea prevent it from collapsing.
  • Within the lungs, the passage divides into smaller tubes called bronchioles, which terminate in balloon-like structures called alveoli.
  • The alveoli provide a vast surface area for the exchange of gases. They are richly supplied with blood vessels. Oxygen from the inhaled air diffuses into the blood, and carbon dioxide from the blood diffuses into the alveoli to be exhaled.
  • Haemoglobin, the red-colored pigment in our blood, has a very high affinity for oxygen and transports it to all the cells of the body.

4. Transportation: The Body's Delivery System

Transportation is the life process that involves carrying substances absorbed or made in one part of an organism's body to other parts.

Transportation in Human Beings

The human circulatory system consists of the heart, blood, and blood vessels.

  • Heart: A muscular, four-chambered organ that acts as a pump. The separation of the right and left sides prevents the mixing of oxygenated and deoxygenated blood. This allows for an efficient supply of oxygen, which is necessary for warm-blooded animals.
  • Double Circulation: In humans, blood flows through the heart twice for each complete circuit of the body. One circulation pumps deoxygenated blood to the lungs (pulmonary circulation), and the other pumps oxygenated blood to the rest of the body (systemic circulation).
  • Blood Vessels:
    • Arteries: Carry oxygenated blood away from the heart (except the pulmonary artery). They have thick, elastic walls.
    • Veins: Carry deoxygenated blood towards the heart (except the pulmonary vein). They have thinner walls and valves to prevent the backflow of blood.
    • Capillaries: Extremely narrow, thin-walled vessels where the exchange of materials between blood and body cells takes place.
  • Lymph: A colorless fluid that also helps in transportation. It carries digested fats and drains excess fluid from tissues back into the blood.

Transportation in Plants

Plants have two main transport tissues:

  • 1. Xylem: Transports water and minerals from the roots upwards to all parts of the plant. This movement is primarily driven by a suction force called transpiration pull, which is created by the evaporation of water from the leaves (transpiration).
  • 2. Phloem: Transports food (soluble products of photosynthesis) from the leaves to other parts of the plant, including storage organs. This process is called translocation and it requires energy from ATP.

5. Excretion: Removal of Waste

Excretion is the biological process of removing harmful metabolic wastes from the body.

Excretion in Human Beings

The human excretory system includes a pair of kidneys, a pair of ureters, a urinary bladder, and a urethra.

The kidneys are the main excretory organs.

Nephron: This is the basic filtration unit of the kidney. Each kidney contains millions of nephrons.

Urine Formation:

  1. Filtration: Blood enters the nephron, and small molecules like glucose, amino acids, salts, urea, and water are filtered out into the Bowman's capsule.
  2. Selective Reabsorption: As the filtrate passes through the tubule, useful substances like glucose, amino acids, and most of the water are reabsorbed back into the blood.
  3. Secretion: The remaining fluid, containing waste products like urea, forms urine, which is collected in the urinary bladder and eventually expelled.

Artificial Kidney (Dialysis): In case of kidney failure, a machine called a dialyzer is used to filter waste products from the blood.

Excretion in Plants

Plants get rid of waste in several ways:

  • Gaseous wastes (CO2 in respiration, O2 in photosynthesis) are removed through stomata.
  • Excess water is removed by transpiration.
  • Some waste products are stored in leaves that eventually fall off.
  • Other wastes are stored as resins and gums, especially in old xylem.
  • Some waste substances are excreted into the soil around them.
