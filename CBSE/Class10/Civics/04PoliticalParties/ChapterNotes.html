Political Parties

Class 10 Civics • Chapter 04

1. Why do we need Political Parties?

A political party is a group of people who come together to contest elections and hold power in the government.

Three Components of a Party:

  1. The Leaders (Formulate policies).
  2. The Active Members (Implement policies, organize meetings).
  3. The Followers (Vote).

Partisanship: A tendency to take a side and inability to take a balanced view on an issue.

2. Functions of Political Parties

  • Contest Elections: Choose candidates.
  • Put forward policies: Reduce multitude of opinions into a few basic positions suitable for governance.
  • Make Laws: Laws are debated and passed in legislature where members belong to parties.
  • Form and Run Govt: Recruit leaders, train them, make them ministers.
  • Role of Opposition: Criticize govt failures, mobilize opposition.
  • Shape Public Opinion: Via pressure groups.
  • Access to Welfare Schemes: Local party leader serves as a link between citizen and government officer.

3. Party Systems

  • One Party System: Only one party allowed to control and run govt (e.g., China - CPC). Not democratic.
  • Two Party System: Power changes between two main parties (e.g., USA, UK).
  • Multi Party System: More than two parties have chance to win (e.g., India). Leads to Coalition Govt. Messy but accommodates diversity.
INDIA India evolved a multi-party system because its social and geographical diversity is too large to be absorbed by 2 or 3 parties.

4. State vs National Parties (Election Commission Criteria)

Election Commission of India (ECI) allots symbols.

State Party (Regional Party)

  • Secure at least 6% of total votes in election to LA of a State.
  • Win at least 2 seats.
  • E.g., AAP, SP, RJD.

National Party

  • Secure at least 6% of total votes in Lok Sabha elections OR Assembly elections in 4 States.
  • Win at least 4 seats in Lok Sabha.
  • E.g., BJP, INC, BSP, CPI(M), NPP, AAP (Recently added).

Major National Parties

  • Indian National Congress (INC): Oldest. Founded 1885. Centrist. Secularism, welfare of weaker sections.
  • Bharatiya Janata Party (BJP): Founded 1980 (from Jana Sangh). Cultural nationalism (Hindutva). Integration of J&K. Uniform Civil Code.
  • Bahujan Samaj Party (BSP): 1984 (Kanshi Ram). Represents Dalits, Adivasis, OBCs.
  • All India Trinamool Congress (AITC): 1998 (Mamata Banerjee). Flower and Grass. West Bengal.

5. Challenges to Political Parties

  1. Lack of Internal Democracy: Concentration of power in few top leaders. No organizational meetings. Ordinary members ignored.
  2. Dynastic Succession: Top positions controlled by family members. Hard for outsider to rise.
  3. Money and Muscle Power: Rich candidates/criminals given tickets to win elections. Short-cuts used.
  4. Lack of Meaningful Choice: Ideological differences between parties are narrowing (e.g., Economic policies of Labour and Conservative party in UK are similar).

6. How can Parties be Reformed?

Recent Efforts

  • Anti-Defection Law: MP/MLA loses seat if they change party after election. (To stop 'Aaya Ram Gaya Ram').
  • Affidavit (Supreme Court order): Mandatory to file affidavit giving details of property and criminal cases.
  • Organizational Elections (ECI order): Mandatory to hold internal elections and list tax returns (often mere formality).

Suggestions:

  • State funding of elections.
  • Regulation of internal affairs.
  • 1/3rd tickets for women.
