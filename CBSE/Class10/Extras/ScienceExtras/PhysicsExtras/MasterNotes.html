1. Reflection of Light

Laws of Reflection:
  1. Angle of Incidence = Angle of Reflection (∠i = ∠r).
  2. The Incident ray, the Reflected ray, and the Normal all lie in the same plane.

2. Spherical Mirrors (Concave & Convex)

Uses (Most Asked):
1. Concave: Shaving mirrors (enlarged image), Torches/Headlights (parallel beam), Solar furnaces (concentrate heat), Dentists.
2. Convex: Rear-view mirrors (Wider field of view + Always erect image).

A. Key Terms (Mirrors & Lenses)

  • Pole (P) / Optical Centre (O): The geometric center of the spherical mirror/lens.
  • Centre of Curvature (C): The center of the hollow glass sphere of which the mirror/lens is a part.
  • Principal Axis: The straight line passing through the Pole (P) and Center of Curvature (C).
  • Principal Focus (F): The point on the principal axis where light rays parallel to the axis converge (Concave Mirror/Convex Lens) or appear to diverge from (Convex Mirror/Concave Lens).
  • Focal Length (f): The distance between the Pole (P) and the Principal Focus (F).
  • Aperture: The diameter of the reflecting surface or the lens.

B. Rules for Image Formation (Mirrors)

  • Rule 1: A ray parallel to Principal Axis passes through Focus (F) after reflection.
  • Rule 2: A ray passing through Focus (F) becomes parallel to Principal Axis.
  • Rule 3: A ray passing through Center of Curvature (C) retraces its path.
  • Rule 4: A ray incident obliquely at the Pole (P) reflects with equal angle (∠i = ∠r).

5. Lenses: Diagrams & Formulas

Lateral Displacement through Glass Slab: When light passes through a glass slab, the emergent ray is parallel to the incident ray but displaced sideways. This perpendicular distance between the incident ray and emergent ray is called the Lateral Displacement.
It increases with: (1) Thickness of the slab, (2) Higher refractive index of glass, (3) Higher angle of incidence.

A. Rules for Image Formation (Lenses)

  • Rule 1: A ray parallel to Principal Axis passes through Principal Focus (F2) after refraction through a Convex Lens, or appears to come from Focus (F2) in a Concave Lens.
  • Rule 2: A ray passing through the Optical Centre (O) goes undeviated (straight through without bending).
  • Rule 3: A ray passing through Principal Focus (F1) of a Convex Lens (or directed towards F1 in Concave Lens) emerges parallel to Principal Axis after refraction.

UNIT 2: HUMAN EYE & COLOURFUL WORLD

1. Structure of the Eye

The human eye works on the same principle as a camera. It is roughly spherical with a diameter of 2.5 cm. The lens is convex and projects a real, inverted, diminished image on the retina.

Part of Eye Description & Function
Cornea Transparent, front bulging part of eye. Responsible for most of the refraction (about 75% of total refraction).
Iris Dark-coloured muscular diaphragm behind cornea. Controls the size of the Pupil. Gives the eye its colour.
Pupil Small aperture in the iris. Dilates in dim light (lets more light in); contracts in bright light (protects retina).
Eye Lens Transparent, biconvex, flexible lens behind iris. Its curvature is adjusted by ciliary muscles to focus on objects at different distances.
Ciliary Muscles Hold the eye lens and change its curvature. Responsible for Power of Accommodation.
Retina The screen of the eye. Contains light-sensitive cells: Rods (detect dim light/black-white) and Cones (detect colour/bright light).
Aqueous Humour Watery fluid between cornea and lens. Maintains pressure and shape.
Vitreous Humour Jelly-like substance filling the space between lens and retina.
Optic Nerve Transmits visual signals from retina to the brain.
Blind Spot Point where optic nerve connects to retina. No photoreceptors here, so no sensation of light.
Yellow Spot / Fovea Point of maximum visual acuity (sharpest vision). Rich in Cone cells.
Figure 2.0: Structure of the Human Eye
Power of Accommodation:
The ability of the human eye to adjust its focal length to clearly see objects at different distances is called the Power of Accommodation. This is done by the Ciliary Muscles changing the curvature (and hence focal length) of the eye lens.

• Near Point (Least Distance of Distinct Vision): The minimum distance at which the eye can see clearly = 25 cm (for normal adult eye).
• Far Point: The farthest point the eye can see clearly = Infinity (for normal eye).
• Range of Vision: 25 cm to Infinity.

Rainbow Formation (From NCERT)

A rainbow is a natural spectrum appearing in the sky after a rain shower. It is caused by dispersion of sunlight by tiny water droplets, present in the atmosphere. A rainbow is always formed in a direction opposite to that of the Sun.

  • Mechanism: The water droplets act like small prisms.
  • 3-Step Process:
    1. They refract and disperse the incident sunlight.
    2. Then they reflect it internally (Internal Reflection).
    3. Finally, they refract it again when it comes out of the raindrop.
Figure 2.5: Rainbow Formation

4. Atmospheric Refraction

Definition: Refraction of light caused by different layers of the atmosphere having varying optical densities (hot air is rarer, cold air is denser).

  • Twinkling of Stars:
    â€¢ Stars are distant point-sized sources of light.
    â€¢ As starlight enters the atmosphere, it undergoes refraction continuously due to changing air density.
    â€¢ The apparent position of the star fluctuates, and the amount of light entering the eye flickers.
  • Why Planets Don't Twinkle:
    â€¢ Planets are closer and appear as extended sources (collection of many point sources).
    â€¢ Changes in light from different points cancel each other out. Net variation is zero.
  • Advanced Sunrise & Delayed Sunset:
    â€¢ The Sun is visible 2 minutes before actual sunrise and 2 minutes after actual sunset.
    â€¢ Reason: Light bends near the horizon due to atmospheric refraction, making the Sun appear higher than it effectively is.

5. Scattering of Light

  • Tyndall Effect: The phenomenon of scattering of light by colloidal particles (e.g., sunlight passing through a dense forest canopy or smoke-filled room). The path of the beam becomes visible.
  • Why Sky is Blue:
    â€¢ Air molecules and fine particles are smaller than the wavelength of visible light.
    â€¢ They scatter shorter wavelengths (Blue/Violet) more strongly than long wavelengths (Red).
    â€¢ The scattered blue light enters our eyes. (Note: We see blue instead of violet because our eyes are more sensitive to blue).
Natural Phenomena Reasoning Summary:
1. Stars Twinkle: Point source + Changing atmospheric density.
2. Planets Don't Twinkle: Extended source + Variations cancel out.
3. Blue Sky: Scattering of blue light by fine air particles.
4. Danger Signals Red: Red has longest Wavelength (λ), scattered least by fog/smoke.

5. Heating Effect & Power (Expanded)

A. Joule's Law of Heating

When electric current flows through a resistor, electrical energy is converted to heat energy. According to Joule's Law, the heat (H) produced in a wire is:

  • Directly proportional to the square of current (H ∝ I2) — doubling current quadruples the heat.
  • Directly proportional to resistance (H ∝ R) — same current, higher R produces more heat.
  • Directly proportional to time (H ∝ t) — longer the current flows, more heat produced.
H = I2Rt (Heat in Joules = Current2 × Resistance × Time)

Also: H = VIt = V2t/R  |  (Use these based on given values)

B. Electric Power

The rate at which electrical energy is consumed (or transferred) is called Electric Power.

Power Formulas:
P = VI  |  P = I2R  |  P = V2/R

Unit: Watt (W) = Joule/Second

Commercial Unit of Energy (kWh):
1 kWh (1 Unit) = 3.6 × 106 Joules
This is what the electricity meter measures.

Calculating Electricity Bill: Units consumed = Power (in kW) × Time (in hours)

C. Practical Applications (Exam Favorite)

  • Electric Bulb: Filament made of Tungsten because:
    1. Very high melting point (3380°C) — can glow without melting.
    2. Does not oxidize (burn) easily at high temperature (usually inert gas like Argon or Nitrogen inside).
    3. High resistivity, so it heats up and glows at working current.
  • Electric Fuse: Safety device made of Lead-Tin Alloy.
    • Has a low melting point — melts and breaks circuit if current exceeds safe limit.
    • Always connected in Series with Live wire.
    • Modern homes use MCB (Miniature Circuit Breaker) instead of fuses — can be reset.
  • Heating Elements (Iron, Toaster, Geyser): Made of Nichrome (Alloy of Nickel, Chromium, Manganese, Iron).
    • High Resistivity & High Melting Point (1400°C).
    • Does not oxidize easily even when red hot.
    • Resistance increases slightly with temperature.

UNIT 4: MAGNETIC EFFECTS OF ELECTRIC CURRENT

1. Magnetic Field & Field Lines

Magnetic Field: The region around a magnet (or current-carrying conductor) where the magnetic force acts.

Properties of Magnetic Field Lines:
  • They are closed curves — from North to South outside the magnet, South to North inside.
  • They never intersect each other (if they did, it would imply two directions of field at one point, which is impossible).
  • Crowded/closer lines = stronger field; farther apart = weaker field.
  • They emerge from North pole and enter the South pole.

2. Magnetic Effect of Current

Oersted's Experiment (1820): Hans Christian Oersted discovered that a current-carrying wire deflects a compass needle placed nearby, proving that electric current produces a magnetic field.

A. Straight Current-Carrying Conductor

The magnetic field around a straight conductor forms concentric circles centred on the wire. As current increases, the field strength increases. As distance from wire increases, field weakens.

B. Circular Loop (Coil) of Wire

At the centre of a circular loop, the magnetic field lines run straight through the loop. The field at the centre is stronger when the number of turns is more.

3. Right-Hand Thumb Rule

What to write in Exam:
Imagine you are holding a current-carrying straight conductor in your right hand such that the thumb points towards the direction of current. Then your fingers will wrap around the conductor in the direction of the field lines of the magnetic field.

4. Solenoid & Electromagnet

Solenoid: A coil of many circular turns of insulated copper wire wound closely in the shape of a cylinder. When current passes through it, it behaves like a bar magnet.
  • The field inside a solenoid is uniform (like between poles of a bar magnet).
  • One end of the solenoid acts as North pole and the other as South pole.
  • To increase field strength: Increase current, increase number of turns, or insert a soft iron core inside (making it an Electromagnet).

6. Force on Conductor & Fleming's Left Hand Rule (Motor Effect)

A current-carrying conductor placed in a magnetic field experiences a mechanical force. This is the principle of the electric motor.

Fleming's Left Hand Rule (Exam Definition):
Stretch the thumb, forefinger and middle finger of your left hand such that they are mutually perpendicular.
• Forefinger (index finger): Points in the direction of Magnetic Field (B).
• Middle finger: Points in the direction of Current (I).
• Thumb: Points in the direction of Motion/Force on conductor (F).

Memory tip: FBI — Forefinger=B (field), Middle=I (current), Thumb=F (force/motion)

7. Domestic Electric Circuit

In our homes, electricity is supplied at 220 V AC, 50 Hz through three wires:

Wire New ISI Colour Code Old Colour Code Function
Live (Phase) Brown Red Carries current at 220V. Dangerous to touch.
Neutral Blue Black Return path. At 0V (earth potential).
Earth Green / Yellow Green Safety wire. Connected to metallic body of appliance. Prevents electric shock from leakage current.
Key Points about Domestic Circuits:
  • All electrical appliances in a house are connected in Parallel (each gets 220V independently).
  • Earthing: The metallic body of high-power appliances (refrigerator, washing machine) is connected to earth wire. If leakage occurs, current safely goes to earth, preventing shock.
  • MCB / Fuse: Connected in series with the live wire. Protects the circuit from short circuits and overloading.
  • Short Circuit: Live and neutral wires touch directly → resistance drops to near zero → current becomes very large → overheating → fire risk.
  • Overloading: Too many appliances connected to same circuit → total current exceeds safe limit → overheating.
AC vs DC (Must Know):
1. AC (Alternating Current): Direction of current reverses periodically. Frequency = 50 Hz in India. Generated by alternator/generator. Used in homes.
2. DC (Direct Current): Flows in ONE fixed direction only. Source: Battery/Cell. Used in electronic devices, torches.
3. Advantage of AC: Can be transmitted over long distances with low energy loss (using transformers to step up voltage and step down current).
