Resources and Development

Class 10 Geography • Chapter 01

1. Resources

Everything available in our environment which can be used to satisfy our needs, provided, it is technologically accessible, economically feasible and culturally acceptable can be termed as 'Resource'.

Classification of Resources

  • On the basis of Origin:
    • Biotic: From biosphere (Human, Flora, Fauna).
    • Abiotic: Non-living (Rocks, Metals).
  • On the basis of Exhaustibility:
    • Renewable: Can be renewed (Solar, Wind, Water).
    • Non-Renewable: Formed over geological ages (Minerals, Fossil Fuels).
  • On the basis of Ownership:
    • Individual: Plots, Houses, Wells.
    • Community: Grazing grounds, Ponds, Parks.
    • National: Roads, Canals, Railways. (All minerals, water resources, forests, wildlife within political boundaries + 12 nautical miles oceanic area).
    • International: Oceanic resources beyond 200 nautical miles of Exclusive Economic Zone (EEZ). Managed by international institutions.
  • On the basis of Status of Development:
    • Potential: Found in a region but not utilized (e.g., Solar in Rajasthan).
    • Developed: Surveyed and quality/quantity determined.
    • Stock: Potential to satisfy needs but no technology to access (e.g., Hydrogen in water as fuel).
    • Reserves: Subset of stock which can be used with existing know-how but kept for future (e.g., Water in dams).

2. Development of Resources

Resources are free gifts of nature but their indiscriminate use has led to:

  1. Depletion of resources for greed of few.
  2. Accumulation of resources in few hands (Haves vs Have-nots).
  3. Global Ecological Crises: Ozone depletion, Global warming.

Sustainable Development

Development should take place without damaging the environment, and development in present should not compromise with needs of future generations.

RIO DE JANEIRO EARTH SUMMIT (1992)
  • First International Earth Summit. Brazil.
  • Adopted Agenda 21 (to combat environmental damage, poverty, disease through global cooperation).

3. Land Resources (India)

  • Plains (43%): Agriculture, Industry.
  • Mountains (30%): Perennial flow of rivers, Tourism.
  • Plateaus (27%): Reserves of minerals, fossil fuels, forests.

Land Degradation Components in India

  • Mining: Jharkhand, Chhattisgarh, MP, Odisha. (Deforestation/Deep scars).
  • Overgrazing: Gujarat, Rajasthan, MP, Maharashtra.
  • Over-irrigation: Punjab, Haryana, Western UP. (Water logging increases salinity/alkalinity).
  • Industrial Effluents: Pollution of land and water.

4. Soil as a Resource

1. Alluvial Soil (Most important/widespread)

  • Coverage: Entire Northern Plains, Eastern coastal plains (deltas).
  • Composition: Sand, Silt, Clay. Rich in Potash, Phosphoric Acid, Lime. (Deficient in Nitrogen).
  • Type: Khadar (New, more fertile, fine) vs Bangar (Old, kankar nodules, less fertile).
  • Crops: Sugarcane, Paddy, Wheat, Pulses.

2. Black Soil (Regur Soil)

  • Coverage: Deccan trap (Basalt) region. Maharashtra, Saurashtra, Malwa, MP, Chhattisgarh.
  • Properties: Made of Lava flows. Clayey material. Holds moisture well. Develops deep cracks in hot weather (self-aeration). Sticky when wet.
  • Crops: Cotton (Ideal).

3. Red and Yellow Soil

  • Coverage: Areas of low rainfall in eastern/southern Deccan plateau (Odisha, Chhattisgarh).
  • Color: Red due to diffusion of iron in crystalline/metamorphic rocks. Yellow when hydrated.

4. Laterite Soil (Brick)

  • Formation: Intense leaching due to heavy rains.
  • Properties: Acidic (pH < 6.0). Deficient in plant nutrients.
  • Crops: Tea/Coffee (Karnataka/Kerala), Cashew nut (TN/AP/Kerala).

5. Arid Soil

  • Coverage: Western Rajasthan.
  • Properties: Sandy texture, Saline nature. Kankar layer at bottom restricts water infiltration.

5. Soil Erosion and Conservation

Erosion: Removal of topsoil. Caused by running water (Gullies/Ravines) and Wind.

Sheet Erosion: Top layer washed away by water flow over large area.

Conservation Methods:

  • Contour Ploughing: Ploughing along contour lines to decelerate water flow.
  • Terrace Farming: Steps cut on slopes (Western/Central Himalayas).
  • Strip Cropping: Strips of grass left to grow between crops to break wind force.
  • Shelter Belts: Planting lines of trees to stabilize dunes (Desert).
