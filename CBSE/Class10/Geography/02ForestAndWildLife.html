1. Introduction: Biodiversity and the Ecological Web

What is Biodiversity (or Biological Diversity)?

Biodiversity is the immense variety of life forms found on Earth. It includes millions of species, from tiny micro-organisms like bacteria and lichens to large mammals like elephants and blue whales, and diverse plants like grasses and banyan trees.

These different species are closely interconnected through a complex ecological system (or web), where each element is interdependent, creating a self-sustaining habitat.

Why is Biodiversity Important for Human Beings?

Humans are an integral part of this ecological system and depend on it for survival.

Life Support Systems: Plants, animals, and micro-organisms work together to create the essential elements for our survival:
  • They purify the air we breathe.
  • They maintain the quality of the water we drink.
  • They create and enrich the soil that produces our food.

Primary Producers: Forests are the primary producers. All other living beings, including humans, directly or indirectly depend on them for food and resources.

Genetic Diversity: Conserving biodiversity preserves the genetic diversity of plants and animals. This is crucial for:

  • Agriculture: We still rely on traditional crop varieties for breeding better, more resilient crops.
  • Fisheries: The fishing industry is heavily dependent on maintaining aquatic biodiversity.

2. The State of Flora and Fauna in India

India is one of the world's richest countries in terms of biodiversity. However, many of its plant (flora) and animal (fauna) species are under threat.

Threat Level: Some estimates suggest that at least 10% of India's recorded wild flora and 20% of its mammals are on the threatened list.

Classification of Species by IUCN

(International Union for Conservation of Nature and Natural Resources)

Category Description Examples
Normal Species Population levels considered normal for survival. Cattle, Sal, Pine, Rodents
Endangered Species In danger of extinction. Survival difficult if negative factors continue. Blackbuck, Crocodile, Indian Wild Ass, Indian Rhino, Lion-tailed Macaque, Sangai deer
Vulnerable Species Population declined to levels likely to become endangered soon. Blue Sheep, Asiatic Elephant, Gangetic Dolphin
Rare Species Small population; may move to endangered/vulnerable category if negative factors continue. Himalayan Brown Bear, Wild Asiatic Buffalo, Desert Fox, Hornbill
Endemic Species Found only in particular areas, isolated by barriers. Andaman Teal, Nicobar Pigeon, Andaman Wild Pig, Mithun in Arunachal Pradesh
Extinct Species Not found after searches of known/likely areas. Asiatic Cheetah, Pink Headed Duck, madhuca insignis (wild mahua)

3. Causes and Impacts of the Depletion of Flora and Fauna

A. Causes of Depletion

Human activities are the primary cause of the depletion of India's forest and wildlife resources.

  • Agricultural Expansion: One of the major causes. Between 1951 and 1980, over 26,200 sq. km. of forest area was converted into agricultural land.
  • Shifting Cultivation (Jhumming): 'Slash and burn' agriculture, especially in north-eastern/central India, led to deforestation.
  • Enrichment Plantation: Planting commercially valuable species (monoculture) while eliminating others. E.g., Teak monoculture in South India, Chir Pine plantations in Himalayas replacing Oak/Rhododendron.
  • Large-Scale Development Projects: Since 1951, over 5,000 sq. km cleared for river valley projects. Dams continue to submerge forests (e.g., Narmada Sagar Project).
  • Mining: Disrupts habitats, blocks migration. E.g., Buxa Tiger Reserve (West Bengal) threatened by dolomite mining.
  • Overgrazing and Fuelwood Collection: Though contributing factors, damage is mainly from lopping branches rather than felling trees.
  • Unequal Access and Consumption: The wealthiest 5% cause more damage than the poorest 25% due to high consumption.
  • Over-exploitation, Hunting, and Poaching: Illegal trade has declined many species. Medicinal plants (like Himalayan Yew) are also over-exploited.

B. Social Impacts of Depletion

  • Loss of Cultural Diversity: Impoverishes indigenous communities relying on forests for food, medicine, and culture.
  • Impact on Women: Women bear the burden of collecting fuel, fodder, and water. Resource depletion increases their drudgery (walking >10km daily), causing health problems and social stress.
  • Poverty and Natural Disasters: Deforestation leads to floods/droughts, hitting the poor hardest. Poverty is a direct outcome of environmental destruction.

4. Conservation of Forest and Wildlife in India

Why is Conservation Necessary?

  • Preserves Ecological Diversity: Protects life-support systems—air, water, and soil.
  • Preserves Genetic Diversity: Ensures plant/animal genes for breeding and agriculture.

Government Initiatives

The Indian Wildlife (Protection) Act, 1972:

  • Landmark law to protect habitats. Published an all-India list of protected species.
  • Banned hunting, gave legal protection to habitats, and restricted trade.
  • Established National Parks and Wildlife Sanctuaries.

Specific Species Protection Projects:

Project Tiger (1973): Launched when tiger population fell to 1,827. Goal: preserve entire ecosystems. Key reserves: Corbett (Uttarakhand), Sunderbans (West Bengal), Periyar (Kerala).

Other Protected Animals: One-horned Rhinoceros, Kashmir Stag (Hangul), Crocodiles (freshwater, saltwater, Gharial), Asiatic Lion, Indian Elephant, Blackbuck, Great Indian Bustard, Snow Leopard.

Shift in Conservation Focus: Now focuses on biodiversity as a whole. Even insects (butterflies, beetles) and plants (since 1991) are added to protected lists.

5. Types and Distribution of Forests in India

Forests managed by the government's Forest Department are classified into three types:

  • Reserved Forests: Most valuable for conservation. >50% of total forest land. Access generally prohibited.
    High % in: J&K, Andhra, Uttarakhand, Kerala, TN, West Bengal, Maharashtra.
  • Protected Forests: Almost 1/3rd of total forest area. Protected from further depletion. Locals have some rights.
    Bulk in: Bihar, Haryana, Punjab, HP, Odisha, Rajasthan.
  • Unclassed Forests: Other forests/wastelands owned by govt/private/communities.
    High % in: North-Eastern states, parts of Gujarat.

Permanent Forests: Reserved + Protected forests. Madhya Pradesh has the largest area under permanent forests.

6. Community and Conservation

Local communities are vital for any successful conservation strategy.

Traditional Community Efforts:

  • Sariska Tiger Reserve, Rajasthan: Villagers fought against mining citing Wildlife Protection Act.
  • Bhairodev Dakav 'Sonchuri', Alwar: 5 villages declared 1,200 hectares as a sanctuary with their own rules against hunting.

Famous People's Movements:

  • The Chipko Movement (Himalayas): Women hugged trees to prevent cutting. Resisted deforestation, promoted community afforestation.
  • Beej Bachao Andolan (Tehri) & Navdanya: Showed diversified crop production without chemicals is economically viable.

Joint Forest Management (JFM):

Involves local communities in managing degraded forests. Began in 1988 in Odisha. Locals protect land in return for non-timber products and share in timber.

7. Sacred Groves: A Form of Nature Worship

What are Sacred Groves? Patches of virgin forest dedicated to local deities ('forests of God and Goddesses'), traditionally protected by communities. Interference is banned.

Nature worship is an age-old belief that all creations of nature must be protected.

Examples:
  • Mundas & Santhal (Chota Nagpur): Worship Mahua and Kadamba trees.
  • Tribals of Odisha & Bihar: Worship Tamarind and Mango trees during weddings.
  • Peepal & Banyan trees are sacred across India.
  • Bishnoi community (Rajasthan): Protect blackbuck, nilgai, and peacocks.
