🗑️ 1. What is Garbage?

Garbage (also called waste or refuse) is any unwanted material that is thrown away. Every day, our homes, schools, markets, factories, and hospitals generate huge amounts of garbage. Proper disposal of garbage is essential to keep our environment healthy.

Two broad types of garbage:
  • Biodegradable (Bio) waste: Waste that can be broken down by microorganisms (bacteria, fungi) in nature. Examples: vegetable peelings, fruit scraps, tea leaves, paper, cotton cloth, wood, food leftovers, dead leaves
  • Non-biodegradable (Non-bio) waste: Waste that CANNOT be broken down by microorganisms and remains in the environment for decades or centuries. Examples: plastic bags, polythene, glass bottles, styrofoam, synthetic cloth, metal cans, e-waste
🧠 Easy Memory Trick Biodegradable = Biology (living things) can break it down
Non-biodegradable = None of the living things can break it down

♻️ 2. The 3 R's — Reduce, Reuse, Recycle

The best and most sustainable approach to managing waste is following the 3 R's principle:

📉
REDUCE — Use LESS in the first place!
Examples: Use both sides of paper, buy only what you need, use a reusable bag instead of plastic bags, reduce packaging, take shorter showers.
🔄
REUSE — Use items AGAIN instead of throwing them out!
Examples: Use old jars as storage containers, repair torn clothes instead of discarding, refill water bottles, give old clothes/books to others.
♻️
RECYCLE — Convert waste into NEW useful products!
Examples: Paper recycled into new paper, glass melted into new bottles, metal recycled into new metal, plastic recycled into bags or furniture.
Note: Recycling requires energy and processing, so Reduce and Reuse are even better!

🌱 3. Composting — Turning Waste into Gold!

Composting is the process of converting biodegradable kitchen and garden waste into a rich, dark, crumbly manure called compost, which is an excellent natural fertilizer for plants.

How to make a Compost Pit:

  1. Dig a pit in a corner of the garden (about 3×3 feet)
  2. Add a layer of soil at the bottom
  3. Add biodegradable kitchen waste — vegetable/fruit peels, tea leaves, eggshells, rice water, wilted flowers, grass clippings, paper
  4. Do NOT add: meat, fish, cooked oily food, or plastic
  5. Sprinkle water to keep it moist
  6. Cover with soil and dry leaves
  7. After 2–3 months: microorganisms decompose the waste → it becomes dark, crumbly compost
  8. Mix compost into garden soil → plants grow better, healthier!
Vermicomposting = using earthworms (redworms) to speed up composting. Earthworms eat biodegradable waste and their excreta (castings) is a very rich fertilizer. A small vermicomposting box can be kept even in a balcony!

🔵🟢 4. Segregation of Waste — Separate at Source!

Before garbage can be properly managed, it must be separated (segregated) into types. This is done using coloured bins:

Bin Color Type of Waste Examples
Green Bin Wet/Biodegradable waste (compostable) Fruit/vegetable peels, food scraps, tea leaves, egg shells, garden waste
Blue/Grey Bin Dry/Non-biodegradable waste (recyclable) Plastic, glass, metal cans, paper cartons, cardboard
Red Bin Hazardous/Toxic waste Batteries, medicines, e-waste (old phones, bulbs), chemicals, medical waste
Why segregate?
If all waste is mixed together, the biodegradable and non-biodegradable waste contaminate each other → harder to compost organic waste, harder to recycle dry waste. Segregated waste is much easier to process properly!

⚠️ 5. Problems Caused by Garbage

  • Land pollution: Open garbage dumps are ugly and contaminate soil. Non-biodegradable waste (plastic) stays in soil for 500+ years
  • Water pollution: Garbage dumped near water bodies or on roadsides gets washed into rivers, lakes, and oceans, harming aquatic life
  • Air pollution: Burning garbage releases toxic gases and dioxins. Rotting garbage releases methane (a greenhouse gas)
  • Disease spread: Rotting garbage is a breeding ground for mosquitoes (dengue, malaria), flies, rats — they spread diseases
  • Plastic danger: Animals accidentally eat plastic bags → die. Plastic in oceans breaks into microplastics → enters the food chain → affects human health

Feature Paper Bags Plastic Bags
Biodegradable? Yes — decomposes in weeks No — lasts 500+ years in soil
Recyclable? Yes Some types, but not all
Harmful to animals? No Yes — animals eat it and die
Made from Trees (renewable, but cuts forests) Petroleum (non-renewable fossil fuel)
Better choice ✅ Reusable cloth bags are best! ❌ Avoid single-use plastic
🛍️ The Best Bag! Neither paper nor plastic bags are ideal. The best option is a reusable cloth/jute bag — durable, biodegradable, and carries more. Carry your own bag!

📝 7. Quick Revision

  1. Biodegradable: broken down by microbes (vegetable peel, paper, food). Non-biodegradable: NOT broken down (plastic, glass, metal)
  2. 3 R's: Reduce (use less) → Reuse (use again) → Recycle (convert to new product)
  3. Composting: biodegradable kitchen/garden waste → dark crumbly compost (manure) after 2–3 months
  4. Vermicomposting: using earthworms to compost waste faster; their castings = excellent fertilizer
  5. Segregation: Green bin (wet/organic), Blue bin (recyclable dry waste), Red bin (hazardous)
  6. Open garbage dumps cause: disease spread (mosquitoes, flies, rats), land/water/air pollution
  7. Plastic stays in soil 500+ years; animals eat it → die; enters ocean food chain
  8. Best substitute for plastic bags = reusable cloth/jute bags
  9. Burning garbage = toxic air pollution; rotting garbage = methane (greenhouse gas)
