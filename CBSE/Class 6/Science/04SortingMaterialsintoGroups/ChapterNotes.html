üóÇÔ∏è 1. Why Do We Group Things?

Every day, we handle hundreds of different things ‚Äî stones, water, paper, wood, metals, plastic, glass, and so on. These things are called materials. To make it easier to study them, scientists sort materials into groups based on their properties (characteristics).

Why grouping is useful:
  • Makes it easy to study large numbers of objects
  • Helps us understand their properties better
  • Makes it convenient to find and use materials
Think of a library ‚Äî books are grouped by subject. Without groups, finding one book would take hours!

üîç 2. Properties of Materials

We can group materials based on many different properties. Let us study each property one by one:

A. Appearance ‚Äî How Does It Look?

Lustre (Shininess): Some materials have a shiny, bright surface ‚Äî these are called lustrous.
   Examples: Gold, silver, iron, copper, aluminium ‚Üí metals are lustrous

Non-lustrous: Most non-metals are dull (not shiny).
   Examples: Wood, paper, plastic, coal, sulfur
‚ö†Ô∏è Tricky Point Fresh metals are lustrous, but they can become dull on exposure to air (due to oxidation). That's why old copper vessels look dark green!

B. Hardness ‚Äî Is It Hard or Soft?

  • Hard materials: Cannot be easily compressed or scratched. Example: Stone, iron, wood, diamond
  • Soft materials: Can be easily compressed or scratched. Example: Cotton, rubber, sponge, foam
Diamond is the hardest natural material on Earth.

C. Soluble or Insoluble in Water?

  • Soluble in water: The material dissolves in water completely ‚Üê forms a clear solution.
    Examples: Salt, sugar, lemon juice (citric acid)
  • Insoluble in water: The material does NOT dissolve in water.
    Examples: Sand, chalk powder, oil, sawdust
üìå Key Difference ‚Äî Solution vs Suspension Solution: A mixture where the material completely dissolves ‚Äî looks clear (e.g., saltwater)
Suspension: A mixture where particles don't dissolve ‚Äî looks cloudy/muddy (e.g., chalk powder + water)
The dissolved material is called the solute, the liquid it dissolves in is the solvent.

D. Floats or Sinks in Water?

  • Materials that float: Less dense than water. Examples: Wood, leaf, oil, piece of wax, paper, empty plastic bottle
  • Materials that sink: Denser than water. Examples: Iron, stone, sand, copper coin, marble
Key Question: Does a heavy ship float? YES! Because the ship displaces a large amount of water ‚Äî the shape matters, not just the weight.

E. Transparent, Translucent, or Opaque?

Based on how light passes through them, materials are divided into three groups:

Type What Happens to Light Examples
Transparent Light passes through completely ‚Äî objects seen clearly through them Clear glass, clean water, air, spectacle glass
Translucent Some light passes through ‚Äî objects seen but not clearly (blurry) Frosted glass, butter paper (tissue paper), thin cloth, wax paper
Opaque No light passes through ‚Äî objects cannot be seen Wood, stone, metal, cardboard, walls, thick cloth

F. Conductors and Insulators

  • Conductors: Materials that allow heat or electricity to pass through them easily.
    Examples: All metals (iron, copper, silver, gold, aluminium)
  • Insulators: Materials that do NOT allow heat or electricity to pass through easily.
    Examples: Wood, plastic, rubber, glass, air, cloth
That's why electric wires are made of copper (conductor) and covered with plastic (insulator)!

üî¨ 4. One Object ‚Äî Many Materials / One Material ‚Äî Many Uses

Many materials ‚Üí One object:
A chair can be made from: wood, plastic, metal, cane

One material ‚Üí Many objects:
Plastic can be used to make: bottles, chairs, bags, toys, combs, pipes, containers

This shows that the purpose (use) of an object determines which material is the most suitable for making it. For example, a frying pan is made of metal (good heat conductor) with a plastic/wooden handle (insulator ‚Äî so we don't get burned).

üìù 5. Quick Revision

  1. Materials can be grouped based on: lustre, hardness, solubility, buoyancy, transparency
  2. Lustrous = shiny (metals). Non-lustrous = dull (non-metals)
  3. Soluble = dissolves in water (salt, sugar). Insoluble = does not dissolve (sand, chalk)
  4. Transparent ‚Üí clear glass. Translucent ‚Üí butter paper. Opaque ‚Üí wood, metal
  5. Conductors = allow heat/electricity (metals). Insulators = block heat/electricity (plastic, rubber)
  6. The same material can be used to make many objects; one object can be made of many materials
  7. Diamond is the hardest natural substance
