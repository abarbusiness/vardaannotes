üåç 1. What is a Habitat?

Every living organism (plant or animal) lives in a specific place that provides it with food, water, shelter, and other conditions needed to survive and reproduce. This natural home/environment of an organism is called its habitat.

Habitat = the place where an organism LIVES naturally
  • A fish lives in water ‚Üí its habitat is the aquatic (water) habitat
  • A camel lives in the desert ‚Üí its habitat is the desert habitat
  • A polar bear lives in Arctic snowfields ‚Üí its habitat is the polar habitat
  • A squirrel lives in forests ‚Üí its habitat is the terrestrial (land) habitat

üåø 2. Components of a Habitat ‚Äî Biotic and Abiotic

A habitat is made up of two types of components:

Type What it means Examples
Biotic Components All living things in the habitat ‚Äî plants, animals, microorganisms Trees, grass, deer, bacteria, earthworms, birds
Abiotic Components All non-living things in the habitat ‚Äî physical and chemical factors Soil, water, air, sunlight, temperature, wind, rocks, minerals
üß† Memory Trick Biotic = Biology = Living things
Abiotic = Absent of life = Non-living things

üèîÔ∏è 3. Major Types of Habitats

Habitats on Earth are broadly classified into two groups:

A. Terrestrial Habitats (Land)

  • Forests: Dense trees, high rainfall, many animals and birds
  • Grasslands: Open areas with grass; antelopes, lions, elephants
  • Deserts: Very little rainfall, extreme heat by day and cold at night; camels, cacti
  • Mountains: Cold temperatures, snow; yaks, pine trees, rhododendrons
  • Polar regions: Extremely cold, icy; polar bears, penguins, walruses

B. Aquatic Habitats (Water)

  • Freshwater: Rivers, ponds, lakes ‚Äî frogs, freshwater fish, water lily, lotus
  • Seawater (Marine): Oceans and seas ‚Äî sharks, whales, dolphins, sea anemones, coral reef
  • Coastal/Estuarine: Where river meets sea (estuaries) ‚Äî mangrove trees, crabs, crocodiles

üê™ 4. Adaptation ‚Äî How Organisms Suit Their Habitat

Over thousands of years, organisms develop special features (in body shape, behaviour, internal processes) that help them survive in their habitat. These special features are called adaptations.

Key Idea: Adaptations are NOT learned ‚Äî they are inherited features that have evolved over generations to suit a specific environment.

C. Adaptations in Mountain Animals (e.g., Snow Leopard, Yak)

  • Thick fur/hair: Traps body heat, acts as insulation against the extreme cold
  • Yak's large lungs: At high altitudes, oxygen is less ‚Äî large lungs take in more air per breath
  • Wide hooves of Snow leopard: Work like snowshoes to walk on snow without sinking
  • Pine trees (conifers): Needle-shaped leaves ‚Äî reduce water loss and snow doesn't pile up on thin needles
  • Sloping/conical shape of mountain trees: Snow slides off easily, preventing branch breakage

üìù 6. Quick Revision

  1. Habitat = natural home of an organism (provides food, water, shelter)
  2. Biotic = living components (plants, animals). Abiotic = non-living (water, soil, sunlight, air)
  3. Adaptations = special features that help survival in a specific habitat (evolved over generations)
  4. Camel adaptations: Hump (fat store), padded feet, long eyelashes, can close nostrils
  5. Cactus: Spiny leaves (reduce water loss), fleshy stem stores water
  6. Fish: Streamlined body, gills (breathe in water), scales, fins
  7. Mountain organisms: Thick fur, large lungs, conical tree shape
  8. Living characteristics: Nutrition, Respiration, Movement, Growth, Response to stimuli, Reproduction, Excretion
  9. Plants are autotrophs (make own food); animals are heterotrophs (eat others)
