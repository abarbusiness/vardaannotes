üìè 1. Why Do We Need Measurement?

In everyday life, we constantly need to measure things ‚Äî how long is a road? How heavy is a bag? How hot is today? Measurement means comparing an unknown quantity with a known fixed quantity (called the unit).

Example of the problem without standard units:
If someone says "the table is 3 arms long" ‚Äî this is useless because everyone's arm is different! People in ancient times used their own body parts to measure, which gave different results for different people.

Body measurements used in ancient times:
  • Cubit (Hath) ‚Äî distance from elbow to fingertip
  • Foot ‚Äî length of the foot
  • Handspan ‚Äî distance between tip of little finger to tip of thumb (hand stretched out)
  • Pace ‚Äî one step
Problem: These vary from person to person ‚Üí inconsistent results!

üåç 2. Standard Units of Measurement ‚Äî The SI System

To solve this problem, scientists worldwide agreed on a standard (fixed) system of units called the International System of Units (SI) in 1960. These units are the same everywhere in the world.

The SI unit of length is the METRE (m)

Units of Length (Most Important for This Chapter):

Unit Symbol Relation to metre Used for measuring
Kilometre km 1 km = 1000 m Long distances (city to city)
Metre m 1 m = 100 cm (BASE UNIT) Height of a person, length of cloth
Centimetre cm 1 cm = 1/100 m = 0.01 m Length of a book, hand
Millimetre mm 1 mm = 1/1000 m = 0.001 m Small objects, thickness
üìå Must Remember Conversions 1 km = 1000 m
1 m = 100 cm
1 cm = 10 mm
Therefore: 1 m = 1000 mm and 1 km = 100,000 cm

üìê 3. How to Use a Ruler/Scale for Measurement

Rules for measuring with a ruler correctly:
  1. Place the ruler straight along the object ‚Äî not tilted
  2. Start measuring from the 0 mark on the ruler (zero error: check if 0 is at the edge or slightly inside)
  3. Keep your eye directly above the measurement point ‚Äî not at an angle (to avoid parallax error)
  4. Take the reading where the end of the object aligns with the ruler marking

Measuring Curved Lines

A straight ruler cannot directly measure curved lines. To measure a curved line, we can use:
Method 1 ‚Äî Thread method: Place a thread along the curved line, then straighten the thread and measure it with a ruler.
Method 2 ‚Äî Divider/Odometer method: Use a map measurement wheel (odometer) rolled along the curve.

üöó 4. Motion ‚Äî Types of Movement

When an object changes its position over time, it is said to be in motion. When its position does NOT change, it is at rest. Motion and rest are always relative ‚Äî they depend on the observer's position (reference point).

Example: A person sitting in a moving bus ‚Äî they are at REST relative to other passengers in the bus, but in MOTION relative to someone standing on the road.

Types of Motion:

Type of Motion Description Examples
Rectilinear (Linear) Motion Motion in a straight line A car on a straight road, a falling stone, a ball rolling on a flat surface
Circular Motion Motion in a circle (curved path around a fixed point) Blades of a fan, hands of a clock, Earth revolving around the Sun, a stone tied to a string being swung
Periodic/Oscillatory Motion Motion that repeats at regular intervals (back and forth) Pendulum of a clock, swing in a park, vibration of a guitar string, heartbeat
Random Motion Motion with no fixed direction or path Movement of a butterfly/bee, movement of dust particles in air (Brownian motion)
üéØ Combined Motion! Some objects show more than one type of motion simultaneously!
A rolling wheel ‚Äî the wheel rotates (circular motion) AND the bicycle moves forward (linear motion).
Earth ‚Äî rotates around its axis (circular motion) AND revolves around the Sun (circular motion at a different scale).

üöÄ 5. Speed ‚Äî How Fast?

To describe how fast something is moving, we use the concept of speed:

Speed = Distance √∑ Time
Speed is how much distance is covered per unit of time.
SI unit of speed: metre per second (m/s)
Other common units: km/h (kilometres per hour), cm/s

üìù 6. Quick Revision

  1. Measurement = comparing a quantity with a standard unit
  2. SI unit of length = metre (m). 1 km = 1000 m; 1 m = 100 cm; 1 cm = 10 mm
  3. Ancient units (cubit, foot, handspan) were unreliable as they varied between people
  4. Measure curved lines using thread method ‚Äî lay thread along curve, then straighten & measure
  5. Motion = change of position over time. Rest = no change in position
  6. Motion is relative ‚Äî depends on the observer (reference point)
  7. Linear (straight line); Circular (around a fixed point); Periodic (repeating, back-and-forth); Random (no fixed path)
  8. Speed = Distance √∑ Time; SI unit = m/s
