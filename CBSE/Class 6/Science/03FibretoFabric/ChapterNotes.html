Fibre to Fabric

Vardaan Learning Institute • Detailed Chapter Notes

👕 1. What Are Fabrics?

Look around you — your school uniform, bedsheets, curtains, bags — these are all made of fabrics. A fabric is a cloth material made by joining many threads/yarns together.

Fabrics are made from fibres. A fibre is a thin, hair-like thread. Many fibres twisted together form a yarn, and many yarns woven/knitted together form a fabric.

The Chain:
🌱 Fibre → 🧵 Yarn → 🧶 Fabric → 👕 Cloth / Garment

🌿 2. Types of Fibres — Natural and Synthetic

Fibres come from two main sources:

Type Source Examples
Natural Fibres Plants or Animals Cotton, Jute, Silk, Wool, Flax (linen)
Synthetic Fibres Man-made, from chemicals (petroleum) Nylon, Polyester, Rayon, Acrylic
📌 Class 6 Focus — Natural Fibres In Class 6, we mainly study plant fibres (Cotton and Jute). Silk and wool (animal fibres) are briefly introduced. Synthetic fibres are covered in Class 8.

🌾 3. Plant Fibres — Cotton and Jute

A. Cotton (Gossypium hirsutum)

Cotton is the most widely used natural plant fibre in the world. It is soft, comfortable, breathes well, and absorbs sweat — perfect for hot climates like India!

Where Cotton is Grown:
  • Cotton requires black soil (also called "regur" soil), warm climate, and moderate rainfall
  • Major cotton-growing states in India: Gujarat, Maharashtra, Madhya Pradesh, Punjab, Rajasthan, Andhra Pradesh
  • Cotton plant is also called the "white gold" of India

🔍 From Cotton Plant to Cotton Yarn:

1
Cotton Bolls → The cotton plant produces small, round seed pods called "cotton bolls" that burst open when ripe, revealing white fluffy cotton fibres.
2
Picking/Harvesting → Cotton bolls are picked by hand (or machine). This is called ginning preparation.
3
Ginning → A machine separates the cotton fibres from the seeds. This process was revolutionized by Eli Whitney's cotton gin.
4
Spinning → Fibres are twisted together to form yarn (thread). This was done on a charkha (spinning wheel) traditionally in India.
5
Weaving / Knitting → Yarns are woven on a loom to form fabric.

B. Jute (Corchorus capsularis)

Jute is called the "Golden Fibre" because of its golden colour and great economic importance. It is strong, rough, and biodegradable — great for making bags, ropes, and mats.

About Jute:
  • Grows in hot and humid (wet) climate
  • Requires alluvial soil (found near rivers)
  • Major growing regions: West Bengal, Bihar, Assam, Odisha
  • India is the world's largest producer of jute
  • Used for: jute bags, gunny sacks, ropes, doormats, carpets

🔍 From Jute Plant to Jute Fibre (Retting):

1
Harvesting → Jute plants are cut when they start flowering.
2
Retting → Jute stems are soaked in water for several days (10–30 days). This softens and rots the stem, making fibres easy to separate. This process is called retting.
3
Fibre Extraction → The softened stems are beaten and washed to extract long golden fibres.
4
Spinning and Weaving → Fibres are spun into yarn and then woven into jute fabric.

🐑 4. Animal Fibres — Wool and Silk

A. Wool

Wool comes from the soft, curly hair of sheep (and also from goats — cashmere and angora, yaks, camels, and llamas). Wool is used to make warm clothes because it traps air and retains heat.

Process: Shearing → Scouring → Sorting → Spinning
  • Shearing → The hair/fleece is cut off the sheep (like a haircut!)
  • Scouring → The wool is washed to remove dirt, oil, and grease
  • Sorting/Grading → Different grades of wool are separated
  • Spinning → Wool fibres are twisted into yarn
  • Knitting/Weaving → Yarn is made into woollen clothes (sweaters, blankets, shawls)

B. Silk (The Queen of Fabrics!)

Silk is one of the most beautiful, smooth, and shining natural fibres. It comes from the cocoon of the silkworm (Bombyx mori).

The Silk Story — Sericulture:
  • Silkworms feed on mulberry leaves
  • The silkworm spins a cocoon around itself using a single long silk thread (up to 800-1200 meters long!)
  • The cocoon is put in boiling water to loosen the thread
  • The silk thread is then carefully unwound (reeled) from the cocoon
  • Many threads are twisted together to make silk yarn
  • The process of rearing silkworms to produce silk is called sericulture

🧵 5. Making Yarn from Fibres — Spinning

Spinning is the process of drawing out fibres from a mass and twisting them together to form a continuous strand called yarn.

Tools used for Spinning:
  • Takli (hand spindle) — The simplest spinning tool; a small wooden/iron pin
  • Charkha (Spinning Wheel) — A traditional Indian spinning wheel; made popular by Mahatma Gandhi as a symbol of self-reliance during the freedom movement
  • Modern spinning machines — Used in large textile mills today

🪡 6. Making Fabric from Yarn — Weaving and Knitting

Once yarn is made, it is used to make fabric in two ways:

1. Weaving: Two sets of yarns are interlaced at right angles (over-under pattern) on a frame called a loom.
   → Makes flat fabrics like cotton cloth, jute fabric, silk sarees

2. Knitting: A single yarn is looped and interlocked with itself using knitting needles.
   → Makes stretchy fabrics like sweaters, socks, T-shirts

📝 8. Quick Revision

  1. Fibre → Yarn → Fabric → Cloth (the chain of textile making)
  2. Fibres are of two types: Natural (cotton, jute, silk, wool) and Synthetic (nylon, polyester)
  3. Cotton = plant seed hair; grown in black soil; ginned to separate from seeds
  4. Jute = plant stem fibre; "Golden Fibre"; extracted by retting (soaking in water)
  5. Silk = from silkworm cocoon; rearing silkworms is called Sericulture
  6. Wool = from sheep hair; obtained by shearing and scouring
  7. Spinning = twisting fibres into yarn
  8. Weaving = interlacing two sets of yarn on a loom
  9. Knitting = looping a single yarn to make stretchy fabric
  10. Charkha = traditional Indian spinning wheel; symbol of self-reliance
