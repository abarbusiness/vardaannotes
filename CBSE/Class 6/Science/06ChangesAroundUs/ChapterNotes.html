🔄 1. Changes Happen Everywhere!

Look around you — things keep changing all the time! Day turns to night. Water becomes ice. A raw egg becomes a boiled egg. A seed becomes a plant. These are all examples of changes.

A change is any alteration in the size, shape, colour, state, or composition of a substance or object.

Everyday Examples of Changes:
  • Ice melting into water ❄️→💧
  • Milk turning sour 🥛→🧀
  • Wood burning to ash 🪵→🔥
  • A bud opening into a flower 🌱→🌸
  • Rusting of iron 🔩→🔴
  • A paper being torn ✂️
  • Dissolving sugar in water 🍬→💧

🔁 2. The Big Question: Can the Change Be Reversed?

The most important way to classify changes is: Can we get back the original substance after the change? This gives us two types of changes:

✅ Reversible Changes

The change can be undone — the original substance can be obtained back.

  • Ice → Water (melt) → Ice (freeze back)
  • Rubber band stretched → released back
  • Paper folded → unfolded
  • Water → vapour → water (water cycle)
  • Dissolving sugar → evaporate to get sugar back
  • Inflating a balloon → deflate back

❌ Irreversible Changes

The change CANNOT be undone — the original substance cannot be obtained back.

  • Wood burning → ash (cannot become wood again)
  • Egg boiling (cannot become raw again)
  • Milk → curd (cannot become milk again)
  • Paper burning → ash
  • Rusting of iron (hard to reverse)
  • Cooking food (raw → cooked cannot reverse)

🔬 3. Types of Changes — More Ways to Classify

A. Physical Change vs Chemical Change

Another important way to classify changes:

Property Physical Change Chemical Change
Composition No new substance formed; same material just changes shape/size/state New substance(s) with different properties are formed
Reversibility Usually reversible Usually irreversible
Examples Cutting paper, melting ice, dissolving sugar, stretching rubber Burning wood, rusting, cooking, souring of milk, digestion
🧠 Easy Way to Remember Physical change = only shape/size/state changes → original substance IS still there
Think: P for Physical = Previous substance present

Chemical change = new substance is formed with different properties
Think: C for Chemical = substances Change into something Completely different

B. Fast and Slow Changes

Fast changes: Happen very quickly — in seconds or minutes.
Examples: Lightning, explosion, burning of paper, evaporation of alcohol

Slow changes: Take hours, days, months, or years.
Examples: Rusting of iron (days-months), weathering of rocks (thousands of years), child growing (years)

C. Desirable and Undesirable Changes

Desirable changes: Changes we WANT to happen (useful/beneficial)
Examples: Ripening of fruits, cooking food, making paper from wood pulp, churning milk to get butter

Undesirable changes: Changes we do NOT want to happen (harmful/wasteful)
Examples: Rusting of iron, spoiling of food, forest fires, rotting of wood

🌡️ 4. Changes Caused by Heat

Heat is one of the commonest causes of change. When we heat a substance, it can change in many ways:

  • Expansion: Most solids, liquids, and gases expand (become bigger) when heated and contract (become smaller) when cooled. That's why thermometers work, and why railway tracks have gaps!
  • Change of state:
    • Solid → Liquid (melting) — e.g., ice melts, butter melts
    • Liquid → Gas (evaporation) — e.g., water boils
  • New substance formed: Burning of wood, cooking of egg (irreversible chemical changes)

❓ Tricky Question: Is cutting bread reversible or irreversible? Cutting paper/bread/vegetables is a physical change (no new substance formed — it's still paper/bread) but it is irreversible (cannot join it back). So a physical change is NOT always reversible!

♻️ 6. Real-Life Applications — Why This Matters

  • Blacksmith's work: A blacksmith heats iron → makes it malleable (physically changes shape) → cools it → it hardens. This is a reversible physical change.
  • Salt from seawater: Evaporation separates salt — a physical change, partly reversible.
  • Why rivers carry clay to sea: Weathering (slow physical/chemical change breaks rocks into smaller particles over thousands of years).
  • Candle burning: The wax melts (physical, reversible) AND burns (chemical, irreversible) — so candle burning involves BOTH types of changes!

📝 7. Quick Revision

  1. Reversible change = can be undone; original substance obtained back (ice melting)
  2. Irreversible change = CANNOT be undone; original substance cannot be recovered (burning, rusting)
  3. Physical change = only size/shape/state changes; no new substance (cutting, melting)
  4. Chemical change = new substance formed with different properties (rusting, burning, souring)
  5. Fast change = lightning, burning. Slow change = rusting, weathering
  6. Desirable = ripening, cooking. Undesirable = rusting, food spoiling
  7. Heat causes expansion, change of state, or new substance (burning)
  8. Melting of ice → reversible physical change; Burning of wood → irreversible chemical change
  9. Candle burning involves BOTH physical (wax melting) and chemical (wax burning) changes
