Chapter Overview

If you accidentally touch a hot pan, you instantly pull your hand away before you even realize it. This lightning-fast reaction is controlled strictly by the Nervous System. It serves as the master control center, coordinating all bodily actions and gathering physical data from the surroundings.

7.1 Neurons: The Basic Units

The nervous system is entirely made up of billions of specialized nerve cells called Neurons. Unlike standard skin or muscle cells, neurons cannot divide or physically replace themselves once destroyed.

Structure of a Typical Neuron:

  • Cell Body (Cyton): The main central star-shaped part containing the nucleus and cytoplasm.
  • Dendrites: Short, highly branched hair-like extensions sprouting from the cyton. They receive incoming chemical messages from other neurons.
  • Axon: A single, exceptionally long tail-like fiber taking the message away from the cyton. It is securely covered by an insulating white sheath called the Myelin Sheath.
  • Synapse: The microscopic physical gap specifically between the axon terminal of one neuron and the dendrites of the next. The electrical signal converts to a temporary chemical signal to cross this gap.

7.2 Divisions of the Nervous System

The nervous system is cleanly divided into two major functional parts.

1. Central Nervous System (CNS)

The core processing center. It includes the Brain and the Spinal Cord.

Part of the Brain Function
Cerebrum (Largest part) Controls voluntary actions, conscious thinking, intelligence, memory, speech, and senses (sight, hearing).
Cerebellum (Small part at the back) Controls physical balance, exact muscular coordination, and body posture. (Why a drunk person cannot walk perfectly straight).
Medulla Oblongata (Brain stem) Controls essential involuntary physiological actions like heartbeat, deeply rhythmic breathing, digestion, and sneezing.

2. Peripheral Nervous System (PNS)

The extensive network of long nerve cables that powerfully connecting the central brain and spinal cord to all other external organs.

  • Cranial Nerves: Nerves originating precisely from the brain to strictly serve the eyes, ears, nose, and face.
  • Spinal Nerves: Nerves explicitly branching forcefully off the long central spinal cord to completely control the arms, heavy torso, and strong legs.

7.3 Reflex Action

Sometimes, immediate physical danger requires an extremely rapid reaction. Sending a pain signal all the way to the brain, consciously thinking about it, and sending a command back simply takes far too long.

Reflex Action: An automatic, completely involuntary, and almost instantaneous physical response to a sudden external stimulus. The brain is deliberately bypassed; the spinal cord makes the exact decision instantly to save vital time and precisely prevent physical injury.

The Reflex Arc Pathway:

Stimulus (Heat) $\rightarrow$ Receptor (Skin) $\rightarrow$ Sensory Neuron $\rightarrow$ Spinal Cord $\rightarrow$ Motor Neuron $\rightarrow$ Effector (Muscle pulls hand away)
