2.1 WORK

In our daily language, the word 'work' is used for some sort of exertion (physical or mental). But in Physics, the term 'work' is used in relation to the displacement produced by a force.

Definition: Work is said to be done only when the force applied on a body makes the body move (i.e., there is a displacement of the body).

[IMAGE: Fig 2.1]
Show a person pushing a car. Label "MOTION" and "FORCE". Use premium illustration style.

Fig 2.1 A man pushing a car

If there is no displacement of the body even when a force acts on it, the work done is zero. For example, a man trying to push a rigid wall or a coolie holding a load on his head while standing still does no work in the scientific sense.

Checkpoint: Understanding Work

Q1. When is work said to be done by a force?

Ans: Work is said to be done by a force when the force produces displacement in the body on which it acts.

Q2. A person man holds a block of mass $M$ for 10 minutes. Work done?

Ans: Zero, because there is no displacement of the block.

2.2 MEASUREMENT OF WORK

The amount of work done by a force depends on two factors: (1) magnitude of the force, and (2) displacement of the body.

GENERAL FORMULA
$$ W = F \times S \cos \theta $$

Where $F$ is force, $S$ is displacement, and $\theta$ is the angle between the force and displacement vectors.

[IMAGE: Fig 2.2]
A girl pulling a trolley with a string at an angle theta to the horizontal. Show force components.

Fig 2.2 Force applied at an angle $\theta$

Note: Work is a scalar quantity (dot product of force and displacement vectors), but it can be positive, negative, or zero depending on the direction of displacement relative to the force.

Special Cases ($\theta$):

Case (i): $\theta = 0^\circ$ (Positive Work)
Direction of displacement is same as force. $\cos 0^\circ = 1$.
$W = F \times S$. (Max positive work).

[IMAGE: Fig 2.3]
Show force and displacement vectors in the same direction.

Fig 2.3 Force and displacement in same direction ($\theta = 0^\circ$)

Case (ii): $\theta = 90^\circ$ (Zero Work)
Direction of displacement is perpendicular to force. $\cos 90^\circ = 0$.
$W = 0$. (e.g., Earth moving around Sun, Centripetal force).

[IMAGE: Fig 2.4]
Show force vector acting vertically and displacement horizontal.

Fig 2.4 Force and displacement at right angles ($\theta = 90^\circ$)

Case (iii): $\theta = 180^\circ$ (Negative Work)
Displacement is opposite to force. $\cos 180^\circ = -1$.
$W = -F \times S$. (e.g., Work done by friction).

[IMAGE: Fig 2.5]
Show force and displacement vectors in opposite directions.

Fig 2.5 Force and displacement in opposite direction ($\theta = 180^\circ$)

Work Done by a Variable Force

If the force is not constant, we find the work done from a Force-Displacement Graph. The area under the curve gives the work done.

[IMAGE: Fig 2.15]
Force-Displacement graph showing a straight line OA or a rectangle. Highlight shaded area under the curve.

Fig 2.15 Work done by a variable force = Area under graph

GRAPH KEY ($W = \text{Area}$)

For a constant force: Area is a Rectangle ($W = F \times S$).
For a linear variable force: Area is a Triangle ($W = \frac{1}{2} F \times S$).

NUMERICAL Work at Angle

A force of $10 \text{ N}$ acts on a body and displaces it through $2 \text{ m}$ in a direction at an angle $60^\circ$. Calculate work done.

Solution: $F=10, S=2, \theta=60^\circ \implies W = 10 \times 2 \times \cos 60^\circ = 20 \times 0.5 = 10 \text{ J}$.

2.3 WORK DONE BY FORCE OF GRAVITY

When a body is moved vertically up or down, the force acting on it is its weight ($mg$).

GRAVITATIONAL WORK
$$ W = mgh $$

where $h$ is the vertical height climbed or descended.

2.4 UNITS OF WORK

SI Unit: The S.I. unit of work is joule (J).
$1 \text{ joule} = 1 \text{ newton} \times 1 \text{ metre}$.

Definition of 1 J: 1 joule of work is said to be done when a force of 1 newton displaces a body through a distance of 1 metre in its own direction.

CGS Unit: The C.G.S. unit of work is erg.
$1 \text{ erg} = 1 \text{ dyne} \times 1 \text{ cm}$.

RELATIONSHIP J vs ERG
$$ 1 \text{ joule} = 10^7 \text{ erg} $$

Checkpoint: Units of Work

Q5. Name the S.I. and C.G.S. units of work. How are they related?

Ans: S.I. unit is joule (J), C.G.S. unit is erg. Relation: $1 \text{ J} = 10^7 \text{ erg}$.

2.5 POWER ($P = W/t$)

In our daily life, we often see that two persons or machines do the same amount of work, but one does it faster than the other. The rate of doing work is called power.

POWER FORMULA
$$ P = \frac{\text{Work Done (W)}}{\text{Time Taken (t)}} $$

Wait! Since $W = F \times S$, we can rewrite this as:

$$ P = F \times \frac{S}{t} = F \times v $$

(Power = Force $\times$ average speed)

Units of Power:

  • SI Unit: watt (W). $1 \text{ W} = 1 \text{ J} \text{ s}^{-1}$.
  • Bigger Units:
    • 1 kilowatt (kW) = $10^3 \text{ W}$
    • 1 megawatt (MW) = $10^6 \text{ W}$
    • 1 gigawatt (GW) = $10^9 \text{ W}$
  • Commercial/Mechanical Unit: Horse power (hp).
    $1 \text{ hp} = 746 \text{ W} = 0.746 \text{ kW}$

Difference between Work and Power

Work Power
Work done by a force is equal to product of force and displacement. Power of source is the rate of doing work.
It does not depend on time. It depends on the time in which work is done.
S.I. unit is joule (J). S.I. unit is watt (W).
EXAM TRAP Work is **independent** of time, but Power **depends** on time. If two boys lift the same bucket to the same height, they do the same work, but the one who does it in less time has more power.

Checkpoint: Understanding Power

Q6. How is the power related to the force and speed?

Ans: Power = Force $\times$ speed ($P = Fv$).

NUMERICAL Power Calculation (Crane)

Example 1: A crane pulls up a car of mass $500 \text{ kg}$ to a vertical height of $4 \text{ m}$. Calculate the work done. If it takes $20 \text{ s}$, calculate power. (Take $g = 9.8 \text{ m/s}^2$)

Solution:
Force required $F = mg = 500 \times 9.8 = 4900 \text{ N}$.
Work $W = F \times h = 4900 \times 4 = 19600 \text{ J}$.
Power $P = W/t = 19600 / 20 = 980 \text{ W}$ (or $0.98 \text{ kW}$).

NUMERICAL Boy vs Girl Climbing

Example: A boy of mass $40 \text{ kg}$ climbs 30 steps each $20 \text{ cm}$ high in $2 \text{ min}$, while a girl of same mass does it in $1.5 \text{ min}$. Compare: (i) work done, (ii) power developed. ($g = 10 \text{ m s}^{-2}$)

Solution:
Total height $h = 30 \times 0.20 = 6 \text{ m}$.
(i) Since mass and height are same, Work Done is same. Ratio = 1 : 1.
(ii) Power $P \propto 1/t$. Ratio $P_1 : P_2 = t_2 : t_1 = 1.5 : 2 = 3 : 4$.

2.6 ENERGY

The capacity to do work is called energy. Energy is a scalar quantity like work and power.

Units of Energy:

The units of energy are the same as that of work (joule and erg).

SPECIAL UNITS
  • watt-hour (Wh): $1 \text{ Wh} = 1 \text{ joule/sec} \times 3600 \text{ sec} = 3600 \text{ J}$.
  • kilowatt-hour (kWh): $1 \text{ kWh} = 1000 \text{ Wh} = 3.6 \times 10^6 \text{ J}$.
  • Calorie: $1 \text{ calorie} = 4.18 \text{ J}$ (approx $4.2 \text{ J}$).
  • Electron-volt (eV): $1 \text{ eV} = 1.6 \times 10^{-19} \text{ J}$. (Used for atomic particles).

Checkpoint: Understanding Energy

Ans: Energy is the capacity to do work. The energy of a body is measured by the total amount of work it can do.

Difference between Energy and Power

Energy Power
Capacity to do work is called energy. Rate of doing work is called power.
It does not depend on time. It depends on time.
S.I. unit is joule (J). S.I. unit is watt (W).

Q8. State the relationship between joule and eV.

Ans: $1 \text{ eV} = 1.6 \times 10^{-19} \text{ J}$.

2.7 ENERGY TRANSFORMATIONS

In various devices, energy is converted from one form to another. This is known as energy transformation.

Common Examples
  • Electric Motor: Electric $\to$ Mechanical
  • Electric Heater: Electric $\to$ Heat
  • Microphone: Sound $\to$ Electric
  • Loudspeaker: Electric $\to$ Sound
  • Photoelectric Cell: Light $\to$ Electric
  • During Photosynthesis: Light $\to$ Chemical

Q9. Name the device which converts: (a) Electric energy to Mechanical, (b) Chemical energy to Electric.

Ans: (a) Electric motor, (b) Electric cell (battery).

Dissipation of Energy (Degraded Energy)

In any energy transformation, only a part of the energy is converted into the useful form. The remaining part is converted into some non-useful form (usually heat due to friction or sound) which is lost to the surroundings. This non-useful form of energy is called Degraded Energy.

DEGRADED ENERGY The conversion of useful energy into the undesirable (non-useful) form is called the dissipation of energy. Since this part is not available for productive work, it is "degraded".

3. Energy

The capacity to do work is called Energy. It is a scalar quantity. SI unit is Joule.

A. Potential Energy (U)

Energy possessed by a body due to its position or configuration (shape).

  • Gravitational PE: Due to height.
  • Elastic PE: Due to change in shape (stretched bow, compressed spring).
GRAVITATIONAL PE
$$ U = mgh $$

Where $m$ = mass, $g$ = acceleration due to gravity, $h$ = height above ground.

Work done against gravity stored as potential energy.

B. Kinetic Energy (K)

Energy possessed by a body due to its motion.

KINETIC ENERGY ($K = \frac{1}{2} mv^2$)
$$ K = \frac{1}{2} mv^2 $$

Forms of Kinetic Energy:

  • Translational: Motion in a straight line. (Example: A car moving, free falling body).
  • Rotational: Motion about an axis. (Example: Spinning top, earth rotating).
  • Vibrational: To and fro motion. (Example: A clamped wire, atoms in a solid).

Relation between Kinetic Energy and Momentum

Since $p = mv \implies v = p/m$

$$ K = \frac{1}{2} m \times (p/m)^2 = \frac{p^2}{2m} \quad \implies p = \sqrt{2mK} $$
NUMERICAL 2 KE & Bullet Penetration

A bullet of mass $5\text{ g}$ travels with a speed of $500\text{ m/s}$. If it penetrates a fixed target and comes to rest after $10\text{ cm}$, find: (a) initial KE, (b) average retarding force.

Solution:
(a) $K = \frac{1}{2} mv^2 = 0.5 \times (5 \times 10^{-3}) \times 500^2 = 625\text{ J}$.
(b) $W = F \times S = K$.
$F \times 0.1 = 625 \implies F = 6250\text{ N}$.

Practice Q7: Kinetic Calculation

NUMERICAL If the kinetic energy of a body is 2500 J and its momentum is 500 kg m/s, find its mass.

Solution: $m = \frac{p^2}{2K} = \frac{500^2}{2 \times 2500} = \frac{250000}{5000} = 50 \text{ kg}$.

Work-Energy Theorem

The work done by a force on a moving body is equal to the increase in its kinetic energy.

$$ W = K_f - K_i $$ $$ = \frac{1}{2}mv^2 - \frac{1}{2}mu^2 $$

4. Conservation of Mechanical Energy

[IMAGE PLACEHOLDER: ROLLER COASTER ENERGY]
Diagram of a simple pendulum or roller coaster track. Label 'Max PE' at the highest points and 'Max KE' at the lowest points. Show energy conversion flow.

The Principle of Conservation of Energy states that energy can neither be created nor destroyed, only changed from one form to another.

Mechanical Energy (E) = Potential Energy (U) + Kinetic Energy (K)

For a freely falling body (ignoring air resistance), the total mechanical energy remains constant at all points.

Verification (Freely Falling Body)

Consider a body of mass $m$ at height $H$.

  1. At Top (Position A): $v=0$.
    $K=0$. $U=mgH$.
    Total = $mgH$.
  2. At Middle (Position B - fall distance $x$):
    $U = mg(H-x)$.

    Using $v^2 = u^2 + 2gx \implies v^2 = 2gx$,

    so $K = \frac{1}{2}m(2gx) = mgx$.

    Total = $mg(H-x) + mgx = mgH$.
  3. At Bottom (Position C): $h=0$.
    $U = 0$.
    Using $v^2 = 2gH$, so $K = \frac{1}{2}m(2gH) = mgH$.
    Total = $mgH$.

Conclusion: Total mechanical energy is conserved throughout the fall.

2.8 POTENTIAL ENERGY (U)

The energy possessed by a body by virtue of its **changed position** (or configuration) is called potential energy.

Forms of Potential Energy:

  • Gravitational Potential Energy: Due to force of attraction of earth on it. At infinity, it is zero. As the distance from earth surface increases, gravitational PE increases.
  • Elastic Potential Energy: Due to change in configuration (size or shape). E.g., Stretched bow, compressed spring.

2.10 POTENTIAL ENERGY (U)

The energy possessed by a body by virtue of its changed position (or configuration) is called potential energy.

Forms of Potential Energy:

(1) Gravitational PE: The potential energy possessed by a body due to the force of attraction of earth on it. Is zero at infinity and negative at a finite distance from earth. It increases as we move the body further away.

(2) Elastic PE: Energy possessed by a body in its deformed state due to change in its configuration (shape). E.g., Bent bow, compressed spring, stretched rubber string.

TEXTBOOK NOTE Elasticity is the property by virtue of which a body regains its original configuration on removal of the external force. The work done in deforming the body is stored as Elastic PE.

2.11 GRAVITATIONAL POTENTIAL ENERGY AT A HEIGHT ($U = mgh$)

Measured by the amount of work done in lifting a body from the ground to a height $h$ (assuming ground PE is zero).

DERIVATION NUANCE

The least upward force $F$ required to lift a body (without acceleration) must be equal to the force of gravity ($mg$).

$$ W = \text{Force } (mg) \times \text{displacement } (h) = mgh $$

Thus, $U = mgh$.

2.9 MECHANICAL ENERGY

The energy possessed by a body due to its state of rest or motion is called mechanical energy. It is the sum of Potential and Kinetic energy.

Total Mechanical Energy (E) = Potential Energy (U) + Kinetic Energy (K)

2.10 KINETIC ENERGY (K)

The energy possessed by a body by virtue of its state of motion is called kinetic energy.

KINETIC ENERGY ($K = \frac{1}{2} mv^2$)
$$ K = \frac{1}{2} mv^2 $$

Expression / Derivation: Consider a body of mass $m$ at rest ($u=0$). A force $F$ is applied which produces acceleration $a$ and velocity $v$ after displacement $S$.
Work done $W = F \times S$.
From $v^2 = u^2 + 2aS \implies S = v^2/2a$.
$W = (ma) \times (v^2/2a) = \frac{1}{2}mv^2$.
Hence, Kinetic Energy $K = \frac{1}{2}mv^2$.

Forms of Kinetic Energy:

  • Translational KE: Motion in straight line (Moving car).
  • Rotational KE: Motion about an axis (Spinning top).
  • Vibrational KE: To and fro motion (Vibrating string).

2.11 RELATION BETWEEN KE AND MOMENTUM

Since momentum $p = mv$ and $K = \frac{1}{2}mv^2$, we can relate them as:

KEY RELATION
$$ K = \frac{p^2}{2m} \quad \text{or} \quad p = \sqrt{2mK} $$
BOARD FAVORITE If two bodies, one light and one heavy, have the same momentum, the light body will have more Kinetic Energy (because $K \propto 1/m$).
NUMERICAL KE vs Momentum

Calculate the kinetic energy of a body of mass $2 \text{ kg}$ moving with a speed of $10 \text{ m s}^{-1}$.

Solution: $K = \frac{1}{2} mv^2 = \frac{1}{2} \times 2 \times 10^2 = 100 \text{ J}$.

Distinction Between Potential Energy and Kinetic Energy

Potential Energy (U) Kinetic Energy (K)
Possessed by virtue of changed position or configuration. Possessed by virtue of state of motion.
Equal to work done in bringing it to that state. Equal to work done it can do before coming to rest.
Can change only in form of KE. Can change into any other form.
Does not depend on speed. Depends on the speed of the body ($K \propto v^2$).

2. Wound up Watch Spring: The spring has elastic PE. As it unwinds, the PE changes into KE which moves the hands of the watch.

2.13 WORK-ENERGY THEOREM

According to the work-energy theorem, the **work done by a force on a moving body is equal to the increase in its kinetic energy**.

MATH EXPRESSION
$$ W = K_f - K_i = \Delta K $$

Where $K_i$ is initial KE and $K_f$ is final KE.

DERIVATION SNAPSHOT If a force $F$ acts on mass $m$, acceleration $a = F/m$. Using $v^2 = u^2 + 2aS$, we get $S = (v^2 - u^2)/2a$.
Then $W = F \times S = (ma) \times \frac{v^2-u^2}{2a} = \frac{1}{2}mv^2 - \frac{1}{2}mu^2$.

Q10: A spring is kept compressed by a small trolley of mass $0.5 \text{ kg}$. On release, the trolley moves with speed $2 \text{ m/s}$. Initial PE?
Ans: $U = \text{KE gained} = \frac{1}{2} \times 0.5 \times 2^2 = 1 \text{ J}$.

Q11 (Rebound): A ball of mass $10\text{ g}$ falls from a height $5\text{ m}$ and rebounds to $4\text{ m}$. Find loss in KE on striking the ground. ($g=9.8 \text{ m/s}^2$)
Ans: Initial PE = $0.01 \times 9.8 \times 5 = 0.49\text{ J}$.
Final PE = $0.01 \times 9.8 \times 4 = 0.392\text{ J}$.
$\text{Loss in energy} = 0.49 - 0.392 = 0.098\text{ J}$. (This energy appears as heat and sound).

Vardaan Master Strategy Remember for slope problems: Work Done by Us = $F \times L$, but PE gained = $mgh$. The difference is always the work done against friction.
