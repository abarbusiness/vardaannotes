(SECTION A) PRINCIPLES & TECHNICAL TERMS

3.1 INTRODUCTION TO MACHINES

Definition: A machine is a device which allows a small effort applied at a convenient point and in a convenient direction to overcome a much larger load, or to obtain a gain in speed.

Contrary to common belief, a machine does not create energy. It only transforms or redirects energy to make work easier for humans.

The Four Fundamental Functions:

  1. Force Multiplier: This is the most common use. By applying a small effort, we can lift an enormous load.
    Example: A Jack. When you pump a jack, your hand moves a large distance (low force), and the car moves a tiny distance (high force). The force is multiplied.
  2. Changing Point of Application: Sometimes effort is hard to apply at the load point.
    Example: In a Bicycle, you apply force to the pedals. The chain transmits this force to the rear wheel. The "point of application" moves from center to rear.
  3. Changing Direction of Effort: Humans find it easier to pull downwards (using body weight) than to pull upwards.
    Example: A Single Fixed Pulley. You pull the rope down to lift a bucket up.
  4. Gain in Speed: In some tools, we want the load to move faster than the effort.
    Example: Scissors. Your fingers move a small distance at the handles, but the tips of the blades move a larger distance quickly to cut the paper.
Vardaan Logic Pillar **The Golden Trade-off**: You can either gain force OR gain speed. You **cannot** have both. If a machine has M.A. > 1 (force gain), its V.R. must also be > 1, meaning you lose speed.

3.2 TECHNICAL DEFINITIONS & RATIOS

To analyze a machine, we need three primary metrics. These are dimensionless numbers (ratios).

1. MECHANICAL ADVANTAGE (M.A.)
$$ M.A. = \frac{\text{Load (L)}}{\text{Effort (E)}} $$

Crucial Fact: M.A. is affected by friction and weight of moving parts. Because it is a ratio of two forces (Newtons / Newtons), it has no unit.

2. VELOCITY RATIO (V.R.)
$$ V.R. = \frac{\text{Velocity of Effort } (V_E)}{\text{Velocity of Load } (V_L)} = \frac{\text{Displacement of Effort } (d_E)}{\text{Displacement of Load } (d_L)} $$

Crucial Fact: V.R. is purely **geometric**. It is constant for a given design and does **not** change with friction. It also has no units.

3. EFFICIENCY ($\eta$)
$$ \eta = \frac{\text{Work Output}}{\text{Work Input}} = \frac{L \times d_L}{E \times d_E} = \frac{M.A.}{V.R.} $$

Efficiency is usually expressed as a percentage ($\eta \% = \frac{M.A.}{V.R.} \times 100$).

The Realistic Truth: In any practical machine, energy is wasted due to: 1. Friction at the bearings/axles. 2. The weight of the moving parts (pulley blocks/lever arms). 3. The string/rope not being perfectly elastic. This is why Output < Input, and Efficiency is always < 100%.
PRACTICE Q1 Efficiency & Logic

Explain why the Mechanical Advantage (M.A.) of a machine can be less than its Velocity Ratio (V.R.), but V.R. remains constant?

Vardaan Master Answer: Efficiency $\eta = M.A. / V.R.$. In practical machines, $\eta$ is always less than 1 because of energy loss to friction and the weight of parts. Therefore, $M.A.$ must be less than $V.R$. However, $V.R.$ depends only on the geometry (distances) of the machine, which doesn't change regardless of how much friction there is.

NUMERICAL Q2 Basic Terms

A machine overcomes a load of $150\text{ kgf}$ by applying an effort of $50\text{ kgf}$. If the effort moves $10\text{ m}$ while the load moves $2\text{ m}$, find (i) M.A., (ii) V.R., and (iii) Efficiency.

Solution:
(i) $M.A. = L/E = 150/50 = \mathbf{3}$
(ii) $V.R. = d_E/d_L = 10/2 = \mathbf{5}$
(iii) $\eta = M.A./V.R = 3/5 = 0.6 = \mathbf{60\%}$

(SECTION B) LEVERS & EQUILIBRIUM

3.3 THE PRINCIPLE OF LEVERS

A lever is a rigid straight (or bent) bar capable of turning about a fixed point called the Fulcrum (F).

LAW OF MOMENTS
$$ \text{Load } \times \text{ Load Arm} = \text{ Effort } \times \text{ Effort Arm} $$ $$ M.A. = \frac{\text{Effort Arm}}{\text{Load Arm}} $$

The Three Classes (The Vardaan "FLE" Rule)

Memory Trick: **F-L-E** (1-2-3). The letter indicates what is in the **middle**.

CLASS I (FULCRUM in middle):

Structure: **L - F - E**. The fulcrum sits between load and effort.

  • M.A. Varieties: Can be $>1, <1, \text{or } = 1$.
  • Force Multipliers: If Effort Arm > Load Arm (e.g., Crowbar, Pliers).
  • Speed Multipliers: If Effort Arm < Load Arm (e.g., Scissors cutting thick paper).
  • Change Direction: If Effort Arm = Load Arm (e.g., Seesaw).
CLASS II (LOAD in middle):

Structure: **F - L - E**. The load sits between fulcrum and effort.

  • M.A. Limit: Always **Greater than 1**.
  • Function: Always acts as a **Force Multiplier**.
  • Examples: Nutcracker ($MA > 1$ because handle is long), Wheelbarrow, Bottle opener.
CLASS III (EFFORT in middle):

Structure: **F - E - L**. The effort is applied between fulcrum and load.

  • M.A. Limit: Always **Less than 1**.
  • Function: Always acts as a **Speed Multiplier**.
  • Examples: Fire tongs, Sugar tongs, Bread knife, Fishing rod.

3.4 LEVERS IN THE HUMAN BODY (Super Detailed)

Our skeletal system is a masterpiece of lever mechanics. Most joints are Class III (speed multipliers).

  • Class I (Nodding): Fulcrum is the joint between spine and skull. Load is the weight of the head (front). Effort is applied by the neck muscles (back).
  • Class II (Raising Toes): Fulcrum is the toes on ground. Load is the weight of your whole body acting through the ankle. Effort is applied by the calf muscle (at the heel).
  • Class III (Forearm): Fulcrum is the elbow joint. Effort is provided by the biceps muscle attached just in front of the elbow. Load is the object in your hand. **Since Effort is in the middle, we sacrifice force to gain speed.**

EXERCISE 3A: LEVER MASTERY SET

NUMERICAL 1 The Crowbar

A crowbar of length $1.5\text{ m}$ is used to raise a load of $75\text{ kgf}$ by placing a sharp edge (fulcrum) $0.5\text{ m}$ from the load end. Calculate (a) the M.A. and (b) the effort needed.

Detailed Solution:
Total Length = $1.5\text{ m}$.
Load Arm = $0.5\text{ m}$.
Effort Arm = Total - Load Arm = $1.5 - 0.5 = 1.0\text{ m}$.
(a) $M.A. = \text{Effort Arm} / \text{Load Arm} = 1.0 / 0.5 = \mathbf{2}$.
(b) $E = \text{Load} / M.A. = 75 / 2 = \mathbf{37.5\text{ kgf}}$.

NUMERICAL 2 Metal Shears

A force of $5\text{ kgf}$ is required to cut a metal sheet. A shears has handles $10\text{ cm}$ long and blades $5\text{ cm}$ long. What effort is needed?

Solution:
Load = $5\text{ kgf}$. Load Arm (Blade) = $5\text{ cm}$. Effort Arm (Handle) = $10\text{ cm}$.
$E \times 10 = 5 \times 5 \implies 10E = 25 \implies E = \mathbf{2.5\text{ kgf}}$.

NUMERICAL 3 The Scissor Speed Gain

A pair of scissors is used to cut cloth keeping it $8\text{ cm}$ from rivet. Fingers apply effort $2\text{ cm}$ from rivet. Find M.A. and describe the action.

Solution:
Load Arm = $8\text{ cm}$. Effort Arm = $2\text{ cm}$.
$M.A. = 2 / 8 = \mathbf{0.25}$.
Since $M.A. < 1$, it acts as a **Speed Multiplier**.

NUMERICAL 4 Balance Logic

A $4\text{ m}$ long rod is supported at a point $125\text{ cm}$ from one end. A load of $18\text{ kgf}$ is at $60\text{ cm}$ from support on the shorter arm. What weight $W$ at $250\text{ cm}$ from support balances it?

Solution:
Load $\times$ Load Arm = Effort $\times$ Effort Arm
$18 \times 60 = W \times 250$
$1080 = 250W \implies W = 1080 / 250 = \mathbf{4.32\text{ kgf}}$.

(SECTION C) PULLEY SYSTEMS

3.5 SINGLE PULLEYS

A. Single Fixed Pulley

A pulley whose axis of rotation is stationary. It is fundamentally used to change the direction of effort.

  • $M.A. = 1, V.R. = 1$.
  • Why use it? Pulling downwards is easier because our body weight helps the effort.

B. Single Movable Pulley

A pulley whose axis moves with the load. It is a **force multiplier**.

  • $M.A. = 2$ (Ideal).
  • $V.R. = 2$ (Effort moves twice the distance of the load).
  • In practice, a fixed pulley is usually added to a movable one to ensure the effort is applied **downwards** for convenience.

3.6 COMBINATION OF PULLEYS (Block & Tackle)

Fig 3.14: Block and Tackle System (n=5)
Diagram of 5 pulleys (3 fixed in upper block, 2 movable in lower block). Show the rope winding from the hook of the movable block (since n is odd). Mark the 5 segments of rope supporting the load.
THE "n" FORMULAS

For a system of $n$ pulleys:

$$ V.R. = n $$ $$ M.A. = n \quad \text{(Ideal Case)} $$

Effect of Weight of Movable Block ($w$):

$$ M.A. = n - \frac{w}{E} $$ $$ \eta = 1 - \frac{w}{nE} $$
Vardaan Logic: Rope Segments The V.R. is always equal to the number of rope segments that effectively lift the lower block. If you see 4 ropes lifting the bottom, $V.R. = 4$.

EXERCISE 3B: PULLEY MASTERY SET

NUMERICAL 1 Real World Fixed Pulley

A woman pulls a $6\text{ kg}$ bucket from a well using a fixed pulley. If the effort she applies is $70\text{ N}$, find the M.A. of the pulley. ($g = 10 \text{ m/s}^2$)

Solution:
Load = $6\text{ kg} \times 10 = 60\text{ N}$.
Effort = $70\text{ N}$.
$M.A. = 60 / 70 = \mathbf{0.86}$.
Note: $M.A. < 1$ because of friction in the pulley axis.

NUMERICAL 2 Block & Tackle (n=3)

A block and tackle system with 3 pulleys raises a $75\text{ kgf}$ load with an effort of $25\text{ kgf}$. Find M.A., V.R. and Efficiency.

Solution:
$M.A. = L/E = 75/25 = \mathbf{3}$.
$V.R. = n = \mathbf{3}$.
$\eta = 3/3 \times 100 = \mathbf{100\%}$. (Ideal system).

NUMERICAL 3 Practical Efficiency

A system has V.R. = 3. Efficiency is $80\%$. Calculate effort needed to lift a $300\text{ N}$ load.

Solution:
$\eta \% = (M.A. / V.R.) \times 100$
$80 = (M.A. / 3) \times 100 \implies M.A. = (80 \times 3) / 100 = 2.4$.
$M.A. = L / E \implies 2.4 = 300 / E \implies E = 300 / 2.4 = \mathbf{125\text{ N}}$.

NUMERICAL 4 Velocity Ratio Trick

In a block and tackle system of 5 pulleys, if the effort moves $50\text{ cm}$ downwards, how much does the load rise?

Solution:
$V.R. = n = 5$.
$V.R. = d_E / d_L \implies 5 = 50 / d_L$
$d_L = 50 / 5 = \mathbf{10\text{ cm}}$.

3.7 THE "ONE FIXED + n MOVABLE" SPECIAL CASE

Sometimes pulleys are combined in a specific sequence where each movable pulley is supported by the previous one. If there are $n$ movable pulleys:

$$ M.A. = 2^n \quad | \quad V.R. = 2^n $$

Example: If 3 movable pulleys are used, $M.A. = 2^3 = 8$. You can lift $800\text{ N}$ with just $100\text{ N}$ effort!

CHALLENGE NUMERICAL Power & Pulley

A fixed pulley lifts $75\text{ kgf}$ load using $100\text{ kg}$ mass falling $8\text{ m}$ in $4\text{ s}$. Find (a) Power input (b) Efficiency.

Solution:
Work Input = Force (falling mass) $\times$ Distance = $(100 \times 10) \times 8 = 8000\text{ J}$.
Power Input = Work / Time = $8000 / 4 = \mathbf{2000\text{ W}}$.
Work Output = Load $\times$ Distance = $(75 \times 10) \times 8 = 6000\text{ J}$.
Efficiency = $(6000 / 8000) \times 100 = \mathbf{75\%}$.

Vardaan Exam Strategy: Diagram Check While drawing pulleys, always count the number of strands supporting the movable block. - If effort is **upwards**, V.R. = strands + 1. - If effort is **downwards** (standard), V.R. = number of pulleys ($n$).
