⛏️ PART 1: Metallic Minerals

1. Iron Ore

  • Uses: Iron is the backbone of modern industry — used to make steel, which is used in construction, railways, ships, machinery, automobiles, and appliances.
  • Types:
    • Magnetite — highest quality (72% iron content); black; magnetic.
    • Haematite — most abundant; 60–70% iron content; red colour. India's main iron ore.
    • Limonite and Siderite — inferior quality.
  • Leading Producing States:
    • Odisha — largest producer (Mayurbhanj, Sundargarh, Joda-Barajamda area).
    • Chhattisgarh — Bailadila range (very high-quality iron ore exported to Japan).
    • Jharkhand — Singhbhum (Noamundi, Gua).
    • Karnataka — Bellary-Hospet area (Kudremukh).
    • Goa — lower quality; exported.

2. Manganese

  • Uses: Primarily used in steel-making (as alloy — increases hardness and toughness). Also used in batteries (dry cells), bleaching powder, insecticides, chemicals.
  • Leading States: Odisha (Sundergarh, Koraput) — largest producer. Maharashtra (Nagpur, Bhandara), Madhya Pradesh, Karnataka, Andhra Pradesh.

3. Copper

  • Uses: Excellent conductor of electricity → electrical wires, cables, electronics, motors, generators. Also used in coins, utensils, and alloys (brass, bronze).
  • Leading States: Jharkhand (Singhbhum — Rakha, Mosaboni mines) — largest reserves. Rajasthan (Khetri — largest copper mine), Madhya Pradesh (Malanjkhand), Andhra Pradesh, Sikkim.

4. Bauxite (Aluminium Ore)

  • Uses: Aluminium (extracted from Bauxite via electrolytic reduction) — used in aircraft, automobiles, electrical cables, utensils, packaging (foil). Lightweight and corrosion-resistant.
  • Leading States: Odisha (Koraput, Kalahandi) — largest. Jharkhand, Maharashtra, Chhattisgarh, Gujarat, Madhya Pradesh.

⚡ PART 2: Conventional Sources of Energy

1. Coal

  • Uses: Thermal power stations (largest use), steel industry (coking coal for coke), domestic fuel, railways (historically), chemical industry.
  • Types: Anthracite (best quality, ~90% carbon) → Bituminous (most abundant; used for industries) → Lignite (brown coal; low quality; Tamil Nadu — Neyveli) → Peat (lowest quality).
  • Advantages: Large reserves in India; cheap and abundant fuel; can be used for both power generation and industrial purposes.
  • Disadvantages: Non-renewable; burns dirty — produces CO₂, SO₂, carbon monoxide (air pollution); coal mines cause deforestation and land degradation; transportation costly.
  • Leading States: Jharkhand (Jharia — largest coalfield; Bokaro, Ramgarh), West Bengal (Raniganj — where coal was first discovered in India), Chhattisgarh (Korba), Odisha, MP, Andhra Pradesh, Telangana.

2. Petroleum (Mineral Oil)

  • Uses: Petrol (automobiles), diesel (trucks, buses, trains), kerosene (cooking, lighting for poor), LPG (cooking gas), jet fuel; raw material for petrochemical industry (plastics, fertilisers, synthetic fibres).
  • Advantages: High energy density; easy to transport (pipelines, tankers); versatile — many refined products; cleaner than coal.
  • Disadvantages: Non-renewable; burning releases CO₂ and pollutants; oil spills damage marine ecosystems; India imports ~85% of its oil — costly foreign exchange outflow.
  • Major Fields:
    • Mumbai High (Maharashtra) — offshore; India's largest producing oil field. (Also written as Bombay High)
    • Digboi (Assam) — oldest oil field in India (1889); first commercial production.
    • Also: Naharkatia, Moran (Assam); Cambay (Gujarat); KG Basin (Andhra Pradesh — offshore).

3. Natural Gas

  • Distribution: Found alongside oil fields. Major reserves in Mumbai High offshore area, Gulf of Khambhat (Gujarat), Krishna-Godavari Basin (Andhra Pradesh — KG-D6 field discovered by Reliance).
  • Advantages: Burns cleaner than coal or oil (lower CO₂, almost no SO₂); easy to transport via pipelines; used in power generation, fertilisers (urea), CNG (Compressed Natural Gas for vehicles), cooking (PNG — piped to homes).
  • Disadvantages: Still fossil fuel (non-renewable); risk of gas leaks (explosion hazard); pipeline infrastructure expensive to build.

4. Hydel Power (Hydroelectric Power)

  • Uses the potential energy of water flowing from a height (dams/reservoirs) to run turbines and generate electricity.
  • Bhakra Nangal Dam: On the Satluj River (Punjab/Himachal Pradesh border). Reservoir = Gobind Sagar Lake. Largest multi-purpose river valley project in India. Irrigates parts of Punjab, Haryana, Rajasthan. One of Asia's highest gravity dams (~226 m).
  • Hirakud Dam: On the Mahanadi River in Odisha. Longest dam in India (~4.8 km). Controls floods in Odisha, provides irrigation and hydroelectric power.
  • Advantages of Hydel Power: Renewable; clean (no air pollution); dams also help with irrigation, flood control, drinking water; no fuel cost after construction.
  • Disadvantages: High initial cost; displacement of communities (submergence of land); disrupts riverbeds and aquatic ecosystems; dependent on rainfall/river flow; silting of reservoirs over decades.

🌞 PART 3: Non-Conventional (Renewable) Sources of Energy

Source How it Works Advantages Disadvantages Important Areas in India
Solar Energy Photovoltaic (PV) cells convert sunlight into electricity; also solar thermal for heating water Abundant in India (300+ sunny days); clean; unlimited; low operating cost High initial cost; inefficient at night/cloudy days; large land area needed Rajasthan, Gujarat (Rann of Kutch), Tamil Nadu, Andhra Pradesh (Pavagada — world's largest solar farm)
Wind Energy Wind turbines capture kinetic energy of wind to generate electricity Clean; renewable; low operating cost; can coexist with agriculture Intermittent (only when wind blows); noise; bird collision risk; installation on windy/remote sites Tamil Nadu (Muppandal wind farm — largest in India), Rajasthan, Gujarat, Maharashtra, Karnataka, Andhra Pradesh
Tidal Energy Turbines harness energy from tidal surges (rise and fall of sea level) Predictable; clean; large potential on India's long coastline Very high cost; limited suitable sites (large tidal range needed); affects coastal ecosystems Gulf of Kutch (Gujarat); Gulf of Khambhat (Gujarat) — high tidal range
Geo-Thermal Energy Heat from inside the Earth used to generate power or for direct heating 24/7 power; clean; baseload power Limited to geologically active zones; high drilling costs Puga Valley (Ladakh), Manikaran (Himachal Pradesh), Tattapani (Chhattisgarh)
Nuclear Energy Energy released from splitting (fission) of heavy atoms (Uranium, Thorium) High energy density; no direct CO₂ emissions; large amounts of power from small amount of fuel Nuclear waste radioactive (thousands of years); catastrophic accidents possible; uranium/thorium non-renewable; high cost Tarapur (Maharashtra) — 1st; Kudankulam (Tamil Nadu); Rawatbhata (Rajasthan); Kaiga (Karnataka); Narora (UP); Kalpakkam (Tamil Nadu)
Biogas Organic waste (cow dung, agricultural waste, sewage) fermented in biogas plants to produce methane gas Uses waste material; clean cooking fuel for rural areas; reduces firewood use (protects forests); residue (slurry) is good fertiliser Requires regular supply of organic waste; small scale; maintenance needed Rural India — widely used through Gobar Gas / National Biogas Programme

📌 Chapter Summary

  • Metals: Iron ore (Odisha/Chhattisgarh), Manganese (Odisha), Copper (Jharkhand/Rajasthan), Bauxite (Odisha) — all vital for industrial and electrical uses.
  • Conventional energy: Coal (Jharia/Raniganj), Petroleum (Mumbai High/Digboi), Natural Gas (KG Basin), Hydel (Bhakra Nangal on Satluj; Hirakud on Mahanadi).
