📖 PART 1: Importance of Forests

  • Ecological: Forests regulate climate, maintain water cycle, prevent soil erosion, provide oxygen, absorb CO₂, maintain biodiversity.
  • Economic: Source of timber, fuelwood, bamboo, medicinal plants, lac, honey, resins, rubber.
  • Social: Tribal communities depend on forests for livelihood. Forests are part of cultural heritage.
  • Hydrological: Forest roots absorb rainwater, reducing runoff and flooding; recharge groundwater.

🌲 PART 2: Types of Natural Vegetation

1. Tropical Evergreen Forests

Feature Details
Rainfall More than 200 cm annually
Temperature High throughout the year (25–30°C)
Characteristic Trees do NOT shed leaves together — hence always GREEN (evergreen). Dense canopy. Multi-layered. No clear boundary between trees.
Distribution Western Ghats (Kerala, coastal Karnataka, Goa), Andaman & Nicobar Islands, Northeast India (Assam, Arunachal, Meghalaya, Sikkim, Mizoram)
Key species Rosewood, ebony, mahogany, rubber, cinchona, bamboo
Problem Commercially difficult to exploit — trees of same species far apart; very diverse

2. Tropical Deciduous Forests (Monsoon Forests)

Feature Details
Rainfall 100–200 cm annually
Characteristic Trees SHED leaves in dry season (to conserve water) — hence "deciduous." Clear monsoon season. Most widespread type in India.
Distribution (a) Moist Deciduous: Eastern slopes of W. Ghats, northeastern Peninsular India, Odisha, Bihar, UP foothills. (b) Dry Deciduous: Deccan plateau, Rajasthan, MP, Andhra Pradesh.
Key species (Moist) Teak (most valuable Indian timber), sal, bamboo, shisham, sandalwood
Key species (Dry) Teak, sal (sparse), acacia, khair

3. Tropical Desert / Scrub Vegetation

  • Found in areas with rainfall below 70 cm (Rajasthan, parts of Gujarat).
  • Plants are xerophytic (drought-resistant): thorny bushes, cacti, acacia, date palms.
  • Leaves reduced to spines or thorns to reduce water loss. Deep root systems to tap groundwater.

4. Littoral / Mangrove Vegetation

  • Found in tidal zones — coastal areas flooded by saltwater (tides).
  • Mangroves have stilt roots (breathing roots — pneumatophores) to survive in muddy, oxygen-poor waterlogged soil.
  • They protect coasts from cyclones/tsunamis and are breeding grounds for fish and crustaceans.
  • Distribution: Sundarbans (West Bengal & Bangladesh — largest mangrove forest in the world), Godavari-Krishna delta (Andhra Pradesh), Mahanadi delta, Andaman & Nicobar Islands, coast of Kerala.

5. Mountain / Alpine Forests

Altitude Zone Vegetation Type Key Species
1,000–2,000 m (foothills) Subtropical broadleaf forests Oak, chestnut, laurel
2,000–3,000 m (temperate) Temperate coniferous forests Pine, deodar (cedar), fir, spruce
3,000–4,000 m (subalpine) Subalpine shrubs Juniper, rhododendron, birch
Above 4,000 m (alpine) Alpine meadows (grasslands) — used for pastoralism in summer Short grasses, mosses, lichens
Above snow line Only mosses and lichens; then permanent snow —

🌳 PART 3: Forest Conservation

Afforestation and Reforestation

  • Afforestation: Planting trees on land that was previously NOT forested.
  • Reforestation: Re-planting trees in areas where forests have been cleared (replanting deforested areas).

Social Forestry

  • Planting trees on common/community lands, roadsides, canal banks, government wastelands — managed by local communities.
  • Provides fuelwood, fodder, and timber to village communities — reduces pressure on natural forests.

Agroforestry

  • Growing trees alongside crops or livestock on the same farm land — combines agriculture and forestry.
  • Benefits: provides shade, reduces wind erosion, improves soil fertility (nitrogen-fixing trees), provides additional income from timber/fruits.

Van Mahotsav

  • Annual tree-planting festival launched in 1950 by India's first Agriculture Minister, K.M. Munshi.
  • Celebrated in the first week of July every year; millions of trees planted across India.

📌 Chapter Summary

  • 5 types of forests: Evergreen (>200 cm, W. Ghats/NE), Deciduous (100–200 cm, most widespread, teak), Scrub/Desert (dry, thorny), Mangrove (coastal, stilt roots, Sundarbans), Mountain/Alpine (altitude zones — conifers).
  • Conservation: Afforestation (new planting), Reforestation (replanting), Social Forestry (community lands), Agroforestry (trees with crops), Van Mahotsav (July, since 1950).
  • Forests' importance: ecological (climate regulation, biodiversity), economic (timber, NTFPs), social (tribal livelihoods), hydrological (water cycle, flood control).
