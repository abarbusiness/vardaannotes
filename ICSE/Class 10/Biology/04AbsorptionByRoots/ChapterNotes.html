Absorption by Roots - The Processes Involved

ICSE Class 10 Biology • Chapter 04

1. Need for Water and Minerals

Roots fix the plant in the soil giving it support, but their life-supporting function is to absorb water and mineral nutrients from the soil. The four primary purposes for water are:

  • Photosynthesis: Used as a raw material (along with CO₂) to synthesize glucose in the green leaves.
  • Transpiration: Huge quantities are evaporated as water vapour for cooling the plant in hot weather and producing a suction force (transpiration pull).
  • Transportation: Used to transport dissolved mineral salts upwards from roots to shoots and manufactured food from leaves to other parts.
  • Mechanical stiffness: Provides turgidity (fully distended condition) to plant tissues, keeping leaves upright and non-woody stems stiff.

Minerals (like nitrates, phosphates, potassium, magnesium, chlorine) are required to synthesize various compounds and enzymes within the cell. Minerals are absorbed as ions (salts) through active transport.

2. Characteristics of Roots for Absorbing Water

The ability of the roots to draw water from the soil is dependent on three key characteristics:

  1. Enormous Surface Area: Roots branch into rootlets which bear millions of root hairs. For example, a four-month-old rye plant has an estimated total root length exceeding 10,000 km! This creates a huge surface area.
  2. Higher Concentration of Cell Sap: The root hairs contain cell sap (a solution of dissolved salts) which is at a higher concentration than the surrounding soil water. This concentration gradient draws water in via endosmosis.
  3. Thin Walls: The root hair is an extension of an epidermal cell and has two outer layers:
    • Cell Wall: Thin and freely permeable (allows all water molecules and dissolved substances to pass freely).
    • Cell Membrane: Very thin and semi-permeable (allows water molecules to pass, but prevents the passage of larger solute molecules). The secret of absorption lies in this selectively permeable membrane.

3. The Five Phenomena of Absorption and Conduction

A. Imbibition

Imbibition: The phenomenon by which living or dead plant cells absorb water by surface attraction.

Substances like cellulose and proteins are hydrophilic (water-loving). For example, dry seeds swell up, and wooden doors swell during the rainy season due to imbibition. The immense imbibitional pressure helps rupture seed coats during germination. This is a passive process.

B. Diffusion

Diffusion: The free movement of molecules of a substance (solute, solvent, liquid or gas) from the region of their higher concentration to the region of their lower concentration when the two are in direct contact.

This continues until the molecules are uniformly distributed. Example: A sugar cube or potassium permanganate crystal spreading uniformly in a beaker of water.

C. Osmosis and Osmotic Pressure

Osmosis: The movement of water molecules from their region of higher concentration (dilute solution) to their region of lower concentration (concentrated solution) through a semi-permeable membrane.

  • Endosmosis (Inward): Inward diffusion of water when the surrounding solution is less concentrated. It causes the cell to swell.
  • Exosmosis (Outward): Outward diffusion of water when the surrounding solution is more concentrated. It causes the cell to shrink.

Osmotic Pressure: The minimum pressure that must be exerted to prevent the passage of the pure solvent into the solution when the two are separated by a semi-permeable membrane.

Differences between Diffusion and Osmosis:

  • Diffusion involves movement of any molecules (solute or solvent); Osmosis involves only the movement of water (solvent) molecules.
  • Diffusion does not require a semi-permeable membrane; Osmosis strictly requires a living or non-living semi-permeable membrane.
  • Diffusion can act over large distances; Osmosis occurs over very short cellular distances.

D. Tonicity

Tonicity refers to the relative concentration of the solutions that determine the direction of osmosis.

  • Isotonic (Same concentration): Same concentration inside and outside the cell. Result: No net movement of water, cell shape remains unchanged.
  • Hypotonic (Lower solute outside): Surrounding solution has a lower solute concentration (more water molecules). Result: Endosmosis occurs, water enters the cell, cell swells and becomes turgid.
  • Hypertonic (Higher solute outside): Surrounding solution has a higher solute concentration (less water molecules). Result: Exosmosis occurs, water leaves the cell, cytoplasm shrinks (plasmolysis).

E. Active Transport

Active Transport: The passage of a substance (like salt/ions) from its lower to higher concentration (opposite to what happens in diffusion), using energy (ATP) from the cell, through a living cell membrane.

Minerals (nitrates, sulphates, potassium) exist as ions in the soil. Because their concentration is already higher inside the root hairs (to maintain osmotic pressure for water absorption), they cannot diffuse in. They must be "forcibly" carried inward against the concentration gradient using energy supplied by the cell.

4. Turgidity, Flaccidity, and Plasmolysis

  • Turgidity: The state of a cell when it cannot accommodate any more water (it is fully distended).
    • Turgor Pressure: The outward pressure exerted by the cell contents (protoplasm) on the cell wall.
    • Wall Pressure: The inward pressure exerted by the rigid cell wall on the cell contents (counter-balancing turgor pressure).
  • Plasmolysis: The contraction of the cytoplasm from the cell wall caused by the withdrawal of water when a cell is placed in a strong (hypertonic) solution.
  • Flaccidity: The condition in which the cell content is shrunken and the cell is limp (no more "tight"). It is the reverse of turgidity.
  • Deplasmolysis: The recovery or reversal of plasmolysis when the flaccid cell is returned to plain water (hypotonic solution) before it dies.

Practical Applications of Plasmolysis:

  • Salting of meat or pickles: Kills bacteria by drawing water out of their cells via exosmosis.
  • Killing weeds: Sprinkling excessive salt around weeds creates a hypertonic environment, causing exosmosis, plasmolysis, and death of the plant.
  • Grapes vs Raisins: Raisins swell in water due to endosmosis (and imbibition). Grapes shrink in salt solution due to exosmosis.

5. Uses of Turgidity to Plants

  1. Rigidity to Soft Tissues: Provides stiffness to leaves. Wilting occurs when transpiration exceeds water absorption, causing leaves to droop.
  2. Pushing through hard ground: Turgor pressure provides the force for germinating seedlings and mushrooms to crack through asphalt or concrete.
  3. Root Pressure: The heavy pressure developed in roots due to continuous inward cell-to-cell osmosis. This force helps push water up the stem.
  4. Opening and Closing of Stomata: When guard cells become turgid (due to endosmosis from nearby cells after glucose synthesis), they arch outward, opening the pore. When flaccid (at night), they straighten, closing the pore.
  5. Turgor Movements: The rapid drooping of leaves in the sensitive plant (Mimosa pudica) when touched is due to a sudden loss of turgor in the pulvinus (swollen base of the petiole). Similar movements govern the closing of insectivorous plant leaves and the bending of sunflowers towards the sun.

6. Upward Movement of Sap and Essential Experiments

The ascent of sap (water and dissolved minerals) from roots to leaves involves four main forces:

  1. Root Pressure: Cell-to-cell osmosis pushes water into the xylem. Excessive root pressure causes Guttation (loss of water as droplets along leaf margins in plants like tomato, grass, banana). Exudation of sap from a cut stem is called Bleeding.
  2. Capillarity: Narrow diameter of xylem vessels causes water to rise due to surface tension.
  3. Transpiration Pull: The primary force in tall trees. As water evaporates from leaves, a suction force is created.
  4. Adhesion and Cohesion: Cohesion is the molecular attraction between water molecules keeping them in a continuous unbroken column. Adhesion is the attraction causing water to stick to the walls of the xylem vessels.

Key Experiments to Remember:

  • Thistle Funnel Experiment (Osmosis): Sugar solution in a thistle funnel covered with cellophane paper (semi-permeable), placed in a beaker of water. The level in the funnel rises due to endosmosis. If muslin cloth is used, sugar diffuses out (freely permeable). If rubber is used, no movement (impermeable).
  • Roots absorb water: A plant in a test tube with water, covered by oil (to prevent evaporation). The water level drops, proving roots absorb water.
  • Xylem conducts water: A young balsam plant placed in eosin (pink) solution. After hours, a transverse section of the stem shows only the xylem vessels stained pink.
  • Ringing/Girdling Experiment (Phloem conducts food): A ring of bark (phloem) is removed, leaving xylem intact. The stem above the ring swells due to accumulated food moving downward. The plant survives for a while but roots eventually die. If xylem is removed instead, the leaves wilt immediately because water supply is cut.

Exam Practice Questions (PYQ Trends)

Give Reasons

REASONING Why are weeds killed in a playground by sprinkling excessive salts around their base?

Ans: Adding excessive salt creates a highly hypertonic solution in the soil outside the roots. This causes exosmosis from the roots of the weeds, leading to plasmolysis. The root cells lose their turgidity and eventually die, killing the weed.

Differentiate

CONCEPT CHECK Differentiate between Plasmolysis and Deplasmolysis.

Ans: Plasmolysis is the shrinkage of the cytoplasm away from the cell wall when placed in a hypertonic solution (due to exosmosis). Deplasmolysis is the recovery of a plasmolysed cell to its normal turgid state when returned to a hypotonic solution or pure water (due to endosmosis).

Identify Structure/Process

NAME THE FOLLOWING The pressure responsible for the movement of water molecules across the cortical cells of the root.

Ans: Root Pressure (driven by cell-to-cell osmosis).

Application

MECHANISM Why does an animal cell burst when placed in distilled water, but a plant cell does not?

Ans: When placed in distilled water, endosmosis occurs. An animal cell bursts because it lacks a rigid cell wall to withstand the increasing turgor pressure. A plant cell has a rigid, cellulose cell wall that exerts an inward wall pressure, counterbalancing the turgor pressure and preventing the cell from bursting.
