The Circulatory System

ICSE Class 10 Biology • Chapter 07

1. Body Fluids & Need for Transport

Every organ requires the involvement of circulating fluids to transport substances. The digestive system transports nutrients, the respiratory system transports $O_2$ and $CO_2$, the excretory system transports urea, and the endocrine system transports hormones.

Principal Fluids in Our Body:

  1. Blood: Contained in the heart and blood vessels (arteries, veins, capillaries).
  2. Tissue Fluid: Occupying spaces between cells in the organs.
  3. Lymph: Contained within lymph vessels and lymphatic organs (like the spleen and tonsils).

2. Blood and its Properties

Blood is a connective tissue which is never stationary. It flows in a closed vascular system.

  • Colour: Bright red in arteries (oxygenated) and dark red in veins (deoxygenated).
  • Volume: An average adult human contains 5 to 6 litres of blood.
  • Taste & pH: Saltish taste. It is slightly alkaline with a pH of 7.3 to 7.45.

Functions of Blood

A. Transport:

  • Digested food from the alimentary canal to tissues.
  • Oxygen from lungs to tissues (as unstable oxyhaemoglobin).
  • Carbon dioxide from tissues to lungs (as carbamino-haemoglobin and dissolved in plasma).
  • Excretory material to liver/kidney/skin.
  • Hormones and distribution of body heat.

B. Protection:

  • Forms a clot to prevent blood loss and entry of disease-causing germs.
  • White blood cells protect the body from diseases by engulfing bacteria (phagocytosis).
  • Produces antitoxins and antibodies.

3. Composition of Blood

Blood consists of two main parts:

  1. Plasma (55-60%): The light-yellow alkaline fluid part. Contains 90-92% water, 7-8% proteins, 1% inorganic salts (NaCl, $NaHCO_3$), and traces of glucose, amino acids, fibrinogen, hormones, and urea. (Note: Plasma without fibrinogen is called Serum.)
  2. Cellular Elements (40-45%): Red blood cells, White blood cells, and Platelets.

A. Red Blood Cells (RBCs or Erythrocytes)

  • Minute, biconcave disc-like structures, flat in the centre and thick at the periphery.
  • About 7 microns in diameter. Small size and concavity provide a large surface area for absorbing oxygen.
  • Small size enables them to travel through very fine capillaries in a single file.
  • Adult male has ~5 million RBCs / $mm^3$; Adult female has ~4.5 million RBCs / $mm^3$.

Haemoglobin ($Hb$) & Carbon Monoxide Poisoning:

Haemoglobin is the respiratory pigment formed of an iron-containing part (haemin) and a protein (globin). It readily combines with oxygen to form unstable oxyhaemoglobin.

Carbon Monoxide Poisoning: Haemoglobin has a very strong affinity for carbon monoxide ($CO$), forming a highly stable compound called carboxyhaemoglobin ($HbCO$). This cuts down the oxygen-carrying capacity of the blood, resulting in death (e.g., from furnaces burning in closed rooms).

Deficient, but More Efficient!

Mature mammalian RBCs are devoid of certain organelles, making them highly efficient at transporting oxygen:

  1. No Nucleus: Makes the cells biconcave, increasing surface area for oxygen absorption and allowing more RBCs to be accommodated in the same space.
  2. No Mitochondria: They cannot use oxygen for cellular respiration for themselves. Thus, all absorbed oxygen is delivered to tissues.
  3. No Endoplasmic Reticulum: Increases the flexibility of RBCs for movement through narrow capillaries.

Life cycle of RBCs: Produced in the marrow of long bones (adults), liver and spleen (embryo). Average life span is 120 days. They are destroyed in the spleen, liver, and bone marrow. The iron part is retained, while the rest is excreted as bilirubin (bile pigment).

B. White Blood Cells (WBCs or Leukocytes)

  • Differ from RBCs by having a nucleus and no haemoglobin.
  • Number is less, usually 4000-8000 per $mm^3$ of blood.
  • Most are amoeboid and can squeeze through capillary walls into tissues (a process called Diapedesis).
  • Phagocytosis: Neutrophils engulf solid particles like bacteria.
  • Inflammation: Local reaction with swelling, redness, heat, and pain due to increased blood supply and diapedesis.
  • Pus: A mixture of destroyed bacterial cells and dead WBCs at a wound.

Types of WBCs:

  1. Granular: Neutrophils (phagocytosis), Eosinophils (allergy), Basophils (release histamine).
  2. Agranular: Lymphocytes (produce antibodies), Monocytes (macrophages/phagocytosis).

C. Blood Platelets (Thrombocytes)

  • Minute, oval or round, non-nucleated fragments derived from giant cells called megakaryocytes in the bone marrow.
  • Normal count: 2,00,000 to 4,00,000 per $mm^3$.
  • Life span: 3 to 5 days. Play a major role in blood clotting.

4. Coagulation of Blood (Blood Clotting)

When a blood vessel is cut, blood escapes but soon forms a clot to stop bleeding.

Mechanism of Blood Clotting:

  1. Injured tissue cells and platelets disintegrate to release the enzyme Thrombokinase (Thromboplastin).
  2. Thrombokinase, in the presence of Calcium ions ($Ca^{++}$), converts the inactive plasma protein Prothrombin into active Thrombin.
  3. Thrombin acts as an enzyme to convert soluble plasma protein Fibrinogen into insoluble Fibrin.
  4. Fibrin forms a sticky network of threads in which RBCs and WBCs are entangled, forming the Clot (Thrombus).

5. Blood Groups and Rh Factor

Discovered by Karl Landsteiner. Human blood is classified based on specific proteins (antigens) on the RBCs.

  • ABO System: Blood groups A, B, AB, and O.
    • Universal Donor: Type O (has no antigens, can be given to anyone).
    • Universal Recipient: Type AB (has no antibodies, can receive from anyone).
  • Rh Factor: An antigen first discovered in the Rhesus monkey. People with it are $Rh^+$; those without are $Rh^-$. An $Rh^-$ person given $Rh^+$ blood will develop antibodies, which can be fatal upon subsequent transfusions. It also causes complications in pregnancy ($Rh^-$ mother, $Rh^+$ fetus).

7. The Heart

Located in the center of the chest cavity, slightly tilted to the left. The size of one's closed fist. Protected by a double-walled membrane called the pericardium (contains pericardial fluid to reduce friction and protect from shocks).

Structure:

  • Four Chambers: Two upper Auricles (Atria) (thin walls, receive blood) and two lower Ventricles (thick walls, pump blood). The Left Ventricle has the thickest wall because it pumps blood to the extremities of the entire body.
  • Septum: A muscular wall separating the left and right sides to prevent mixing of oxygenated and deoxygenated blood.
  • Valves: Ensure unidirectional flow.
    • Right Atrio-ventricular Valve (Tricuspid Valve): Has 3 flaps. Located between right auricle and right ventricle.
    • Left Atrio-ventricular Valve (Bicuspid / Mitral Valve): Has 2 flaps. Located between left auricle and left ventricle.
    • Semilunar Valves: Pocket-like valves at the base of the pulmonary artery and aorta.
    • Chordae Tendineae: Tendinous cords holding the flaps of the tricuspid/bicuspid valves to the papillary muscles, preventing them from turning inside out during ventricular contraction.

8. Blood Circulation (Double Circulation)

Double Circulation: Blood flows twice through the heart to complete one full circuit around the body. It consists of:

  1. Pulmonary Circulation: Path of blood from Right Ventricle $\rightarrow$ Lungs $\rightarrow$ Left Auricle. (For oxygenation).
  2. Systemic Circulation: Path of blood from Left Ventricle $\rightarrow$ Body Organs $\rightarrow$ Right Auricle. (For distribution).

Hepatic Portal System: Instead of going directly to the heart, veins from the stomach and intestines unite to form the Hepatic Portal Vein, which enters the Liver. The liver acts as a checkpoint to regulate glucose, store nutrients, and detoxify poisons before the blood reaches the general circulation via the hepatic vein.

9. The Cardiac Cycle & Heart Sounds

  • Systole: Contraction phase.
  • Diastole: Relaxation/Expansion phase.

The sequence of events in a single heartbeat (approx. 0.85 sec) is called the cardiac cycle.

  • Pacemaker (Sino-atrial node - SAN): Located in the walls of the right auricle. It initiates the heartbeat impulse.
  • Heart Sounds:
    • LUBB: Produced when the Atrio-ventricular (tricuspid/bicuspid) valves close sharply at the start of ventricular systole.
    • DUPP: Produced when the Semilunar valves close sharply at the start of ventricular diastole.

10. Blood Pressure and Pulse

  • Pulse: The alternate expansion and elastic recoil of the wall of the artery during ventricular systole. Usually felt at the wrist. Normal is 72 per min.
  • Blood Pressure: The pressure exerted by blood on the walls of blood vessels. Measured with a Sphygmomanometer.
    • Systolic Pressure: Upper limit (approx. 120 mm Hg) when ventricles contract.
    • Diastolic Pressure: Lower limit (approx. 80 mm Hg) when ventricles relax.

11. Tissue Fluid and Lymphatic System

As blood flows through capillaries, plasma (without large proteins) and leukocytes leak out into the intercellular spaces to form Tissue Fluid. It bathes the cells, supplying O2 and picking up CO2.

Some of this fluid enters closed-ended lymph capillaries and is now called Lymph. It lacks RBCs and platelets, contains fewer proteins, and flows in one direction (towards the heart) via lymph vessels.

  • Lymph Nodes: Act as filters and produce lymphocytes to destroy germs.
  • Spleen: A large lymphatic organ. Acts as a blood reservoir, produces lymphocytes, and destroys old worn-out RBCs.

Exam Practice Questions (PYQ Trends)

Give Reasons

REASONING Why do mature mammalian erythrocytes lack a nucleus and mitochondria?

Ans: The lack of a nucleus makes the RBC biconcave, increasing its surface area for better oxygen absorption and allowing more RBCs to fit in the same space. The lack of mitochondria prevents the RBC from using up the oxygen it carries for its own cellular respiration, ensuring all oxygen reaches the tissues.

Differentiate

CONCEPT CHECK Differentiate between Artery and Vein based on the presence of valves and direction of blood flow.

Ans: Arteries carry blood away from the heart and do not have valves. Veins carry blood towards the heart and have pocket-shaped valves to prevent backflow of blood.

Heart Function

MECHANISM Why is the wall of the left ventricle thicker than the right ventricle?

Ans: The right ventricle only pumps blood a short distance to the lungs, whereas the left ventricle must pump blood at a high pressure to all the distant extremities of the entire body.

Identify Structure

NAME THE FOLLOWING The tendinous cords which hold the flaps of the bicuspid and tricuspid valves in position.

Ans: Chordae tendineae.
