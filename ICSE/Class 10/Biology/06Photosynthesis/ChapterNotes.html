Photosynthesis — Provider of Food for All

ICSE Class 10 Biology • Chapter 06

1. What is Photosynthesis?

Photosynthesis: The process by which living plant cells, containing chlorophyll, produce food substances (glucose and starch) from carbon dioxide and water, by using light energy. Plants release oxygen as a waste product during photosynthesis.

Importance of Photosynthesis:

  1. Food for all: Ultimate source of energy and food for all living beings (directly for herbivores, indirectly for carnivores).
  2. Oxygen to breathe in: The only biological process that releases oxygen into the atmosphere.

2. Chlorophyll — The Vital Plant Pigment

Chlorophyll is the green colouring matter found in microscopic cell organelles called chloroplasts.

  • Chloroplast Structure: Minute oval bodies bounded by a double membrane. Their interior contains closely packed flattened sacs called thylakoids arranged in piles called grana lying in a colourless ground substance called stroma.
  • Distribution: Mainly contained in the mesophyll cells (palisade and spongy) of leaves. There are 40-50 chloroplasts in a single cell.

Chlorophylls absorb light at both ends of the visible spectrum i.e. BLUE and RED light, and reflect away the GREEN light (which is why chlorophyll appears green). The absorbed blue and red lights are most effective for photosynthesis.

3. Regulation of Stomatal Opening

Stomata are minute openings on the lower surface of a leaf used to let in $CO_2$. There are two theories about their opening and closing mechanism:

1. Sugar Concentration Theory (Old)

During daytime, guard cells begin photosynthesis and produce sugar (glucose). This increases the osmotic pressure which draws in water from adjoining cells due to endosmosis. The guard cells become turgid and bulge outwards due to their thin outer wall, widening the stomatal opening. At night, water is drawn out, making them flaccid and closing the stoma.

2. Potassium ($K^+$) Ion Concentration Theory (Recent)

During daytime, chloroplasts in guard cells photosynthesize leading to ATP production. This ATP is used to actively pump potassium ions ($K^+$) of the adjacent cells into the guard cells. Increased $K^+$ concentration makes them hypertonic, drawing in water, making them turgid and opening the pore. The reverse happens at night.

4. The Process of Photosynthesis

Mesophyll cells are the principal centres. Light energy is trapped by chlorophyll in the palisade cells. $CO_2$ enters by diffusion through stomata. Water is taken up by roots and distributed by xylem.

$$6CO_2 + 12H_2O \xrightarrow{\text{light energy & chlorophyll}} C_6H_{12}O_6 + 6H_2O + 6O_2\uparrow$$

5. Two Main Phases of Photosynthesis

A. Light-Dependent Phase (Photochemical Phase)

Occurs in the thylakoids (grana) of the chloroplasts. Light plays the key role. Occurs in two main steps:

  1. Activation of chlorophyll: On exposure to light, chlorophyll is activated by absorbing photons (smallest unit of light energy).
  2. Splitting of water (Photolysis): The absorbed energy is used to split the water molecule ($H_2O$) into hydrogen and oxygen.
    $$2H_2O \xrightarrow{\text{energy of 4 photons, grana}} 4H^+ + 4e^- + O_2$$

End results of Photolysis:

  • The hydrogen ions ($H^+$) are picked up by NADP to form NADPH.
  • The oxygen ($O$) component is given out as molecular oxygen ($O_2$).
  • The electrons ($e^-$) are used to convert ADP into energy-rich ATP by adding an inorganic phosphate ($P_i$). This process is called Photophosphorylation.

B. Light-Independent Phase (Dark Phase / Biosynthetic Phase)

Occurs in the stroma of chloroplasts. These reactions do not require light energy and occur simultaneously with the light reaction. (The old term "dark phase" did not mean it occurs at night, but rather that it is independent of light).

  • The hydrogen of NADPH is used to combine with $CO_2$ using ATP energy to ultimately produce glucose ($C_6H_{12}O_6$).
  • Several glucose molecules are transformed to produce one molecule of starch; this process is called polymerisation.

6. Adaptations in Leaf for Photosynthesis

  1. Large surface area: For maximum light absorption.
  2. Leaf arrangement: At the right angle to the light source.
  3. Cuticle and upper epidermis: Are transparent and waterproof to allow light to enter freely.
  4. Numerous stomata: Allow rapid exchange of gases.
  5. Thinness of leaves: Reduces the distance between cells facilitating rapid transport.
  6. Chloroplasts concentrated in upper layers: To obtain light energy quickly.
  7. Extensive vein system: For rapid transport to and from mesophyll cells.

7. End Results of the Products

  1. Glucose: Immediately consumed, stored as insoluble starch, converted into sucrose, or used to synthesize fats/proteins.
  2. Water: Re-utilized in the continuance of photosynthesis.
  3. Oxygen: Some used in leaf respiration, but the major portion diffuses out through stomata.

Translocation: Food is manufactured in the leaf and converted into insoluble starch for temporary storage. At night, it is reconverted into soluble sugar and transported through the phloem to other parts of the plant.

8. Factors Affecting Photosynthesis

A. External Factors

  • Light Intensity: Rate increases with intensity up to a certain limit ($0.02\% \ CO_2$), then stabilizes.
  • $CO_2$ Concentration: If increased further (e.g. to $0.05\%$), rate increases again before stabilizing.
  • Temperature: Rises up to an optimum temperature of $35^\circ C$. A rise of $10^\circ C$ doubles the rate. Above $40^\circ C$, the enzymes are destroyed and the process stops.
  • Water Content: Scarcity reduces absorption, leading to stomatal closure, which decreases $CO_2$ intake.

B. Internal Factors

  • Chlorophyll: Nutritional deficiencies cause loss of chlorophyll, dropping energy trapping.
  • Protoplasm: Dehydration or accumulation of carbohydrates reduces the rate.
  • Structure of Leaf: Cuticle thickness and stomata distribution influence light and $CO_2$ entry.

9. Experiments on Photosynthesis

Destarching: A plant is destarched by placing it in the dark for 24 to 48 hours. This ensures leaves are free from starch before starting an experiment.

Iodine Test for Starch:

  1. Dip leaf in boiling water for a minute to kill the cells.
  2. Boil the leaf in methylated spirit over a water bath to remove chlorophyll (leaf becomes hard & brittle).
  3. Place it in hot water to soften it.
  4. Pour Iodine solution. Presence of starch gives a blue-black colour. Without starch, it shows brown colour.
  • Exp 1 (Chlorophyll is necessary): Use a variegated leaf (Coleus/Geranium). Only the green parts turn blue-black.
  • Exp 2 (Sunlight is necessary): Cover part of a leaf with black paper having a cut design. Only exposed parts turn blue-black.
  • Exp 3 ($CO_2$ is necessary): Half a leaf is inserted into a conical flask containing Potassium Hydroxide (KOH) which absorbs $CO_2$. The part inside remains brown, the part outside turns blue-black.
  • Exp 4 (Oxygen is produced): Place Elodea or Hydrilla in a beaker under an inverted funnel with a test-tube over the stem. Bubbles of oxygen collect in the tube and rekindle a glowing splinter.

10. Carbon Cycle & Importance

  • Food Chain: Sequence of organisms feeding on one another. The starting point is always a plant (producer).
  • Carbon Cycle: Series of chemical reactions in which carbon is removed from the air ($CO_2$), used by living organisms, and returned to the air (via respiration, decay, and combustion).

Exam Practice Questions (PYQ Trends)

Give Reasons

REASONING Why is it necessary to destarch a plant before performing an experiment on photosynthesis?

Ans: To ensure that any starch found in the leaves after the experiment is produced solely during the course of the experiment.

Identify

NAME THE FOLLOWING The chemical used to remove $CO_2$ from inside a flask in photosynthesis experiments.

Ans: Potassium Hydroxide (KOH).

Experiment Based (Iodine Test)

CONCEPT CHECK Why is the leaf boiled in methylated spirit in a water bath?

Ans: To dissolve and remove the green pigment chlorophyll, making the leaf pale-white so that the colour change from the iodine test can be clearly observed. A water bath is used because alcohol is highly flammable.

Reaction Mechanisms

MECHANISM Mention the exact site of the light-dependent and light-independent phases of photosynthesis.

Ans: Light-dependent phase occurs in the thylakoids (grana) of chloroplasts. Light-independent phase occurs in the stroma of chloroplasts.
