Genetics - Some Basic Fundamentals

ICSE Class 10 Biology • Chapter 03

1. What is Genetics?

Genetics: The study of transmission of body features (both similarities and differences) from parents to offspring and the laws relating to such transmission.

  • Gregor Mendel (An Austrian Monk) is known as the Father of Genetics.

Modern Applications of Genetics

  1. Genetic Engineering: The technique in which the genetic constitution of an organism (bacterium) is altered by introducing new genes into its chromosomes. E.g., the hormone insulin-producing gene of mammals has been successfully introduced in certain bacteria to produce insulin in large quantities.
  2. Genetic Counselling: Newly married couples consult a specialist regarding possibilities of any undesirable traits their children might inherit (e.g., haemophilia, thalassaemia, sickle cell anaemia).

2. Heredity and Variations

Heredity: Transmission of genetically based characteristics from parents to offspring.

Variations: Small differences among individuals within a species or population.

Like begets like: It means that young ones look like their parents (Cats produce cats, mango seed germinates into mango tree).

Characters and Traits

Any inheritable feature is a character. The alternative forms of a character are called traits.

Character Dominant Trait Recessive Trait
Eye colour Brown Blue
Tongue Rolling Non-rolling
Hand use Right-handedness Left-handedness
Ear lobe Free Attached
Colour vision Normal Colour blind (red-green)

Some uncommon hereditary traits: Polydactyly (Extra fingers & toes) is Dominant. Albinism (Total absence of skin pigment) is Recessive.

3. Chromosomes and Genes

Chromosomes are only visible when a cell nucleus is about to divide. The chromosome number is constant for the individuals of a species. Humans have 46 chromosomes (23 pairs).

Autosomes and Sex Chromosomes

  • Autosomes: Chromosome pairs numbered 1-22 which have identical chromosomes.
  • Sex Chromosomes: The 23rd pair. Females have XX (similar partners). Males have XY (dissimilar, Y is much smaller than X).

Sex Determination

The sex of the child depends upon the kind of sperm that fertilises the egg. All eggs are alike (contain X), but sperms are either X-bearing or Y-bearing.

  • Egg (X) + Sperm (X) → XX (Female / Daughter)
  • Egg (X) + Sperm (Y) → XY (Male / Son)

Genes and their Alleles

Genes: Specific parts (DNA segments) of a chromosome, which determine the hereditary characteristics.

Genome: The full complement of DNA (including all genes and intergenic regions) of an organism.

Alleles: Alternative forms of a gene, occupying the same position (locus) on homologous chromosomes and affecting the same characteristic but in different ways.

Out of two alleles of a gene, one is dominant (superruling) and the other is recessive (subordinate). Dominant genes are represented by capital letters (e.g. 'R'), recessive by small letters (e.g. 'r').

Genotype and Phenotype

  • Genotype: The set of genes present in the cells of an organism (the genetic constitution). E.g. RR, Rr, rr.
  • Phenotype: The observable characteristic which is genetically controlled. E.g. Tongue roller.

Types of Genotypes:

  • Homozygous dominant: Similar pair of dominant alleles (RR).
  • Heterozygous dominant: Dissimilar pair of alleles (Rr).
  • Homozygous recessive: Similar pair of recessive alleles (rr).

4. Sex-Linked Inheritance

Sex-linked inheritance: The appearance of a trait which is due to the presence of an allele exclusively either on the X chromosome or on the Y chromosome.

Certain disorders like haemophilia and colour-blindness are caused by recessive genes on the X chromosome. They are more common in males because a male needs only one defective gene (X°Y) to suffer, whereas a female needs two (X°X°). A female with one defective gene (XX°) is a normal carrier.

Criss-Cross Inheritance

Inheritance of X-linked genes is passed from Mother to Son and Father to Daughter. A colour blind father passes the defective gene to his daughter (making her a carrier), and she may pass it to her son.

5. Mendel's Experiments on Inheritance

Mendel selected the garden pea (Pisum sativum) for three reasons:

  1. Many varieties were available in alternative forms of a character.
  2. Varieties were available in pure forms that bred true.
  3. Peas are normally self-pollinated but can be cross-pollinated artificially.

A. Monohybrid Cross

A cross between two pure breeding different varieties taking the alternative traits of one single character.

  • Example: Pure tall (TT) crossed with pure dwarf (tt).
  • F1 generation: All hybrid tall (Tt).
  • F2 generation (on selfing F1):
    • Phenotypic ratio: 3 : 1 (3 Tall : 1 Dwarf)
    • Genotypic ratio: 1 : 2 : 1 (1 TT : 2 Tt : 1 tt)

B. Dihybrid Cross

A cross considering alternative traits of two different characters.

  • Example: Pure round yellow (RRYY) crossed with wrinkled green (rryy).
  • F2 generation Phenotypic ratio: 9 : 3 : 3 : 1 (9 Round Yellow, 3 Round Green, 3 Wrinkled Yellow, 1 Wrinkled Green).

6. Mendel's Laws of Inheritance

  1. Law of Dominance: Out of a pair of contrasting characters present together, only one is able to express itself (dominant) while the other remains suppressed (recessive).
  2. Law of Segregation (Law of purity of gametes): The two members of a pair of factors separate during the formation of gametes. They do not blend but segregate.
  3. Law of Independent Assortment: When there are two pairs of contrasting characters, the distribution of the members of one pair into the gametes is independent of the distribution of the other pair.

7. Mutation

Mutation: A sudden change in one or more genes, or in the number or structure of chromosomes.

Mutation alters the hereditary material. Examples:

  • Sickle cell anaemia: A blood disease caused by a gene mutation resulting in sickle-shaped RBCs.
  • Radioactive radiations: Can alter gene structure (e.g., atomic explosion during WW-II in Japan led to deformities persisting today).

Exam Practice Questions (PYQ Trends)

Frequently Asked (1 Mark)

NAME THE FOLLOWING The alternative forms of a gene occupying the same position on homologous chromosomes.

Ans: Alleles

Give Reasons

REASONING Why are males more prone to colour blindness than females?

Ans: Colour blindness is an X-linked recessive disorder. Males have only one X chromosome, so a single defective gene causes the disease. Females have two X chromosomes and need two defective genes to suffer from it.

Ratios

CONCEPT CHECK State the phenotypic and genotypic ratios of a monohybrid cross in the F2 generation.

Ans: Phenotypic ratio = 3 : 1. Genotypic ratio = 1 : 2 : 1.
