Structure of Chromosomes, Cell Cycle and Cell Division

ICSE Class 10 Biology • Chapter 02

1. Structure of Chromosomes

Chromosomes: Highly condensed coiled chromatin fibres. When a normal unstained living cell is observed under a light microscope, its nucleus may not appear to contain any particular thing inside. But when stained with suitable dyes, they become noticeable.

Discovery of Chromosomes

  • First discovered in 1882 by a German Scientist, Walther Fleming.
  • He noticed them in the rapidly dividing cells of salamander larvae.
  • He called their division mitosis (literally meaning "thread").

A. Chromatin vs Chromosomes

The chromatin material that constitutes the fibre is formed of two substances:

  • DNA (Deoxyribonucleic acid) - about 40%
  • Histones (a particular type of proteins) - about 60%

B. Molecular Structure (The Nucleosome)

The DNA strand winds around a core of eight histone molecules. This core can be imagined like a football, around which a long rope is wound with one or two loops. Each such complex is called a nucleosome.

  • A single human chromosome may have about a million nucleosomes!
  • The entire chromatin fibre is coiled and super-coiled (like a telephone cord).

C. Structure of DNA

  • Studied by Rosalind Franklin in 1953; structure worked out by Watson and Crick in the same year.
  • DNA is a very large single molecule (a macromolecule).
  • It is composed of two complementary strands wound around each other in a double helix.
  • Each DNA strand is composed of repeating nucleotides which are made of three components:
    1. Phosphate
    2. Sugar (pentose)
    3. Nitrogenous base

The Nitrogenous Bases:

  • Adenine (A) pairs with Thymine (T) with two hydrogen bonds.
  • Guanine (G) pairs with Cytosine (C) with three hydrogen bonds.

D. What are Genes?

Genes: Specific sequences of nucleotides on a chromosome, that encode particular proteins which express in the form of some particular feature of the body.

2. Cell Division - New Cells from Existing Ones

New cells need to be produced for:

  1. Growth: Single cell (fertilized egg) divides repeatedly to form tissues and organs.
  2. Replacement: E.g., 20 million red blood cells are destroyed every minute and replaced by new cells from bone marrow.
  3. Repair: Accidental injuries, cuts, fractures are repaired by dividing cells covering gaps.
  4. Reproduction: Amoeba/bacteria divide to produce two similar cells. In higher forms, meiosis produces sperms and eggs.

How old are some of our body cells?

  • Cells of eye lens, nerve cells of cerebral cortex and most muscle cells last a life time (not replaced once dead).
  • Skin cells are replaced every two weeks or so.
  • Red blood cells last for about 120 days and are replaced.
  • Bone cells are replaced every 10 years or so in adults.
  • Liver cells are replaced every 300-500 days.

3. Types of Cell Division

There are two types of cell division:

  1. Mitosis: Cell division leading to growth and development.
  2. Meiosis: Cell division leading to the production of gametes (sex cells).

A. Mitosis (Karyokinesis)

Mitosis (mitos: thread): One parent cell divides into two identical daughter cells. The most important aspect is that the same normal chromosome number is maintained at each cell division.

Phases of Mitosis

Karyokinesis (division of nucleus) occurs in four main phases:

1. Prophase (pro: first)

  • Chromosomes become short and thick and are clearly visible.
  • Each chromosome has already duplicated to form two chromatids attached at the centromere.
  • The centrosome (in animal cells) splits into two along with simultaneous duplication of centrioles. They move apart to opposite "poles" forming an aster.
  • Spindle fibres appear between the two daughter centrioles.
  • Nuclear membrane and nucleolus disappear.

2. Metaphase (meta: after)

  • Duplicated chromosomes arrange on the equatorial plane.
  • Each chromosome gets attached to a spindle fibre by its centromere.

3. Anaphase (ana: up/back)

  • The centromere attaching the two chromatids divides.
  • Sister chromatids separate and are drawn apart towards opposite poles by the contraction of spindle fibres.
  • A furrow starts in the cell membrane at the middle (in animal cells).

4. Telophase (telo: end)

  • Each chromatid lengthens, becomes thinner and turns into a network of chromatin threads.
  • Nuclear membrane and nucleoli reappear.
  • Spindle fibres disappear.

B. Cytokinesis (Division of Cytoplasm)

At the end of telophase, a cleavage furrow deepens in the animal cell and separates the two daughter cells. In plant cells, cytokinesis happens by cell plate formation at the equatorial plane.

Feature Mitosis in Animals Mitosis in Plants
Asters Asters are formed. Asters are not formed.
Cytokinesis By furrowing of cytoplasm. By cell plate formation.
Occurrence Occurs in most tissues throughout the body. Occurs mainly at growing tips and sides.

Significance of Mitosis: 1. Growth (increase in body size), 2. Repair of damaged tissues, 3. Replacement of old/dead cells, 4. Asexual reproduction (e.g., Amoeba), 5. Maintains same chromosome number in daughter cells.

4. The Cell Cycle

Cell Cycle: The sequence of events involving growth and division of a cell. "Divide, grow and redivide".

The interphase itself has three phases:

  • First Growth Phase ($G_1$): RNA and proteins are synthesised, volume of cytoplasm increases. Mitochondria and chloroplasts divide (since they have their own DNA and ribosomes).
  • Synthesis Phase ($S$): More DNA is synthesised, the chromosomes are duplicated.
  • Second Growth Phase ($G_2$): Shorter growth phase, RNA and proteins necessary for cell division continue to be synthesised.

5. Meiosis (Reduction Division)

Meiosis: The kind of cell division that produces the sex cells or gametes. It takes place in reproductive organs (testis and ovary in humans, anthers and ovary in plants).

The most significant aspect of meiosis is that the number of chromosomes is halved. (Diploid $2n \rightarrow$ Haploid $n$). Meiosis is completed in two divisions: meiosis I (reduction division) and meiosis II (mitotic division).

Significance of Meiosis

  1. Chromosome number is halved: So that on fertilization, the normal diploid number ($2n$) is restored.
  2. Mixing up of genes: Provides genetic recombination in two ways:
    • Maternal and paternal chromosomes get mixed up during reduction division.
    • Crossing over: Chromatid material gets exchanged between two members of a homologous pair resulting in genetic variations (Chiasma formation).

6. Mitosis vs Meiosis

Feature Mitosis Meiosis
Where it occurs Somatic (body) cells. Reproductive cells.
What for Growth and replacement. Gamete formation.
Daughter Cells Produced Two daughter cells. Four daughter cells.
Chromosomes Passed Full set (Diploid, $2n$). Half the number (Haploid, $n$).
Number of Nuclear Divisions Single nuclear division. Two nuclear divisions.
Identity of Chromosomes Identical to parent cell. Randomly assorted (genetic variations).
