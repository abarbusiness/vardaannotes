Acids, Bases and Salts

ICSE Class 10 Chemistry • Chapter 03

1. Acids

Arrhenius Definition: A substance that produces H⁺ ions (or H₃O⁺ hydronium ions) when dissolved in water.

Examples: HCl, H₂SO₄, HNO₃, CH₃COOH

Properties of Acids

  • Sour taste
  • Turn blue litmus red
  • pH less than 7
  • React with metals to produce hydrogen gas
  • React with bases to form salt and water (neutralization)
  • Conduct electricity (electrolytes)

Strong vs Weak Acids

Strong Acids Weak Acids
Complete ionisation in water Partial ionisation in water
HCl, H₂SO₄, HNO₃ CH₃COOH, H₂CO₃, H₃PO₄
pH close to 0 pH between 4-6

Ionisation of Acids

HCl → H⁺ + Cl⁻

H₂SO₄ → 2H⁺ + SO₄²⁻

CH₃COOH ⇌ H⁺ + CH₃COO⁻ (reversible, weak acid)

2. Bases & Alkalis

Base: A substance that produces OH⁻ ions when dissolved in water OR accepts H⁺ ions.

Alkali: A base that is soluble in water (e.g., NaOH, KOH, Ca(OH)₂)

Properties of Bases

  • Bitter taste
  • Soapy/slippery feel
  • Turn red litmus blue
  • pH greater than 7
  • React with acids to form salt and water

Strong vs Weak Bases

Strong Bases Weak Bases
Complete ionisation Partial ionisation
NaOH, KOH, Ca(OH)₂ NH₄OH, Mg(OH)₂

3. The pH Scale

pH: A measure of hydrogen ion concentration in a solution.

$pH = -\log[H^+]$

Scale: 0 to 14

pH Value Nature H⁺ Concentration
0 - 6 Acidic High [H⁺]
7 Neutral [H⁺] = [OH⁻]
8 - 14 Basic/Alkaline Low [H⁺], High [OH⁻]

Lower pH = More Acidic = Higher H⁺ concentration

Higher pH = More Basic = Lower H⁺ concentration

4. Salts

Salt: An ionic compound formed by partial or complete replacement of the hydrogen ion(s) of an acid by a metal or ammonium ion.

Acid + Base → Salt + Water (Neutralisation)

Types of Salts

Type Formation pH Examples
Normal Salt Complete replacement of H⁺ Varies NaCl, Na₂SO₄
Acid Salt Partial replacement of H⁺ Acidic NaHCO₃, NaHSO₄
Basic Salt Excess base reacts Basic Pb(OH)Cl, Mg(OH)Cl

pH of Salt Solutions

Acid Type Base Type Salt pH Example
Strong Strong 7 (Neutral) NaCl
Strong Weak < 7 (Acidic) NH₄Cl
Weak Strong > 7 (Basic) CH₃COONa
Weak Weak ≈ 7 (Neutral) CH₃COONH₄

5. Methods of Preparing Salts

Method Reaction Type Example
Direct Combination Metal + Non-metal 2Na + Cl₂ → 2NaCl
Neutralisation Acid + Base HCl + NaOH → NaCl + H₂O
Displacement Metal + Acid Zn + H₂SO₄ → ZnSO₄ + H₂↑
Precipitation Two salt solutions NaCl + AgNO₃ → AgCl↓ + NaNO₃
Acid + Carbonate Acid + Carbonate 2HCl + CaCO₃ → CaCl₂ + H₂O + CO₂↑
Titration Acid + Alkali For soluble salts of weak bases

6. Water of Crystallisation

Water of Crystallisation: Fixed number of water molecules present in one formula unit of a crystalline salt.

Hydrated Salt: Contains water of crystallisation

Anhydrous Salt: Without water of crystallisation

Hydrated Salt Formula Common Name
Blue vitriol CuSO₄·5H₂O Copper sulphate
Green vitriol FeSO₄·7H₂O Ferrous sulphate
White vitriol ZnSO₄·7H₂O Zinc sulphate
Washing soda Na₂CO₃·10H₂O Sodium carbonate
Gypsum CaSO₄·2H₂O Calcium sulphate

7. Efflorescence and Deliquescence

Efflorescence Deliquescence
Loss of water of crystallisation on exposure to air Absorption of moisture from air to form solution
Salt becomes powdery Salt becomes wet/liquid
Examples: Na₂CO₃·10H₂O, CuSO₄·5H₂O Examples: NaOH, CaCl₂, conc. H₂SO₄

8. Action of Dilute Acids

On Carbonates: Salt + Water + CO₂ (brisk effervescence)

Na₂CO₃ + 2HCl → 2NaCl + H₂O + CO₂↑

On Bicarbonates: Salt + Water + CO₂

NaHCO₃ + HCl → NaCl + H₂O + CO₂↑

On Sulphites: Salt + Water + SO₂ (suffocating smell)

Na₂SO₃ + 2HCl → 2NaCl + H₂O + SO₂↑

On Sulphides: Salt + H₂S (rotten egg smell)

Na₂S + 2HCl → 2NaCl + H₂S↑
