Chapter 5: The High Court and Subordinate Courts

1. The High Court

The High Court is the highest judicial authority in a State. Every state in India has a High Court, though Parliament can establish a common High Court for two or more states (e.g., Punjab and Haryana High Court, or Bombay High Court which also covers Goa).

Composition of the High Court

  • Every High Court consists of a Chief Justice and such other judges as the President of India may from time to time appoint.
  • The number of judges varies from state to state depending on the size and population of the state.

Qualifications for a High Court Judge

Qualifications To be appointed as a Judge of a High Court, a person must:
  • Be a citizen of India.
  • Have held a judicial office in the territory of India for at least 10 years; OR
  • Have been an Advocate of a High Court for at least 10 years.

Appointment and Term of Office

  • Appointment: The Chief Justice of a High Court is appointed by the President after consulting the Chief Justice of India and the Governor of the state. Other judges are appointed by the President after consulting the CJI, the Governor, and the Chief Justice of that High Court.
  • Term: A High Court judge holds office until attaining the age of 62 years.
  • Removal: They can be removed (impeached) by the President on the same grounds and in the same manner as a judge of the Supreme Court.

Jurisdiction and Powers of the High Court

A. Original Jurisdiction

  • Disputes related to state elections (MLAs).
  • Matters concerning admiralty, will, marriage, divorce, and company laws.
  • Enforcement of Fundamental Rights: Under Article 226, the High Court has the power to issue Writs for the enforcement of Fundamental Rights and for any other purpose. (Note: The writ jurisdiction of a High Court is wider than that of the Supreme Court, as the SC issues writs only for Fundamental Rights).

B. Appellate Jurisdiction

  • Hears appeals against the judgments of Subordinate Courts in civil and criminal matters.
  • In criminal cases, if a Sessions Court awards a death sentence, it must be confirmed by the High Court before execution, even if there is no appeal.

C. Revisory Jurisdiction and Supervisory Power

  • The High Court has the power of superintendence over all courts and tribunals within its territorial jurisdiction (except military tribunals).
  • It can call for returns from such courts, make general rules for their proceedings, and transfer cases from lower courts to itself.

D. Court of Record

  • Like the Supreme Court, the judgments of the High Court are kept as records for future reference and it has the power to punish for contempt of itself.

2. Subordinate Courts

Below the High Court, there is a hierarchy of Subordinate Courts at the district level to administer justice to the people locally.

Structure of Subordinate Courts

Hierarchy The courts at the district level are divided into two main categories:
  1. Civil Courts: Deal with cases related to property, contracts, marriage, divorce, and money disputes. The highest civil court in a district is the Court of the District Judge. Below it are Courts of Sub-Judge and Munsif Courts.
  2. Criminal Courts: Deal with crimes like theft, murder, assault, etc. The highest criminal court in a district is the Court of the Sessions Judge. (Note: The District Judge acts as the Sessions Judge when hearing criminal cases). Below it are Courts of Chief Judicial Magistrate and Judicial Magistrates.

Lok Adalats (People's Courts)

Concept Lok Adalat means 'People's Court'. It is a forum where cases pending in the courts or disputes at the pre-litigation stage are settled amicably through compromise and negotiation. It provides quick, cheap, and accessible justice.

Advantages of Lok Adalats:
  • Speedy Justice: Disputes are settled quickly without lengthy court procedures.
  • Economical: There is no court fee. If a case is referred from a regular court, the fee is refunded.
  • Finality: The award (decision) made by the Lok Adalat is final and binding on both parties. No appeal lies against it in any court, which saves time and money.
  • Reduces Burden: It helps reduce the massive backlog of pending cases in regular courts.
