Chapter 2: The First World War (1914–1918)

The First World War (1914–1918), also called the Great War, was the first global-scale war involving major world powers across Europe, Asia, Africa, and even the Pacific. It resulted in massive destruction, the collapse of four empires, and completely reshaped the political map of the world.

Quick Facts Duration: 28 July 1914 – 11 November 1918  |  Allied Powers: Britain, France, Russia, Italy (from 1915), USA (from 1917), Japan  |  Central Powers: Germany, Austria-Hungary, Ottoman Empire, Bulgaria

1. Causes of the First World War — MAIN

Memory Tip MAIN = Causes of WWI:
M – Militarism  |  A – Alliance System  |  I – Imperialism  |  N – Nationalism

Militarism

  • European nations were engaged in a fierce arms race, building up massive armies and powerful navies.
  • Germany challenged Britain's naval supremacy by building a powerful fleet under Admiral Tirpitz — the Anglo-German Naval Race.
  • Military leaders had enormous influence over government policies; war was glorified as a means of national prestige.
  • France increased its military service from 2 to 3 years; Germany expanded its army significantly after 1912.
  • The military-industrial complex in all major powers made war preparations a permanent feature of national life.

Alliance System

Europe was divided into two armed camps bound by treaties of mutual defence. When one country was attacked, all its allies were automatically drawn into the war.

Imperialism

  • European powers competed intensely for colonies in Asia and Africa, creating deep rivalries.
  • First Morocco Crisis (1905): Germany challenged France's right to control Morocco to test the strength of the Anglo-French Entente. War was narrowly avoided.
  • Second Morocco Crisis (1911): Germany sent the gunboat Panther to Morocco. Again, war was averted by negotiation but tensions increased further.
  • Germany, as a late entrant into the colonial race, was jealous of the large empires of Britain and France and demanded its "place in the sun."

Nationalism

  • Pan-Slavism: Russia positioned itself as the protector of Slavic peoples (Serbs, Croats, Bulgarians) in the Balkans against Austria-Hungary.
  • Pan-Germanism: Germany's aggressive nationalism threatened neighbouring countries and challenged the existing order.
  • The Balkans were called the "Powder Keg of Europe" — a region of intense nationalist conflicts where the Ottoman Empire was collapsing and Austria-Hungary and Russia competed for influence.
  • The Balkan Wars (1912–13) had already destabilized the region before WWI.
Immediate Cause Assassination of Archduke Franz Ferdinand — 28 June 1914:
Archduke Franz Ferdinand, heir to the Austro-Hungarian throne, and his wife Sophie were assassinated while visiting Sarajevo, Bosnia. The assassin was Gavrilo Princip, a 19-year-old Bosnian Serb nationalist and member of the secret society "Black Hand" (Union or Death). Austria-Hungary blamed Serbia, issued a humiliating 10-point ultimatum, and when Serbia partially refused, declared war on Serbia on 28 July 1914. The alliance system then dragged in all major powers within days.

2. Course of the War

Western Front — Stalemate and Trench Warfare

  • Germany launched the Schlieffen Plan — rapidly invade France through neutral Belgium, then turn east to face Russia.
  • The plan failed when Belgium resisted (bringing Britain into the war) and France held at the Battle of the Marne (September 1914).
  • Both sides dug defensive trenches stretching from the English Channel to Switzerland — the war became a bloody stalemate.
  • Trench conditions were horrific: mud, rats, disease, shell shock. Soldiers lived in narrow ditches, exposed to artillery bombardment.
  • Battle of Verdun (1916): 10 months of fighting; approximately 7 lakh casualties combined. France held but at enormous cost.
  • Battle of the Somme (1916): British-led offensive; 60,000 British casualties on Day 1 alone — the bloodiest day in British military history. Tanks were used for the first time.

Eastern Front

  • Russia fought against Germany and Austria-Hungary on the vast Eastern Front.
  • Russia suffered massive losses due to poor equipment, supply problems, and incompetent leadership.
  • The Russian Revolution (February and October 1917) brought the Bolsheviks under Lenin to power. They pulled Russia out of the war.
  • Treaty of Brest-Litovsk (March 1918): Russia surrendered large territories (Poland, Baltic states, Ukraine) to Germany to end its involvement in the war.

USA Enters the War (April 1917)

  • Germany used unrestricted submarine (U-boat) warfare — attacking all ships in the Atlantic, including neutral ones.
  • The British passenger ship Lusitania was sunk in 1915 — 1,198 people killed, including 128 Americans.
  • The intercepted Zimmermann Telegram (January 1917) — Germany's Foreign Secretary Arthur Zimmermann secretly offered Mexico an alliance and help to recover Texas, New Mexico, and Arizona from the USA if Mexico attacked America.
  • President Woodrow Wilson asked Congress to declare war on Germany on 6 April 1917.
  • Fresh American troops (the "Doughboys"), supplies, and money gave the Allied Powers an overwhelming advantage.

3. End of the War — Armistice and Treaty of Versailles

The Armistice (11 November 1918)

  • By late 1918, the German military situation was hopeless. German allies were collapsing, and revolution broke out in Germany itself.
  • Kaiser William II abdicated and fled to the Netherlands.
  • Germany signed the Armistice at 11 AM on 11 November 1918 — the "eleventh hour of the eleventh day of the eleventh month." Fighting stopped on the Western Front.

Treaty of Versailles (28 June 1919)

The formal peace settlement was signed at the Hall of Mirrors, Palace of Versailles, France on 28 June 1919 — exactly 5 years after the assassination of Archduke Franz Ferdinand.

Key Terms Terms of the Treaty of Versailles:
  • War Guilt Clause (Article 231): Germany was forced to accept full blame for starting the war. This was deeply humiliating for Germans.
  • Reparations: Germany had to pay £6.6 billion (approximately $33 billion) in war damages — a crippling sum that Germany continued paying until 2010.
  • Territorial Losses: Alsace-Lorraine returned to France; the Polish Corridor separated Germany from East Prussia; Germany lost all overseas colonies (to France, Britain, Japan as "mandates").
  • Military Restrictions: German army limited to 1,00,000 soldiers; no tanks, aircraft, or submarines; Rhineland demilitarized; no air force permitted.
  • League of Nations established (ironically, Germany initially excluded from it).
Important The harsh and humiliating terms of the Treaty of Versailles created deep resentment in Germany. The "War Guilt Clause" was seen as an insult by all Germans. The crippling reparations destroyed the German economy. These conditions created fertile ground for extremist politics — Hitler exploited this resentment to rise to power, and it became a direct cause of the Second World War.

4. Formation and Objectives of the League of Nations

Concept The League of Nations was the world's first attempt at an international peacekeeping organisation — proposed by US President Woodrow Wilson in his famous Fourteen Points speech (January 1918). It was established as part of the Treaty of Versailles and came into existence on 10 January 1920. Headquarters: Geneva, Switzerland.

Formation of the League

  • Wilson's Fourteen Points (January 1918) called for a "general association of nations" to guarantee the political independence and territorial integrity of all countries — Point 14.
  • The Paris Peace Conference (1919) incorporated the League's Covenant (constitution) as the first part of the Treaty of Versailles.
  • The League began operation on 10 January 1920 with 42 founding member states.
  • Ironically, the USA never joined. The US Senate refused to ratify the Treaty of Versailles (led by Senator Henry Cabot Lodge) — America retreated into isolationism. Wilson was devastated; this was a fatal blow to the League's authority.
  • Germany was initially excluded (joined 1926); the USSR joined only in 1934 (and was expelled in 1939).

Objectives of the League of Nations

  • To preserve world peace and prevent future wars through collective security — an attack on one member would be treated as an attack on all.
  • To promote international disarmament — reducing the militarism that had caused WWI.
  • To settle international disputes peacefully through arbitration, mediation, and negotiation rather than war.
  • To promote international cooperation in economic, social, and humanitarian matters.
  • To enforce the terms of the peace treaties and protect the new international order created after WWI.
  • To protect minorities and manage the mandate system for former German and Ottoman colonies.

Structure of the League

  • Assembly: All member nations; each had one equal vote; met annually.
  • Council: 4 permanent members (Britain, France, Italy, Japan) + elected non-permanent members; dealt with crises.
  • Permanent Court of International Justice (World Court): Settled legal disputes between nations — predecessor of the ICJ.
  • Secretariat: Administrative body.
  • Specialized bodies: International Labour Organization (ILO), Health Organization (precursor to WHO), etc.

Why the League of Nations Failed

  • USA never joined — the League lacked the support of the world's most powerful nation.
  • No standing army — the League had no forces to enforce its decisions; it depended on member nations voluntarily contributing troops.
  • Unanimity rule — all major decisions required unanimous consent; easy to block.
  • Failed to stop Japan's invasion of Manchuria (1931), Italy's invasion of Abyssinia (1935), and Germany's violations of Versailles.
  • Eventually dissolved in 1946, replaced by the United Nations.

5. Consequences of the First World War

Political Consequences

  • Fall of four great empires: German Empire, Austro-Hungarian Empire, Russian Empire, and Ottoman Empire.
  • New nations emerged from the ruins: Poland, Czechoslovakia, Yugoslavia, Hungary, Austria, Finland, Estonia, Latvia, Lithuania.
  • The League of Nations was established in 1920 to prevent future wars (proposed by US President Woodrow Wilson in his 14 Points).
  • USA emerged as the world's leading economic and military power.
  • Russia became the world's first communist state after the 1917 Revolution.

Economic and Social Consequences

  • Approximately 1 crore (10 million) soldiers died; another 2 crore were wounded; countless civilians perished.
  • Vast areas of France, Belgium, and Eastern Europe were devastated — farms, cities, and infrastructure destroyed.
  • The Spanish Flu pandemic (1918–19) killed an estimated 5 crore people worldwide — more than the war itself.
  • Women entered the workforce in large numbers during the war, accelerating the women's rights movement.
  • Germany's economy was crippled by reparations, leading to hyperinflation and mass unemployment in the 1920s.
  • The USA emerged as the world's largest creditor — European nations owed huge debts to American banks.
