üåç Introduction

The Vedic Period refers to the age associated with the Aryans and the composition of the Vedas. It is divided into two phases: Early Vedic Period (c. 1500‚Äì1000 BCE) and Later Vedic Period (c. 1000‚Äì600 BCE).

üìö PART 1: Sources ‚Äì The Four Vedas and Epics

The Four Vedas

  • Rigveda: Oldest Veda; 1,028 hymns in Sanskrit; addressed to Indra, Agni, Varuna. Primary source for Early Vedic life.
  • Samaveda: Songs of praise (sƒÅmans) derived from Rigveda; associated with music and ritual.
  • Yajurveda: Sacrificial formulas for priests during yajnas (fire rituals). Has two versions ‚Äî Krishna and Shukla.
  • Atharvaveda: Latest Veda; hymns, spells, and incantations for healing and everyday life; reflects popular religion.

The Epics

  • Mahabharata: 100,000+ verses; war of Pandavas vs Kauravas; contains the Bhagavad Gita.
  • Ramayana: By Valmiki; story of Rama; both epics are sources for Later Vedic social values.

Social Organisation ‚Äì Family Structure

  • Kula (family) ‚Üí Gram (village, led by Gramini) ‚Üí Vish (clan, led by Vishpati) ‚Üí Jana (tribe, led by Rajan) ‚Üí Rashtra (kingdom).
  • Patriarchal joint families were the norm. Father was the griha-pati (lord of household).

Rig Vedic Assemblies ‚Äì Sabha, Samiti, Vidhatha

Assembly Nature Function
Sabha Select council of elders/nobles Advisory, judicial; debated political matters
Samiti General assembly of all tribal members Elected/confirmed the king; public policy
Vidhatha Oldest, most powerful assembly Discussed war, booty, economic matters ‚Äî disappeared in Later Vedic period

Sabha = exclusive (nobles/elders). Samiti = popular assembly (whole tribe). Vidhatha = oldest ‚Äî disappeared later. Both Sabha and Samiti could check the king's power.

Position of Women in Early Vedic Society

  • Women enjoyed a high status ‚Äî attended Sabha and Vidhatha.
  • Could study Vedas and compose hymns ‚Äî female poets like Lopamudra and Gargi.
  • Widow remarriage (niyoga) permitted. Could choose own husbands (svayamvara).
  • No child marriage, no purdah system.

Early Vedic Economy

  • Pastoral ‚Äî cattle-based. Cattle were primary wealth. Word for war = gavishthi (search for cows).
  • Agriculture began ‚Äî barley (yava) was main crop. Barter economy; cows as medium of exchange.

üèôÔ∏è PART 3: Later Vedic Society (c. 1000‚Äì600 BCE)

The Four Varnas (Caste Divisions)

Varna Occupation Status
Brahmanas Priests, teachers Highest
Kshatriyas Warriors, rulers Second
Vaishyas Farmers, merchants Third
Shudras Servants, labourers Lowest
  • Caste became hereditary and birth-based (not occupation-based). Untouchability emerged.

The Four Ashramas (Stages of Life)

Ashrama Stage Duties
Brahmacharya Student (celibate) Study Vedas, serve guru
Grihastha Householder Marriage, raising children, livelihood
Vanaprastha Forest dweller Semi-retirement from worldly life
Sanyasa Renouncer Complete renunciation; pursue moksha

Education ‚Äì Gurukul System

  • Education at a Gurukul ‚Äî residential school where students (shishyas) lived with the guru.
  • No fees ‚Äî students served the guru. The Upanishads (philosophical texts) were the highest form of learned discussion.

Position of Women in Later Vedic Society

  • Status declined ‚Äî excluded from Vedic study and political assemblies.
  • Child marriage and sati emerged. Widow remarriage discouraged. Polygamy increased among nobles.

Later Vedic Economy

  • Agriculture became primary ‚Äî iron plough used; rice (vrihi) added to crops.
  • Guilds (shrenis) formed. Coined money (nishka, satamana) in use. Trade across river routes.

üìå Chapter Summary

  • Vedic Period: Early (1500‚Äì1000 BCE) and Later (1000‚Äì600 BCE). Sources: Rigveda is the most important.
  • Early Vedic: pastoral, tribal, women had high status, three powerful assemblies checked the king.
  • Later Vedic: agricultural, rigid four-tier hereditary caste, women's status fell, Brahmanical religion dominated.
  • Four Ashramas structured the ideal life from student to renunciant.
