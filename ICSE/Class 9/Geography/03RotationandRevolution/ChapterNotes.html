üîÑ PART 1: Rotation of the Earth

Rotation is the spinning of the Earth on its own axis (an imaginary line joining the North and South Poles). The Earth completes one full rotation in approximately 24 hours (23 hours 56 minutes 4 seconds = 1 sidereal day; ~24 hours = 1 solar day).

Direction of Rotation

  • The Earth rotates from west to east (anticlockwise when viewed from above the North Pole).
  • This is why the Sun appears to rise in the east and set in the west.
  • Speed of rotation at the Equator: approximately 1,670 km/h (=1,037 mph). Speed decreases towards the poles (poles = 0 km/h ‚Äî just rotating in place).

Effects of Rotation

Effect Explanation
Day and Night As Earth rotates, only one half faces the Sun (day) while the other half is in shadow (night). Dividing line = circle of illumination.
Direction of Sun rise/set Sun appears to rise in the East and set in the West due to Earth's west-to-east rotation.
Coriolis Effect Earth's rotation causes moving objects (winds, ocean currents, projectiles) to deflect: to the right in the Northern Hemisphere, to the left in the Southern Hemisphere. Causes cyclones, ocean gyres, trade winds to curve.
Flattening at poles Centrifugal force due to rotation causes Earth to bulge at equator and flatten at poles ‚Üí oblate spheroid shape.
Tides Rotation affects sea levels along with Moon's gravity.

üåç PART 2: Revolution of the Earth

Revolution is the movement of the Earth around the Sun in its orbit. The Earth completes one revolution in approximately 365 days 6 hours (365.25 days) ‚Äî the basis of 1 solar year. Every 4 years, the extra 6 hours √ó 4 = 24 hours ‚Üí this extra day is added as Feb 29 in a Leap Year.
  • Earth's orbit is elliptical (not perfectly circular) ‚Äî so Earth is closer to the Sun at Perihelion (~147 million km, around January 3) and farther at Aphelion (~152 million km, around July 4).
  • Earth's axis is tilted at 23.5¬∞ from the perpendicular to its orbital plane ‚Äî this tilt is constant in direction (always pointing towards Polaris/Pole Star) and is the primary cause of seasons.

üåû PART 3: Seasons ‚Äî Summer and Winter Solstice, Equinoxes

Event Date What Happens Effects
Summer Solstice (Northern Hemisphere) ~June 21 Earth's North Pole is tilted towards Sun. Sun is directly overhead at Tropic of Cancer (23.5¬∞N). Longest day in NH (about 17 hrs at 50¬∞N); shortest night; Arctic Circle has 24-hr daylight. Southern Hemisphere = Winter Solstice (shortest day).
Winter Solstice (Northern Hemisphere) ~December 22 Earth's South Pole is tilted towards Sun. Sun is directly overhead at Tropic of Capricorn (23.5¬∞S). Shortest day in NH; longest night; Antarctic Circle has 24-hr daylight. Southern Hemisphere = Summer Solstice (longest day).

Autumn Equinox (Autumnal Equinox) ~September 23 Neither pole is tilted towards Sun. Sun is directly overhead at Equator. Day = Night = 12 hours everywhere on Earth. Autumn begins in NH; Spring begins in SH.

Why Seasons Occur

  • Seasons are caused by the 23.5¬∞ tilt of Earth's axis combined with its revolution around the Sun. (NOT because Earth is closer/farther from the Sun ‚Äî actually, Earth is closer to the Sun in January, during NH winter!)
  • When the NH is tilted towards the Sun: Sun's rays fall more directly (high angle ‚Üí more heat per unit area); days are longer. ‚Üí Summer in NH.
  • When the NH is tilted away from the Sun: rays are more slanted (low angle ‚Üí less heat per unit area); days are shorter. ‚Üí Winter in NH.

Effects of Revolution

Effect Explanation
Seasons Due to axial tilt + revolution ‚Äî Earth has 4 seasons in temperate regions
Change in length of day/night Day length varies throughout the year (longer days around summer solstice, shorter around winter solstice)
Variation in altitude of Sun Sun's angle in the sky changes throughout the year at any location outside the tropics
Occurrence of year One revolution = 1 year (365.25 days)
